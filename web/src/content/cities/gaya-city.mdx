overview: |
  Gaya, in Bihar, is one of Hinduism's most significant sites for
  ancestral rites, centred on Vishnupad Temple and the practice of Pind
  Daan, offerings made for the salvation of one's ancestors. The city's
  spiritual roots predate recorded history in the region and coincide
  with the era of the Buddha and Mahavira in the 6th century BCE, when
  Gaya was part of the Shishunaga dynasty and later the Magadha Empire.
  Gaya is distinct from, though adjoining, Bodh Gaya, the separate
  Buddhist pilgrimage site where the Buddha attained enlightenment.

history: |
  Vishnupad Temple enshrines a sacred footprint held to be Vishnu's own,
  imprinted in rock at the spot where he is said to have pressed his
  foot on the body of the demon Gayasura. The present temple structure
  was built on the older site by Ahilyabai Holkar of Indore in the
  mid-18th century, following the same pattern of temple restoration
  she funded at several other major sites across India. Hindu tradition
  holds that even Rama performed Pind Daan for his ancestors at Gaya,
  reinforcing the site's standing as the pre-eminent Hindu location for
  this rite.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Vishnupad Temple's Nagara-style granite structure, with a roughly
  100-foot Vimana and pillared Mandapa, sits directly beside the Phalgu
  river, whose waters and sands are integral to the Pind Daan ceremony.
  Within the temple courtyard, the Akshayavat ("undecaying banyan
  tree") is held to make rituals performed beneath it exceptionally
  meritorious. Pind Daan may be performed at Gaya on any day, though the
  Shraddha tithi (death anniversary) of the ancestor is considered most
  auspicious, and the Pitru Paksha period draws especially large
  numbers of pilgrims.
