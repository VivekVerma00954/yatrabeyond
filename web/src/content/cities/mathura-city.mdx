overview: |
  Mathura, on the banks of the Yamuna river in Uttar Pradesh, is
  regarded as the heart of Braj Bhoomi and the traditional birthplace
  of Krishna, of the Yadu dynasty. One of Hinduism's seven sacred
  cities, it is the primary pilgrimage site for the annual Janmashtami
  celebration marking Krishna's birth, and is closely linked with, but
  distinct from, neighbouring Vrindavan.

history: |
  Mathura's ancient history is attested in the Ramayana, and the city
  flourished under the Kushan Empire before the 1st century CE, later
  becoming a stronghold for both Buddhist and Jain communities in the
  2nd century CE; art historians have called Mathura the "mother of
  Indian iconography" for the wide influence of its sculptural
  traditions on later Indian temple art. The city's Krishna
  Janmabhoomi site has been repeatedly targeted across its history:
  Mahmud of Ghazni plundered its temples in 1018 CE, the city was
  sacked several further times between 1500 and 1757, and Aurangzeb
  built the Gyanvapi-adjacent mosque structure over the Janmabhoomi
  temple site in the 17th century, a history that continues to shape
  the site's status today.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The Krishna Janmasthan Temple Complex, built around the traditional
  birthplace cell (Garbhagriha) of Krishna, is Mathura's central
  pilgrimage site, particularly during Janmashtami. Dwarkadhish Temple,
  built in 1914 by Seth Gokuldas of Gwalior, is considered the city's
  main active temple. Mathura forms part of the wider Krishna
  pilgrimage circuit alongside Vrindavan, Barsana, Govardhan, Gokul,
  Kurukshetra, Dwarka, and Puri.
