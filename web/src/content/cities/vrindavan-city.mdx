overview: |
  Vrindavan, in Mathura district, Uttar Pradesh, is held in Hindu
  tradition as the place where Krishna spent his childhood among the
  cowherds of Braj. Its name, from Sanskrit vṛndā (holy basil) and vana
  (grove/forest), reflects its identity as a sacred pastoral landscape
  as much as a town, and it is said to hold roughly 5,500 temples
  dedicated to Krishna and Radha, making it one of the densest temple
  concentrations of any pilgrimage city in India.

history: |
  Vrindavan's modern devotional prominence is closely tied to Chaitanya
  Mahaprabhu, the 16th-century Bengali Vaishnava saint who visited the
  area in 1515 specifically to rediscover and identify holy sites
  associated with Krishna's life, a project continued by his followers,
  the Six Goswamis of Vrindavan, who founded many of the town's oldest
  and most significant temples in the decades that followed.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Vrindavan's devotional character centres on Krishna and Radha
  worship, expressed through temple traditions ranging from the Radha
  Raman and Radha Madan Mohan lineages (associated with the Goswamis)
  to Banke Bihari's distinctive Radha-Krishna combined form, to the
  20th-century international Gaudiya Vaishnava mission represented by
  ISKCON. The town forms part of the Indian government's wider "Krishna
  circuit" tourism initiative, alongside Mathura, Barsana, Govardhan,
  Gokul, Kurukshetra, Dwarka, and Puri, and is one of the most visited
  pilgrimage towns in North India, particularly during Janmashtami and
  Holi.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to March offers the most comfortable
  weather; Janmashtami (Krishna's birth), Holi (including the
  distinctive Phoolon ki Holi, "Holi of flowers," at Banke Bihari
  Temple), and Radha Ashtami are the town's most significant annual
  observances.

  **Getting there:** Vrindavan is reached by road from Mathura (a short
  distance away) or directly from Delhi/Agra; Mathura has the nearest
  railway station with strong connectivity.
