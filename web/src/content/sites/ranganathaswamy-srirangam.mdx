overview: |
  Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple in Srirangam, near Tiruchirappalli, Tamil
  Nadu, is the largest functioning Hindu temple complex in the world,
  spanning roughly 156 acres (63 hectares) with 81 shrines, 21 towering
  gopurams, and 39 pavilions integrated around water tanks within its
  walls. It holds the unique distinction of being the foremost (first)
  among the 108 Divya Desams dedicated to Vishnu, and remains a fully
  active, continuously functioning site of worship rather than primarily
  an archaeological monument.

history: |
  The temple's history traces back to the Sangam era (roughly 500 BCE to
  300 CE), with its initial construction traditionally attributed to the
  Chola ruler Dharmavarma. Successive South Indian dynasties, including
  the Cholas, Pandyas, Hoysalas, the Vijayanagara Empire, and later the
  Marathas, made substantial additions over the following centuries,
  producing the complex's present layered architectural character. Its
  Raja Gopuram, one of the tallest temple towers in Asia at roughly 236
  feet, was itself completed only in the 20th century, illustrating how
  the complex continued to grow long after its ancient origins.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As the foremost of the 108 Divya Desams celebrated by the Tamil Alvar
  poet-saints, Srirangam holds a position of particular reverence within
  Sri Vaishnavism, the theological tradition associated with the
  medieval philosopher Ramanuja, who is closely linked with the temple's
  own institutional history. Ranganatha is depicted here in his
  distinctive reclining form, resting on the serpent Adishesha, with
  Ranganayaki (a form of Lakshmi) as consort, a central Vaishnava
  iconographic motif also seen at Padmanabhaswamy Temple, Thiruvananthapuram.
