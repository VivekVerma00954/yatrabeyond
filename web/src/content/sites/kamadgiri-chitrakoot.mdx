overview: |
  Chitrakoot, straddling the Madhya Pradesh-Uttar Pradesh border, is
  traditionally identified as the forest region where Rama, Sita, and
  Lakshmana spent a substantial portion (traditionally given as eleven
  and a half of the fourteen years) of their exile described in the
  Ramayana. Two sites anchor the pilgrimage here: Kamadgiri Hill, the
  hill itself worshipped as a living embodiment of Rama, and Ram Ghat on
  the Mandakini river, where the three are said to have first bathed
  after arriving from Ayodhya. This platform treats Chitrakoot as a
  distinct pilgrimage cluster in its own right, separate from the
  disputed Ramgiri Shakti Peetha claim (also associated with Chitrakoot
  by some sources), which is covered on its own dedicated page.

history: |
  Chitrakoot's association with Rama's exile is described in the Ayodhya
  Kanda of the Valmiki Ramayana, which recounts the period the three
  spent living among the region's forests, rivers, and hills after
  leaving Ayodhya. In later centuries, Chitrakoot became closely
  associated with the poet-saint Tulsidas, who is traditionally said to
  have composed and lived near these riverside sites while working on
  the Ramcharitmanas, adding a further devotional-literary layer to the
  location's significance.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The barefoot 5 km circumambulation of Kamadgiri Hill (Kamadgiri
  Parikrama) is considered the single most important devotional act
  available at Chitrakoot, treating the hill itself as Rama's living
  form rather than merely the site of a historical stay. Ram Ghat, on
  the Mandakini, is understood as the spiritual heart of the wider
  Chitrakoot pilgrimage circuit, and the nearby Bharat Milap Temple
  marks the traditional site of Rama's reunion with his brother Bharat
  during the exile period, a story with major devotional weight within
  the wider Ramayana narrative.

scriptures: |
  Chitrakoot's role in the exile narrative is documented in the Ayodhya
  Kanda of the Valmiki Ramayana. Tulsidas's association with the site is
  a widely repeated devotional and literary tradition; precise dating
  and textual corroboration require expert verification before
  publication.
