overview: |
  Tarapith Temple, near the Dwaraka river in Rampurhat, Birbhum district,
  West Bengal, is dedicated to Tara, the second of the ten Mahavidyas,
  and is one of the classical 51 Shakti Peethas, traditionally marking
  the spot where Sati's third eye fell. Recognised as both a Shakti
  Peetha and a Siddha Peetha, Tarapith is among the most historically
  and devotionally significant centres of Tantra and Shakti worship
  anywhere in India, and, notably, the village's own name changed from
  Chandipur to Tarapith, "tara" being the Bengali word for eyeball, a
  direct reference to the site's body-part attribution.

history: |
  The present temple structure was established roughly 300 years ago
  (in the Bengali year 1225), built in the distinctive four-chala style
  and reaching a height of 64 feet. According to legend, the sage
  Vasishtha performed intense penance at this site, reciting the Tara
  mantra 300,000 times; pleased with his devotion, the goddess Tara
  appeared before him and incarnated herself as the stone image now
  worshipped in the temple's sanctum.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Tarapith is inseparably associated with the 19th-20th-century mystic
  saint Bamakhepa, whose deep, unconventional spiritual practice at the
  site is central to its modern devotional identity; the adjoining
  cremation ground is one of the most revered and actively used sites
  for Tantric practice in the whole of Shaktism, distinguishing
  Tarapith's devotional character from most other Shakti Peethas covered
  on this platform. The sanctum houses two images of Tara: the
  "primordial" stone form, depicting the goddess suckling Shiva, is
  normally concealed behind a three-foot metal image seen by most
  devotees.

scriptures: |
  Tarapith's identification with Sati's third eye is documented in the
  Devi Bhagavata Purana, Kalika Purana, Markandeya Purana, and the
  Shakti Peetha Stotram; Tara's identity as the second Mahavidya draws
  on the separate Dasa Mahavidya tradition. Specific verse-level
  citation for the Vasishtha legend requires expert verification before
  publication.
