overview: |
  Gangotri, in Uttarakhand's Uttarkashi district, is one of the four sites
  of the Himalayan "Chota Char Dham" (alongside Yamunotri, Kedarnath, and
  Badrinath) and marks the point closest to the source of the sacred
  Ganga (Ganges) river accessible by road. The river's actual glacial
  source lies further upstream at Gaumukh, in the Gangotri Glacier,
  reached by a separate trek; below Gangotri, the river is known as the
  Bhagirathi, taking the name "Ganga" only after the Alaknanda merges into
  it further downstream, near Devprayag.

history: |
  The present temple was built in the 18th century by the Gorkha military
  commander Amar Singh Thapa, and was later restored in the early 20th
  century by Maharaja Madho Singh II of Jaipur, when it had fallen into
  disrepair. The temple's mythological association, however, is described
  as far older: tradition holds that the Ganga descended from the heavens
  to earth at this location, in response to King Bhagiratha's long penance
  seeking to redeem the souls of his cursed ancestors, and that Shiva
  agreed to break the force of her descent by receiving her first in his
  own matted locks (jata), before releasing her to flow across the earth
  at Gangotri.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As the traditional starting point of the Char Dham Yatra of Uttarakhand
  (the circuit is traditionally undertaken beginning at Yamunotri and
  ending at Badrinath, with Gangotri as the second stop), Gangotri holds a
  foundational place in the Himalayan pilgrimage sequence. Bathing in the
  Bhagirathi here, and collecting Gangajal (sacred Ganga water) to carry
  home or use in rituals elsewhere in India, are central pilgrimage
  practices at this site, given the river's unmatched status as the most
  sacred river in Hindu tradition.

scriptures: |
  The Bhagiratha and descent-of-Ganga legend is recorded across multiple

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The temple opens and closes annually following a
  calendar similar to the other Char Dham of Uttarakhand, typically late
  April/May to October/November; May–June and September–October offer the
  most stable weather. Gangotri is the second stop on the traditional
  Char Dham Yatra sequence, after Yamunotri.

  **Getting there:** Reached by road from Uttarkashi, itself accessible
  from Rishikesh; no trekking is required to reach the temple and town.

  **The Gaumukh trek:** For pilgrims wishing to reach the river's actual
  glacial source, a separate, moderately challenging trek of approximately
  19 km each way leads to Gaumukh, typically requiring a full day or an
  overnight stay at Bhojwasa, the usual midway camping point. This trek
  is optional and distinct from visiting the main temple.

familySuitability: |
  The main temple and town are well suited to families of all ages, given
  the motorable road access. The optional Gaumukh trek is considerably
  more demanding and better suited to fitter members of a group.

accommodation: |
  Gangotri town offers GMVN guesthouses, dharamshalas, and private
  hotels serving Char Dham pilgrim traffic; those attempting the Gaumukh
  trek typically camp overnight at Bhojwasa, with only very basic facilities
  available there.

localServices: |
  Gangotri has a reasonable range of pilgrim services — food, small shops,
  and basic medical facilities; services become minimal beyond the town
  itself on the Gaumukh trek route.

preservation: |
  The Gangotri Glacier, source of the Bhagirathi/Ganga, is under
  documented pressure from glacial recession linked to climate change, a
  matter of significant environmental concern given the river's
  importance to hundreds of millions of people downstream. The Gangotri
  region is also part of a broader eco-sensitive zone subject to
  Supreme Court and National Green Tribunal oversight regarding
  construction and visitor management.
