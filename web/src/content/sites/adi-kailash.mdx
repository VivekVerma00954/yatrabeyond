overview: |
  Adi Kailash — also known as Chhota Kailash, Baba Kailash, or Jonglingkong
  Peak — is a Himalayan peak in Pithoragarh district, Uttarakhand, close to
  the tri-junction of India, Nepal, and Tibet. Standing at approximately
  5,945–6,191 m depending on the source consulted, it is often described as
  the second most important of the Panch Kailash after Mount Kailash itself
  in Tibet, and as a more accessible echo of it — hence the name "Chhota"
  (small) Kailash. The sacred Jolingkong Lake sits at its base, with a
  small Shiva temple beside it, and the nearby Om Parvat is closely
  associated with the same pilgrimage circuit.

history: |
  Adi Kailash is regarded in local and pilgrim tradition as sacred to Shiva
  in the same manner as Mount Kailash in Tibet, though on a smaller and more
  accessible scale. Jolingkong Lake at its base is popularly likened to a
  miniature Lake Manasarovar. The adjoining Om Parvat, where natural snow
  and rock patterns form what devotees read as the sacred syllable "Om" on
  the mountain's face, has become closely linked to the pilgrimage in
  popular devotion and tourism material, though this specific
  Om-formation tradition appears to be a comparatively recent devotional
  and tourism-era framing rather than an ancient scriptural reference —
  this should be verified before publication.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Adi Kailash offers pilgrims and visitors an opportunity to undertake a
  genuinely Himalayan, border-region Kailash pilgrimage without the
  international travel and extreme remoteness required to reach Mount
  Kailash itself. Its comparative accessibility has made it increasingly
  popular in recent years as domestic Himalayan tourism and pilgrimage
  infrastructure has developed in this part of Uttarakhand. Darshan of the
  peak from Jolingkong Lake, and viewing the Om formation at Om Parvat, are
  both treated as complete pilgrimage acts in their own right by many
  visitors, without any expectation of approaching or climbing the peak.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** May to mid-October is the practical window, with June
  and September widely cited as the most reliable months for clear weather,
  stable roads, and good visibility of Om Parvat; the route is closed by
  snow outside this window.

  **Getting there:** The journey begins at Pithoragarh and proceeds via
  Dharchula, Gunji, and Nabhidhang, largely on motorable border-area roads.
  The Jolingkong Lake viewpoint for Adi Kailash, and the separate Nabhidhang
  viewpoint for Om Parvat, both involve only short final walks on uneven
  ground rather than a demanding trek. An Inner Line Permit is required, as
  the route runs through a restricted border zone near the India-Nepal-Tibet
  tri-junction; this is typically arranged through registered tour operators
  based in Pithoragarh or Dharchula.

  considerations, and travel logistics (permits, remote roads) require
  planning regardless of the physical ease of the final approach.

accommodation: |
  Dharchula and Gunji offer guesthouse and homestay-style accommodation
  serving this route; options become progressively more basic closer to
  Nabhidhang and the border. Most itineraries are organised as multi-day
  road trips with overnight stops at these points rather than as a single
  long push.

localServices: |
  Dharchula has the fuller range of services for this route — food, basic
  medical facilities, and permit-processing support from local operators.
  Gunji and Nabhidhang offer only basic facilities; mobile connectivity is
  limited to nonexistent in the border zone approaching Adi Kailash and Om
  Parvat.
