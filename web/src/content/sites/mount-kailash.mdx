overview: |
  Mount Kailash (Kailash Parvat), rising to 6,638 m in the remote Ngari
  Prefecture of the Tibet Autonomous Region, is among the most
  multi-religiously sacred mountains on Earth — revered simultaneously by
  Hindus, Tibetan Buddhists, Jains, and followers of the Bon religion. For
  Hindus, it is the literal abode of Shiva, where he sits in eternal
  meditation with Parvati, and is regarded as the paramount site among the
  five peaks grouped together as the Panch Kailash. Its distinctive
  near-pyramidal form, and its position beside the sacred Lake Manasarovar,
  have made it a pilgrimage destination for millennia despite its extreme
  remoteness.

history: |
  Mount Kailash has no single founding legend in the way built temples do;
  its sanctity is treated as inherent to the mountain itself across all four
  traditions that revere it. In Hindu tradition it is Shiva's eternal abode.
  In Tibetan Buddhism it is associated with Mount Meru, the axis of the
  Buddhist cosmological universe, and with the tantric deity Demchok
  (Chakrasamvara). In Jain tradition, under the name Ashtapada, it is held
  to be the site where Rishabhanatha, the first Tirthankara, attained
  moksha. In the Bon religion, which predates Buddhism in Tibet, it is
  called Yundrung Gu Tse, the "nine-story Swastika Mountain," and is
  regarded as the spiritual centre of the universe.

  The mountain has never been climbed. In the mid-1980s, the Chinese
  government reportedly offered the celebrated mountaineer Reinhold Messner
  permission to attempt the summit; he declined. In the early 2000s, a
  Spanish climbing team obtained permission from Chinese authorities to
  attempt an ascent, but withdrew the plan after strong international
  religious and diplomatic objection. Chinese authorities have since
  maintained a ban on summit attempts.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Rather than climbing, pilgrims perform the kora, a circumambulation of the
  mountain's base. In Hindu and Buddhist tradition alike, a single
  circumambulation is believed to cleanse the sins of a lifetime; Tibetan
  Buddhist tradition in particular holds that completing 108 koras can lead
  to nirvana. Many pilgrims also bathe in or collect water from nearby Lake
  Manasarovar, itself considered highly sacred and frequently visited as
  part of the same pilgrimage (the combined "Kailash Manasarovar Yatra").
  The mountain's stature as a site revered across four distinct religious
  traditions, with no recorded ascent in the modern mountaineering era, gives
  it a symbolic weight distinct from any other site on this platform.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The main pilgrimage season runs from roughly May to
  September, avoiding the harshest winter conditions; many tour operators
  concentrate departures in June through August.

  **Getting there:** Two broad approaches exist. Travellers can enter via
  China, typically flying into Lhasa and travelling overland (roughly 3–4
  days) to Darchen, the base town for the kora, with a Tibet Travel Permit
  and Alien Travel Permit arranged through a licensed agency. Alternatively,
  Indian pilgrims can join a Kailash Manasarovar Yatra organised with
  involvement from the Indian government, historically routed via the
  Lipulekh Pass in Uttarakhand or, in some years, via Nepal (through
  Simikot). Route availability has been affected in various years by
  India-China border relations and should be confirmed directly with
  current official sources before planning.

  **The kora:** The circumambulation covers roughly 52 km, usually completed
  over three days, crossing the Dolma La pass at approximately 5,630–5,660
  m — the physically hardest single stretch of the route. Many pilgrims
  combine the kora with a visit to nearby Lake Manasarovar.

accommodation: |
  Darchen, the main base town for the kora, has basic guesthouses and
  tour-operator lodges. Along the kora route itself, simple guesthouses
  exist at Dirapuk and Zutulpuk, the two common overnight stops; conditions
  are basic. Most travellers arrange the full trip, including accommodation,
  through a licensed tour operator given the permit requirements involved.

localServices: |
  Darchen has limited shops, basic food, and some mobile connectivity
  depending on network provider; along the kora itself, expect minimal
  services beyond the guesthouses at Dirapuk and Zutulpuk. Medical
  facilities are limited in this remote region — travellers should carry
  any required medication and consider altitude-sickness precautions
  seriously.
