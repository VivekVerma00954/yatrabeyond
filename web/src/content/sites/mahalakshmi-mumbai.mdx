overview: |
  Mahalakshmi Temple, in the Mahalakshmi area of central Mumbai, is one
  of the city's most visited and revered temples, enshrining images of
  the Tridevi (three-goddess) trinity: Mahakali, Mahalakshmi, and
  Mahasaraswati. This platform notes that Mahalakshmi Kolhapur, covered
  elsewhere as one of the 18 Maha Shakti Peethas, is a separate, older,
  and independently significant Mahalakshmi temple in Maharashtra; the
  two are not the same site and should not be confused.

history: |
  Accounts of the temple's founding vary: some sources date its
  construction to 1831 under the Hindu merchant Dhakji Dadaji, while
  others place its origins somewhat earlier, between 1761 and 1771. The
  temple's celebrated origin legend is tied to the Hornby Vellard, a
  major 18th-century British colonial land-reclamation project intended
  to link Mumbai's originally separate islands via causeway, which
  reportedly suffered repeated destruction by the sea during
  construction. According to the legend, Goddess Mahalakshmi appeared in
  a dream to the project's chief engineer, instructing him to recover
  idols from the Worli Creek and install them on a nearby hillock; after
  doing so, the causeway construction is said to have succeeded, and the
  temple was subsequently built to house the recovered idols.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Beyond its devotional significance as a Tridevi shrine, Mahalakshmi
  Temple maintains an active and well-documented social welfare
  programme through its trust, including free medical dispensaries
  (offering allopathic, ayurvedic, and homeopathic services), medical
  assistance for the poor, scholarships for underprivileged students
  regardless of religion, and disaster relief support — giving the
  temple a civic as well as devotional identity within the city. The
  temple draws especially large crowds during Navratri.
