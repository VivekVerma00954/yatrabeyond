overview: |
  Treta Ke Thakur (also called Treta Mandir), near Naya Ghat on the Saryu
  riverbank in Ayodhya, is traditionally identified as the site where
  Rama performed the Ashwamedha Yajna (a major royal sacrificial rite)
  after his return to Ayodhya and the events described in the Ramayana's
  Yuddha Kanda — giving the temple its name, referencing the Treta Yuga
  in which Rama's life is traditionally set. This platform includes Treta
  Ke Thakur as a fifth major historical temple in the Ayodhya cluster,
  alongside Ram Mandir, Hanuman Garhi, Kanak Bhawan, and Nageshwarnath,
  given its distinct antiquity and its documented, multi-century
  reconstruction history independent of the modern Ram Janmabhoomi
  dispute.

history: |
  Devotional tradition attributes an original shrine at this site to
  King Vikramaditya, consistent with the broader pattern of Vikramaditya-
  era temple attributions found across several of Ayodhya's most ancient
  sites. The temple's better-documented, more recent reconstruction
  history records that the Raja of Kullu built a new temple here
  approximately 300 years ago (commonly dated to the 18th century), which
  was subsequently further improved in 1784 by Ahilyabai Holkar, the
  Maratha queen whose patronage of Hindu temple reconstruction — spanning
  Somnath, Omkareshwar/Mamleshwar, Kashi Vishwanath, Aundha Nagnath, and
  Parli Vaijnath, among others — makes her one of the most significant
  single patrons in the physical history of Hindu temple architecture
  across India.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As the traditional site of Rama's Ashwamedha Yajna, Treta Ke Thakur
  marks a distinct moment in the Ramayana narrative from the temples
  associated with his birth (Ram Mandir), his protector (Hanuman Garhi),
  or his wife's residence (Kanak Bhawan) — specifically, his re-establishment
  of dharmic kingship after the war with Ravana. This gives the temple a
  particular significance for pilgrims interested in the fuller narrative
  arc of Rama's reign, beyond the more commonly visited birth and
  childhood-associated sites.

scriptures: |
  The Ashwamedha Yajna itself is described in the Ramayana's Uttara
  Kanda. The specific identification of this exact riverside location as
  the site of that yajna is a matter of long-standing local and regional
  devotional tradition rather than a matter of settled textual or
  archaeological consensus, and should be presented to readers with that
  distinction clear.
