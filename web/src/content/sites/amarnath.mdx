overview: |
  Amarnath is a Himalayan cave shrine in Anantnag district, Jammu and
  Kashmir, at approximately 3,888 m, holding one of the most distinctive
  natural phenomena in Indian pilgrimage: a swayambhu (naturally formed)
  ice stalagmite, revered as a lingam of Shiva, which forms as water
  dripping from the cave ceiling freezes, and which is said to wax and
  wane with the lunar cycle. The cave itself is roughly 36 m wide and 23
  m high, sloping some 24 m into the mountain. It is accessible only for a
  short window each summer and is one of the most physically and
  logistically demanding major pilgrimages covered on this platform.

history: |
  Amarnath's antiquity as a sacred site is well documented in historical
  texts independent of any single founding legend. The 12th-century
  chronicle Rajatarangini, by the Kashmiri historian Kalhana, records that
  a ruler named Sandimati visited the ice lingam roughly two thousand
  years earlier, indicating the site's sacred status long predates the
  medieval period. The cave and its associated lore also appear in the
  Nilamata Purana, the Bhrigu Samhita, and a dedicated Amarnath Mahatmya
  text.

  A separate, more recent Kashmiri folk legend concerns a shepherd named
  Buta Malik, who is popularly credited with rediscovering the cave after
  a sage gave him a sack of coal that turned to gold; on returning to
  thank the sage, Malik instead found the cave and its ice lingam. This
  rediscovery legend, commonly dated to the 19th century, should be
  understood as an account of the cave's more recent re-entry into wider
  pilgrimage awareness, not as evidence against the much older, textually
  documented sacred history of the site itself.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The central legend associated with Amarnath describes Shiva revealing
  "Amar Katha" — the secret of immortality — to Parvati within this cave,
  choosing the location specifically for its remoteness, to ensure no
  living being could overhear. Tradition holds that a pair of pigeons
  nonetheless overheard the tale and were granted immortality as a
  result; pilgrims who report sighting a pair of pigeons near the cave
  during the yatra widely regard this as an especially auspicious sign.

  Darshan of the ice lingam, whose size is popularly (though unofficially)
  associated with the moon's waxing and waning, is considered a
  particularly potent spiritual experience specifically because of the
  yatra's difficulty and the lingam's ephemeral, naturally-formed nature —
  it is not a carved or installed image, but a temporary formation
  understood by devotees as a direct, unmediated manifestation of Shiva.
  The yatra concludes each year on Raksha Bandhan, when the Chhari
  Mubarak (a sacred ceremonial mace) is carried in procession to the cave,
  marking the pilgrimage season's formal close.

  Separate from its primary identity as a Shiva ice-lingam shrine, at
  least one composite Shakti Peetha source list also names Amarnath as
  one of the classical 51 Shakti Peethas, marking the spot where Sati's
  throat fell, with the goddess given as Mahamaya Devi. This platform
  treats this as a secondary, less consistently attested layer alongside
  the far more established Amar Katha/ice-lingam tradition described
  above, rather than as an equally weighted alternate identity for the
  site.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The yatra runs for a fixed window each summer,
  typically from late June or early July to Raksha Bandhan in
  August — roughly 45–60 days depending on the year's lunar calendar.
  Confirm exact dates with the Shri Amarnathji Shrine Board (SASB) for the
  specific year of travel; the shrine is not accessible outside this
  official window.

  **Registration (mandatory):** Every pilgrim must register in advance,
  either online via the official SASB portal/app or in person at
  designated bank branches, and must upload a Compulsory Health
  Certificate issued within the window specified for that year. On
  arrival, pilgrims complete biometric verification to receive an RFID
  tracking card; access checkpoints at Chandanwari (Pahalgam route) and
  Domail (Baltal route) will not admit any pilgrim without both a valid
  permit and this card.

  **Routes:** The Pahalgam route (starting at 2,130 m) is longer (roughly
  46–48 km) but gentler, passing Chandanwari, Pissu Top, and Sheshnag
  before crossing the Mahagunas Pass (4,276 m, the yatra's highest point)
  and descending to Panchtarni, then the cave. The Baltal route is much
  shorter (roughly 14 km) but considerably steeper, typically completed
  in one to two days. Ponies, palanquins (dolis), and helicopter services
  from Neelgrath and Panjtarni are available on both routes and should be
  booked in advance during peak season.

  **Security context:** The yatra passes through a region with an active
  security presence, and the route has, on rare occasions in past decades,
  been the target of security incidents. The Jammu and Kashmir
  administration and Indian security forces maintain a substantial
  security deployment along both routes throughout the yatra period; check
  current government travel advisories before planning a visit.

familySuitability: |
  Not recommended for young children or those without high-altitude
  trekking fitness. The compulsory medical certificate requirement exists
  specifically because of the genuine physical risk involved — extreme
  altitude, cold, and a demanding multi-day trek even on the shorter
  route. Families considering the yatra should assess fitness honestly
  and consider the helicopter option, which substantially reduces the
  physical demand, particularly for older or less physically prepared
  members of a group.

accommodation: |
  Base towns — Pahalgam and Baltal — offer a range of hotels and
  guesthouses; along both routes, the Shrine Board and various
  organisations (langars) set up seasonal camps offering basic tented
  accommodation and free community meals to pilgrims throughout the yatra
  period. Advance booking for base-town accommodation during peak yatra
  season is strongly advised.

localServices: |
  Extensive seasonal infrastructure is set up by the Shrine Board,
  government agencies, and volunteer langar organisations along both
  routes during the yatra period — medical camps, food, and rest points at
  regular intervals — reflecting the scale of the annual pilgrimage.
  Outside the yatra season, the route reverts to being largely
  inaccessible and unsupported.

preservation: |
  The scale of the annual yatra — over half a million pilgrims in 2024
  alone — brings substantial seasonal environmental and infrastructure
  pressure to a genuinely fragile high-altitude Himalayan ecosystem. The
  Shri Amarnathji Shrine Board manages waste, sanitation, and route
  infrastructure for the yatra period, and mandatory registration numbers
  are also used, in part, as a crowd-management and route-capacity tool.

givingBack: |
  Support the extensive volunteer langar (community kitchen) network along
  both routes, which relies substantially on donations and volunteer
  labour to feed pilgrims throughout the yatra. Follow all Shrine Board
  waste-management guidance, and use registered pony, palanquin, and
  helicopter operators rather than informal arrangements, both for safety
  and to support the formal local yatra economy.
