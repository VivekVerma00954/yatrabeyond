history: |
  The Jivitputrika Vrat is rooted in the legend of Jimutavahana, a noble
  prince who offered his own life to Garuda in place of a young boy who
  would otherwise have been taken and eaten; moved by this act of
  selflessness, Garuda is said to have spared the child and blessed
  humanity. The observance built around this legend has been practised
  for generations across the wider Bihar/Jharkhand/eastern Uttar Pradesh
  region and in Nepal, documented chiefly through oral, community, and
  folk-song tradition (Jitiya geet) rather than through the founding
  record of a single temple.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Jitiya (Jivitputrika Vrat) is observed by mothers over three lunar
  days in Krishna Paksha of the Ashvin month, primarily for the long
  life, health, and prosperity of their children. During the
  observance, devotees narrate the Jimutavahana legend to family
  members and worship symbolic representations, sometimes including
  clay or cow-dung figures of an eagle (Garuda) and a jackal, alongside
  invocations to Vishnu, Shiva, Ganesha, and the Mother Goddess. In
  Nepal, the festival includes ritual river baths, offerings of khar
  grass, and traditional Jhamta dances and songs; in Bihar and
  Jharkhand, women decorate courtyards with alpana and rangoli as part
  of the observance.
