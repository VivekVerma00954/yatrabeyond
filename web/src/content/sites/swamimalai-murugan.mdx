overview: |
  Swamimalai Murugan Temple, on the banks of a Kaveri tributary near
  Kumbakonam, is the fourth of the six Arupadaiveedu abodes. Here Murugan
  is worshipped as Swaminatha Swamy ("Teacher of God"), commemorating a
  distinctive legend in which the young Murugan instructs his own
  father, Shiva, in the meaning of the sacred syllable Om.

history: |
  The temple's existence is generally dated to as early as the 2nd
  century BCE, with the Chola king Parantaka Chola I credited with a
  significant early building phase. The temple suffered considerable
  damage during the Anglo-French conflict involving Hyder Ali and the
  British in 1740, a rare instance among the six abodes of direct
  documented damage from 18th-century regional warfare.

spiritualSignificance: |
  According to legend, the young Murugan asked Brahma how he created
  living beings; Brahma answered that he did so through the power of the
  Vedas, and began reciting the sacred verse starting with the Pranava
  mantra (Om). When Murugan asked Brahma to explain the mantra's meaning
  and Brahma could not answer, Murugan imprisoned him; Shiva then
  intervened and challenged Murugan to explain the meaning himself, and
  Murugan duly taught his own father the meaning of Om, earning the
  title Swaminatha ("teacher of the Lord/Shiva"). The temple stands atop
  a hillock with shrines to Shiva (Sundareswarar) and Parvati (Meenakshi)
  at its base, and its 60 steps, each named for a year of the traditional
  60-year Tamil calendar cycle, are a distinctive architectural feature
  tied directly to this legend of divine knowledge.
