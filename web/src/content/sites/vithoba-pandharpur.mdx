overview: |
  Vithoba Temple in Pandharpur, on the banks of the Chandrabhaga river in
  Maharashtra, is the spiritual centre of the Varkari tradition, one of
  the most significant Bhakti-movement devotional communities in Indian
  history. Vithoba (also called Vitthal or Panduranga) is worshipped here
  as a form of Vishnu/Krishna, alongside his consort Rakhumai (Rukmini),
  and the temple anchors the annual Pandharpur Wari, one of the largest
  walking pilgrimages in the world.

history: |
  The origins of the present temple are generally traced to the 12th
  century, with construction traditionally credited to the patronage of
  King Vishnuvardhana of the Hoysala Empire. The Varkari devotional
  tradition itself crystallised in 13th-century Maharashtra as part of
  the wider Bhakti movement, with the poet-saint Sant Dnyaneshwar
  (1275-1296 CE) playing a foundational role by composing the
  Dnyaneshwari, a Marathi-language commentary on the Bhagavad Gita that
  made Vedic philosophy directly accessible to ordinary devotees rather
  than only those able to read Sanskrit.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The Pandharpur Wari, an annual pilgrimage walked on foot from numerous
  starting points across Maharashtra (with routes covering roughly 250
  km for some groups), draws well over a million pilgrims (Varkaris)
  each year, organised into processional groups (dindis) that carry the
  palanquins (palkhis) of revered saints toward Pandharpur. The
  tradition, at least 900 years old, is built on an explicitly
  egalitarian devotional philosophy, treating pilgrims of all castes and
  social classes as equal in their shared bhakti (devotion) to Vithoba,
  a distinguishing feature of the Varkari movement within the wider
  history of Indian Bhakti traditions.

scriptures: |
