overview: |
  Madhyamaheshwar (also spelled Madmaheshwar) is the fourth stop in the
  traditional Panch Kedar circuit, sited at approximately 3,497 m in
  Gaundar village, Rudraprayag district. Tradition holds it to enshrine the
  **navel and belly (madhya/nabhi)** of Shiva, from the shared Panch Kedar
  origin legend. The trek to reach it — 16 to 24 km depending on the exact
  route and source, through dense oak and rhododendron forest — makes it
  one of the more demanding of the five, though less extreme than
  Rudranath.

history: |
  Madhyamaheshwar shares the Panch Kedar founding legend with the other
  four temples (see the Panch Kedar overview page for the full account):
  after Shiva evaded the Pandavas by taking bull form and sinking into the
  earth, his navel and stomach are said to have surfaced here. As with the
  other Panch Kedar, the temple is traditionally attributed to the
  Pandavas, with no independently verified construction date. Its
  architecture is described as similar in style to Kedarnath and Tungnath
  (the North Indian "Katyuri" Himalayan temple style).

spiritualSignificance: |
  Madhyamaheshwar is the middle temple of the Panch Kedar in more than
  just body-part symbolism — its position, roughly midway in both the
  physical circuit and in trek difficulty, gives it a distinct role for
  pilgrims undertaking the full five-temple journey. The secondary shrine
  of Budha Madmaheshwar ("elder Madmaheshwar"), a further 3 km and roughly
  200 m higher than the main temple, is considered by many pilgrims
  especially auspicious, and a common second-day extension for those who
  have made the full trek.

scriptures: |
  As with the other Panch Kedar, the founding legend draws on the
  Pandavas' post-Kurukshetra narrative from the **Mahabharata**, elaborated
  in regional material associated with the **Kedara Khanda** of the
  **Skanda Purana**. No specific verse-level citation for Madhyamaheshwar's
  individual legend has been confirmed; treat the summary above as
  devotional tradition pending expert verification.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** Open roughly May to October/November, closing for
  winter (the deity is moved down to the valley during this period, as
  with the other higher Panch Kedar sites). May offers freshly opened
  temple access and blooming rhododendrons; September–October brings
  clearer skies and thinner crowds.

  **Getting there:** Ukhimath is the nearest town with road connectivity;
  Ransi village, reached from Ukhimath, is the last motorable point. From
  Ransi, the trek to Gaundar and the temple runs 16–18 km through oak and
  rhododendron forest (some sources describe the fuller route distance as
  closer to 24 km) — most pilgrims take two days to reach the temple and
  two to return.

  **Extension:** The secondary Budha Madmaheshwar shrine is a further 3 km
  and roughly 200 m of additional ascent above the main temple, commonly
  visited as a second-day trip for those who have already made the main
  trek.

familySuitability: |
  Not recommended for young children. This is a genuine multi-day trek at
  altitude through forest terrain; achievable for fit families with older
  children and proper preparation, but should not be underestimated
  because it is less extreme than Rudranath.

accommodation: |
  Ukhimath has a fuller range of guesthouses and hotels. Ransi and Gaundar
  offer basic guesthouse and homestay-style accommodation; GMVN and forest
  rest-house options exist along parts of the route. This is not a
  same-day pilgrimage — plan for at least one, more likely two, overnight
  stays en route.

localServices: |
  Ukhimath has food, basic medical facilities, and better mobile
  connectivity. Along the trek and at Gaundar/the temple, expect only
  basic food stalls and very limited or no network coverage. Carry cash;
  no ATMs beyond Ukhimath.

preservation: |
  As with the other less-visited Panch Kedar sites, Madhyamaheshwar's
  moderate visitor numbers keep ecological pressure lower than at
  Kedarnath or Tungnath, but the oak and rhododendron forest the trek
  passes through, and the high-altitude meadow around the temple and the
  Budha Madmaheshwar extension, remain sensitive terrain with limited
  formal conservation infrastructure.

givingBack: |
  Carry out all waste generated during the multi-day trek, infrastructure

  Gaundar homestay/guesthouse economy directly rather than booking through
  distant, unaffiliated operators. Donate through official
  Badrinath-Kedarnath Temples Committee channels where the temple's own
  upkeep is concerned.
