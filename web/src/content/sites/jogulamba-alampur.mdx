overview: |
  Jogulamba Temple, in Alampur, Telangana, on the banks of the
  Tungabhadra river near its confluence with the Krishna, is one of the
  Ashtadasha (18) Maha Shakti Peethas, traditionally marking the spot
  where Sati's upper teeth fell. The goddess is depicted in a
  distinctive fierce, unclothed form with her tongue stretched outward,
  an iconography associated with granting siddhi (spiritual attainment)
  in yogic practice, giving her the name Jogulamba, a Telugu contraction
  of "Yogula Amma" ("Mother of Yogis"). Alampur itself is traditionally
  regarded as the western gateway to Srisailam, also covered on this
  platform.

history: |
  The original temple is reported to have been built in the 7th century
  CE, standing within the same complex as the Navabrahma Temples, a
  group of nine Shiva temples built between the 7th and 8th centuries.
  The Bahmani Sultanate destroyed the temple in 1390 CE; the Vijayanagara
  emperor Harihara Raya II subsequently sent an army to resist further
  attacks and fortified the complex, and the main idol was relocated to
  the nearby Bala Brahma temple within the same Navabrahma complex for
  safekeeping. Following extensive restoration, all idols were formally
  repositioned in a newly constructed temple on 13 February 2005,
  marking the site's most recent major rebuilding after roughly six
  centuries.

spiritualSignificance: |
