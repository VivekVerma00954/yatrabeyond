overview: |
  Grishneshwar Temple, in Verul village directly adjoining the Ellora
  Caves in Maharashtra, is traditionally counted as the twelfth and final
  of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Shiva. It is the smallest of the twelve
  Jyotirlinga temples, at roughly 240 by 185 feet, and is unusual in
  Maharashtra for its South Indian architectural style. Its proximity to
  the UNESCO World Heritage-listed Ellora Caves makes it a common
  combined pilgrimage and heritage visit.

history: |
  The temple's roots are traced to the 6th century, during the Rashtrakuta
  dynasty, though the structure seen today reflects a long history of
  destruction and rebuilding. It was destroyed by forces of the Delhi
  Sultanate in the 13th and 14th centuries and suffered further damage
  during the Mughal-Maratha conflicts. According to devotional tradition
  recorded in the Shiva Purana, the temple was first rebuilt in the 16th
  century by Maloji Bhosale, grandfather of Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj,
  and later rebuilt in its present form in the 18th century by the
  Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar — the same patron responsible for major
  reconstructions at Somnath, Omkareshwar/Mamleshwar, and Kashi
  Vishwanath, making her one of the most significant single figures in
  the modern architectural history of the Jyotirlinga tradition as a
  whole.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The Puranic legend associated with Grishneshwar centres on Ghushma
  (also spelled Kusuma), a devout woman said to have crafted and worshipped
  101 small Shivalingas daily; when her devotion was tested through
  tragedy, Shiva is said to have intervened and manifested here as
  Grishneshwar in response to her unwavering faith — a legend that gives
  the site a particular association with steadfast personal devotion
  rather than a royal or dynastic founding narrative, distinguishing it
  from several of the other Jyotirlingas.
