overview: |
  Radha Raman Temple, in Vrindavan, houses one of Braj's most
  theologically significant deities: Radha Raman, a form of Krishna
  understood by devotees to be self-manifested (swayambhu) rather than
  carved or installed by human hands, said to have emerged directly from
  a Shaligram stone in 1542. The temple was established by Gopal Bhatta
  Goswami, one of the Six Goswamis of Vrindavan, the foundational
  theologian-disciples of Chaitanya Mahaprabhu who systematised Gaudiya
  Vaishnava practice and devotional theology in 16th-century Braj.

history: |
  Gopal Bhatta Goswami was born around 1503 CE at Srirangam in South
  India into a Sri Vaishnava family, and served as a priest at the
  Ranganathaswamy Temple there before travelling north to Vrindavan at
  the inspiration of Chaitanya Mahaprabhu. According to the temple's own
  account, on the auspicious day of Narasimha Chaturdashi, Gopal Bhatta,
  reflecting on Vishnu's manifestation from a stone pillar as Narasimha,
  prayed for the opportunity to serve Krishna in a fully manifest form.
  Returning from his ritual bath to attend to his collection of Shaligram
  stones, he found that one of them had transformed into a deity of
  Krishna playing the flute, in the graceful three-bending (tribhanga)
  pose. The deity was formally installed on the full moon day of
  Vaishakha (April/May) in 1542.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Radha Raman's self-manifested origin gives the deity a particular
  theological status within Vrindavan's devotional hierarchy, and the
  temple maintains what is widely described as the highest standard of
  deity worship (seva) across all of Braj — including unusually intricate
  physical detail on the deity itself, such as visible fingernails and
  teeth, details understood by devotees as further evidence of the
  deity's uniquely manifest, rather than carved, nature. As founder of
  one of the Gaudiya tradition's most important lineages, Gopal Bhatta
  Goswami's own theological and liturgical writings also carry
  significant weight within the wider Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition beyond
  this temple specifically.
