overview: |
  Muktinath ("Lord of Liberation") is an ancient temple in the Muktinath
  Valley of Mustang, Nepal, at the foot of the Thorong La pass. Standing at
  approximately 3,760 m (sources vary between roughly 3,710 m and 3,800 m),
  it is among the highest-altitude temple sites on this platform and one
  of the most religiously significant examples of Hindu-Buddhist shared
  sacred space anywhere in the Himalaya. Hindus revere it as a Vishnu
  shrine and one of the eight self-manifest (Svayam Vyakta) Vaishnava
  sites; Tibetan Buddhists know it as Chumig Gyatsa ("Hundred Waters") and
  revere the site's central image as a manifestation of Avalokiteshvara
  (Chenrezig), the bodhisattva of compassion.

history: |
  The site is understood to have been a place of worship for many
  centuries, if not longer, before the present temple structure was built.
  The current pagoda-style temple is generally attributed to Queen
  Subarna Prabha, wife of King Rana Bahadur Shah and daughter-in-law of
  King Prithvi Narayan Shah (the unifier of modern Nepal), and dated to
  around 1815 CE — though the underlying sacred site and its Vishnu image
  are held by historians and pilgrimage tradition alike to substantially
  predate the current building.

  Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhav), the founder of Tibetan Buddhism, is said
  in Buddhist tradition to have meditated at Muktinath on his journey into
  Tibet, anchoring the site's importance in Tibetan Buddhist as well as
  Hindu pilgrimage tradition. The temple has long stood as a rare example
  of a single sacred site actively and jointly maintained by both religious
  communities, with Buddhist monks and Hindu priests both present at the
  site.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Muktinath's name reflects its central significance: pilgrims come seeking
  "mukti," liberation from the cycle of rebirth. The central ritual is
  bathing beneath the 108 stone water spouts (Mukti Dhara) behind the main
  temple, shaped as bull or cow heads, believed to cleanse sins; devout
  pilgrims traditionally aim to touch all 108. Nearby, the Jwala Mai
  (Jwalamukhi) shrine houses a natural gas-fed eternal flame alongside a
  spring, understood by devotees as a rare, sacred union of the elements
  of fire and water at a single point.

  The Kali Gandaki river, which flows through the valley below Muktinath,
  is the source of shaligram shila — sacred ammonite fossils venerated by
  Vaishnavites as a non-anthropomorphic manifestation of Vishnu, collected
  by pilgrims along the riverbed as an extension of the Muktinath
  pilgrimage.

  Separate from its primary identity as a Vaishnava/Buddhist shared
  shrine, at least one composite Shakti Peetha source list also names
  this site (given there as "Gandaki Chandi," Mustang) as one of the
  classical 51 Shakti Peethas, marking the spot where Sati's cheek fell,
  with the goddess venerated as an avatar of Lakshmi and Shiva present as
  Chakrapani. This platform treats this as a secondary, less
  consistently attested layer alongside the far more established Vishnu/
  Avalokiteshvara tradition described above.

scriptures: |

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** March–May and September–November offer the most
  stable weather and clearer mountain views; winter brings heavy snow and
  can disrupt both flights to Jomsom and road access, and the monsoon
  months (June–August) bring cloud, rain, and higher landslide risk on the
  approach roads.

  **Getting there — the standard pilgrim route:** Fly from Kathmandu or
  Pokhara to Jomsom, then continue approximately 20 km by jeep
  (1.5–2 hours) or on foot (6–8 hours) to Muktinath. Flights to Jomsom are
  frequently delayed or cancelled due to mountain weather, especially in
  the afternoon — build flexibility into any itinerary.

  **Getting there — the trekking route:** Trekkers completing the
  Annapurna Circuit reach Muktinath after crossing the Thorong La pass at
  5,416 m, a demanding high-altitude crossing requiring proper
  acclimatisation and multi-day trekking preparation; this is a
  significantly more difficult approach than the Jomsom road route and
  should not be treated as equivalent in difficulty.

familySuitability: |
  Not generally recommended for young children given the altitude (around
  3,760 m); older children and adults in reasonable health typically
  manage the Jomsom-jeep route without great difficulty, since it involves
  limited walking. The Thorong La trekking approach is not appropriate for
  children or inexperienced trekkers under any circumstances.

accommodation: |
  Muktinath village itself has a range of guesthouses and lodges serving
  pilgrims and Annapurna Circuit trekkers; Jomsom, the main air gateway,
  has a fuller range of hotels and services. Booking ahead during peak
  pilgrimage and trekking seasons (spring and autumn) is advisable.

localServices: |
  Jomsom has the fuller range of services for this region — food, basic
  medical facilities, and reasonable mobile connectivity. Muktinath
  village has more limited but adequate services for pilgrims, including
  guesthouses, small shops, and prasad/offering vendors near the temple.

preservation: |
  Muktinath's remoteness and challenging access have historically limited
  visitor volume relative to lower-altitude pilgrimage sites, though
  growth in both religious pilgrimage and Annapurna Circuit trekking
  tourism has increased pressure on the village's infrastructure and trail
  network in recent years. The fragile high-altitude Mustang ecosystem
  warrants careful, low-impact visitation.
