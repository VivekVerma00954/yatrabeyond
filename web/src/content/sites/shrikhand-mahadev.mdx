overview: |
  Shrikhand Mahadev, also called Shrikhand Kailash or Shikhar Kailash, is a
  peak in the Nirmand sub-division of Kullu district, Himachal Pradesh,
  considered an abode of Shiva and Parvati. It is the site of a naturally
  formed rock pillar near the summit, commonly cited at around 72–75 feet
  tall, venerated by pilgrims as a self-manifested Shivling. It is widely
  described as the third most important of the Panch Kailash, and, by
  consistent consensus across trekking literature, the most physically
  demanding and dangerous of the five.

  **A note on altitude figures:** sources give the peak's altitude as
  approximately 5,227 m (about 17,150 ft), while some describe the Shivling
  formation itself as standing at around 18,570 ft, a figure that would
  place it above the stated peak altitude. This internal inconsistency,
  similar to the one flagged on the Rudranath page in the Panch Kedar
  cluster, has not been resolved here and should be checked against an
  authoritative source before publication.

history: |
  Local and pilgrimage tradition holds the rock pillar near the summit to
  be a swayambhu (self-manifested) Shivling, marking the site as sacred to
  Shiva and Parvati without any built temple. The approach trail passes
  several sites with their own attached legends: Bhimdwar, a cave
  associated with the Pandava Bhima; Parvati Bagh, said to be a garden
  associated with Parvati and also, in contemporary trekking accounts, the
  point at which altitude sickness most often begins to affect trekkers;
  and Nain Sarovar, a high-altitude lake likewise associated with Parvati
  in local tradition. None of these attributions carry settled scriptural
  citation and should be treated as regional devotional tradition pending
  expert review.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Shrikhand Mahadev's extreme difficulty is treated within Shaivite
  pilgrimage culture as itself a mark of the site's spiritual weight —
  completing the trek is regarded as a significant act of devotion and
  endurance, comparable to or exceeding the difficulty of Rudranath in the
  Panch Kedar circuit. The presence of past fatalities on the route,
  documented in multiple trekking accounts, has led Himachal Pradesh
  authorities and yatra organisers to introduce medical checkpoints and
  registration requirements for the official annual yatra, reflecting the
  genuine physical risk involved alongside the site's religious
  significance.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The official yatra window is narrow, generally
  July–August, when snow conditions make the route passable; even within
  this window, weather can change rapidly and glacier sections remain
  hazardous.

  **Getting there:** The trek begins at Jaon village, near Nirmand in
  Kullu district, reached via Rampur and the Kullu valley. From Jaon, the
  route covers roughly 32–35 km one way via Singhnag, Bhimdwar, Parvati
  Bagh, Kunsha Nala, and Nain Sarovar, before the final ascent to the
  Shivling. Most itineraries take 7–8 days round trip.

  **Registration and safety:** Given documented fatalities from altitude
  sickness, particularly above Parvati Bagh, the Himachal Pradesh
  administration has in various years required registration and set up
  medical checkpoints along the official yatra route. Confirm current
  requirements before travelling, and take any symptoms of altitude
  sickness (headache, breathlessness, dizziness, vomiting, blurred vision)
  seriously and descend immediately if they occur.

  begins. Along the route itself, expect tent camping only; there is no
  fixed accommodation infrastructure between Jaon and the summit area, and
  trekkers must be self-sufficient for the full 7–8 day duration.
