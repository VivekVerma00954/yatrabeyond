overview: |
  Kamakhya Temple, on Nilachal Hill overlooking the Brahmaputra River in
  Guwahati, Assam, is one of the oldest and most important centres of
  Tantric Hindu practice in India, and one of the classical 51 Shakti
  Peethas, marking the spot where Sati's yoni is traditionally said to
  have fallen. Unusually among major Hindu temples, Kamakhya has no
  anthropomorphic idol; the goddess is worshipped through a natural rock
  fissure resembling a yoni within the sanctum.

history: |
  The present temple is architecturally dated to the 8th–9th century,
  with substantial rebuilding history since. After destruction by
  invaders, the temple was rebuilt in the 16th century by King
  Naranarayan and his brother Chilarai of the Koch dynasty; the Ahom
  kings, particularly Rajeshwar Singha, later contributed significantly
  to its further expansion. The resulting hybrid architectural style is
  known locally as Nilachal style, reflecting the temple's layered
  reconstruction history.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Kamakhya's central annual event, the Ambubachi Mela (held in mid-June,
  during the Assamese month of Ahar), celebrates the belief that the
  goddess undergoes her own annual menstrual cycle at this time. The
  temple closes for three days during the mela, understood as a period of
  the goddess's rejuvenation; on reopening, prasad is distributed in two
  distinctive forms — Angodak (spring water) and Angabastra (a piece of
  the red cloth used to cover the yoni-shaped rock during the closure) —
  making this one of the most theologically and ritually distinctive
  celebrations at any Shakti Peetha, explicitly honouring menstruation as
  a sacred expression of fertility and creative power rather than treating
  it as a source of ritual impurity, a notable departure from more
  common practice elsewhere in Hindu tradition.
