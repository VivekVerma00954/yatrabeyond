overview: |
  Pashupatinath Temple, on the banks of the sacred Bagmati River in
  Kathmandu, is among the oldest and most significant Hindu temple
  complexes in South Asia, and Nepal's most important Shiva shrine. A
  UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979 (as part of the Kathmandu Valley
  designation), the complex spans roughly 246 hectares and includes 518
  smaller temples, ashrams, and shrines around the principal two-tiered
  pagoda housing the four-faced Pashupati linga.

  Many devotees regard Pashupatinath as the "crown" or head of the twelve
  Jyotirlingas of Shiva, and hold that a Jyotirlinga pilgrimage is
  incomplete without darshan here. **This should be treated as devotional
  belief rather than settled fact**: Pashupatinath is not part of the
  canonical list of twelve Jyotirlingas, all of which are located within
  India, and this platform's own Jyotirlinga content should not describe
  Pashupatinath as one of the twelve without flagging this distinction
  clearly.

history: |
  Records of worship at this site date back to at least the 5th century
  CE, with the current temple structure largely dating from the 17th
  century after earlier structures were rebuilt or renovated over the
  centuries. A widely told founding legend holds that Shiva and Parvati,
  in the form of deer, roamed the forest near the Bagmati River; other
  gods, seeking Shiva's return to his divine form, seized the deer by its
  horns, and one horn broke off. This horn was worshipped as a Shivalinga
  for a time before being lost and buried. Centuries later, the divine cow
  Kamadhenu was said to pour milk over a particular spot; when the ground
  was dug up, the linga was recovered and worship resumed at what is now
  Pashupatinath.

  In the mid-15th century, King Yaksha Malla of Nepal invited Bhatta
  Brahmin priests from Karnataka, South India, to take over the temple's
  rituals according to Vedic tradition — a parallel to the South Indian
  Rawal priestly tradition associated with several of the Char Dham and
  Panch Kedar shrines in India. Only these Bhatta priests are permitted to
  touch the linga and enter the innermost sanctum; this restriction applies
  even to Nepali Hindus, and entry to the main temple courtyard itself is
  restricted to Hindus generally.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Pashupatinath's role as Nepal's foremost Shiva temple gives it deep
  national religious significance beyond its status as a pilgrimage
  destination. The adjoining Aryaghat cremation grounds on the Bagmati
  River are used for last rites, including historically for Nepali royalty,
  reflecting the Hindu philosophical framing of the temple precinct as
  encompassing the full cycle of life, death, and liberation. Maha
  Shivaratri draws exceptionally large crowds of pilgrims, including many
  from India, and is the temple's single most significant annual
  observance.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** Year-round, as the temple sits in Kathmandu at a
  moderate altitude with no seasonal closure. October–November and
  February–March offer the most comfortable weather; Maha Shivaratri
  (typically February/March) draws by far the largest crowds and is a major
  festival experience in its own right, but also the most crowded time to
  visit.

  **Entry restrictions:** Only Hindus may enter the main temple courtyard
  and inner precinct; only the four Bhatta Brahmin priests may enter the
  innermost sanctum or touch the linga. Non-Hindu visitors can view the
  main pagoda from a designated area across the Bagmati River and can
  freely explore the wider UNESCO-listed complex, including many of its
  518 subsidiary shrines.

  **Getting there:** Located within Kathmandu, easily reached by taxi or
  local transport from anywhere in the city; Tribhuvan International
  Airport is the nearest airport.

familySuitability: |
  Well suited to families of all ages, given the easy urban access and lack
  of any trekking requirement. Visitors should be prepared for the sight of
  open-air cremation ceremonies at the adjoining ghats, which some younger
  children or sensitive visitors may find confronting; this is a normal
  and continuous part of temple life at this site, not an unusual event.
