overview: |
  Kamakshi Amman Temple, in the temple city of Kanchipuram, Tamil Nadu,
  is one of the classical 51 Shakti Peethas, traditionally marking the
  spot where Sati's navel fell — giving the site its alternate names
  Nabhi Peetham or Odhyana Peetham. The temple is also the seat of the
  Kanchi Kamakoti Peetham, a major Hindu monastic institution, giving
  Kamakshi Amman an institutional significance well beyond its status as
  a pilgrimage destination alone.

history: |
  The temple is believed to have been founded between the 5th and 8th
  centuries CE by the Pallava dynasty, whose capital was Kanchipuram
  itself, with further modification in the 14th and 17th centuries and
  significant expansion under later Chola and Vijayanagara rulers, who
  added numerous smaller shrines around the main sanctum. Adi
  Shankaracharya is credited with consecrating the Sri Chakra (a sacred
  geometric yantra) within the temple's sanctum specifically to pacify
  the goddess's intense energy and establish her devotional character as
  a compassionate, nurturing mother figure — a theological and ritual
  intervention that also anchors the temple's connection to the Kanchi
  Kamakoti Peetham.

spiritualSignificance: |
  A distinctive local legend recounts that the gods, tormented by the
  demon Bhandasura, took the form of parrots perching on champaka trees
  within the temple to perform penance and appeal to the goddess for
  aid — a detail reflected in the parrot iconography traditionally
  associated with both Kamakshi and the neighbouring Meenakshi tradition
  in Madurai. A separate legend describes Parvati's own journey to
  Kanchipuram to worship Shiva as Ekambareswara beneath a mango tree by
  the Vegavati river, culminating in their marriage — linking Kamakshi
  Amman devotionally to Kanchipuram's other major temple, Ekambareswarar.
