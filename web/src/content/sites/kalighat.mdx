overview: |
  Kalighat Kali Temple, in the Kalighat area of Kolkata, is one of the
  classical 51 Shakti Peethas, traditionally marking the spot where the
  toes of Sati's right foot fell. Dedicated to Kali, one of the ten
  Mahavidyas and the central deity of the Kalikula tradition of Tantric
  worship, Kalighat is among the most significant pilgrimage sites in
  Bengal and, by extension, one of the best-known Kali temples anywhere
  in the world — the name "Kolkata" itself is popularly, though not
  conclusively, linked to "Kalikata," a reference to this temple.

history: |
  The temple is referenced in literary works such as Mansar Bhasan and
  Kavi Chandi, suggesting devotional recognition of the site by at least
  the early 16th century, when a modest hut-like shrine is said to have
  been commissioned by King Manasingha. The temple as it stands today,
  however, dates from a later phase of construction, completed around
  1809 under the patronage of the Sabarna Roy Chowdhury family of
  Barisha. According to a widely told founding legend, a Brahmin named
  Atma Ram discovered a toe-shaped stone in the Bhagirathi River and,
  after a dream revealing its identity as Sati's fallen toe, established
  worship at the site; the temple's distinctive sandstone idol — with
  three large eyes, a long protruding golden tongue, and a garland of
  silver human heads — was crafted by the saints Atmaram Giri and
  Brahmananda Giri.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Kalighat's central place in Bengali Shaktism reflects the region's
  broader devotional emphasis on Kali worship, distinct in character from
  the Durga-centred and Vaishnava-centred traditions found more strongly
  elsewhere in India. The temple's continuous, high-volume daily worship
  and its location within one of India's largest cities give it a
  markedly urban devotional character compared to many of this
  platform's more remote Shakti sites.

  Kalighat also carries a further, more specific distinction: alongside
  Kamakhya, Vimala (Puri), and Tara Tarini, all covered elsewhere on this
  platform, it is named in the Devi Bhagavata Purana and Kalika Purana as
  one of the **Four Adi Shakti Peethas**, the oldest and most powerful of
  the entire network, where the goddess is referenced under the specific
  name Dakshina Kalika.
