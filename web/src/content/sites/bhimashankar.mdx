overview: |
  Bhimashankar Temple, in the Sahyadri hills of Pune district, Maharashtra,
  is one of three Jyotirlingas of Shiva located within Maharashtra and is
  traditionally counted as the sixth of the twelve. This platform lists
  the Maharashtra site as the primary Bhimashankar Jyotirlinga, based on
  its long, continuous historical and devotional recognition, its
  citation in Adi Shankaracharya's Dwadasha Jyotirlinga Stotra, and its
  Archaeological Survey of India-documented history.

  **A note on a competing claim:** a separate temple near Guwahati, Assam,
  is also asserted by some to be the true Bhimashankar Jyotirlinga, a
  claim that gained wide public attention after a 2023 Assam state
  government advertisement and public statements by Assam's Chief
  Minister. This platform treats that claim as a flagged, separate
  assertion with its own page (see below) rather than folding it into
  this one, given how recently it entered public debate relative to the
  long-established Maharashtra tradition. Readers should be told plainly
  that a genuine, actively contested claim exists.

history: |
  Local tradition dates a temple at this site to around the 12th century
  CE, though physical evidence of that original structure has not
  survived given subsequent renovation. The temple as it now stands was
  substantially renovated in 1733 by Chimaji Antaji Bhide Naik, with
  further repair work carried out by Dikshit Patwardhan in 1766.

  The presiding legend describes Shiva defeating the demon Tripurasura,
  who threatened to destroy the three worlds (heaven, earth/hell, and the
  netherworld). After the battle, Shiva is said to have rested in the
  Sahyadri hills, and the Bhima River is said to have originated from his
  sweat during this rest — the river is later known as the Chandrabhaga
  at Pandharpur, another major Maharashtra pilgrimage site.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As one of three Jyotirlingas within Maharashtra (alongside Trimbakeshwar
  and Grishneshwar), Bhimashankar holds a distinctive place within the
  state's own pilgrimage geography, in addition to its national
  significance as one of the twelve. Its location within the Bhimashankar
  Wildlife Sanctuary, a Western Ghats biodiversity hotspot and habitat for
  the endangered Indian giant squirrel (Shekru, Maharashtra's state
  animal), also gives the site an unusually strong ecological dimension
  compared to most other Jyotirlingas.

scriptures: |
  Bhimashankar is named in Adi Shankaracharya's Dwadasha Jyotirlinga
  Stotra and referenced in the Shiva Purana and other Puranic literature
  associated with the twelve Jyotirlingas, including the Shivlilamrita.
  The competing Assam claim rests on a different reading of the location
  named "Dakini" in the Koti Rudra Samhita section of the Shiva Purana.
  Specific verse-level resolution of this dispute has not been achieved by
  any single scholarly or governmental authority and should not be
  presented as settled.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to February offers the most comfortable
  weather for the ghat-road journey; the monsoon (June–September) brings
  heavy rain and lush scenery to the surrounding sanctuary but can make
  roads more difficult.

  **Getting there:** Bhimashankar is roughly 125 km from Pune, reached by
  road via Manchar and Ghodegaon; Pune has the nearest airport and major
  railway station.
