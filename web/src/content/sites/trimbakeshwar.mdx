overview: |
  Trimbakeshwar Shiva Temple, in the town of Trimbak, roughly 28 km from
  Nashik, Maharashtra, enshrines one of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Shiva
  and is unique among the twelve for its lingam's three faces, understood
  to represent Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva together in a single form. The
  temple is also revered as the source of the sacred Godavari River,
  which rises nearby on Brahmagiri hill.

history: |
  Tradition holds that the sage Gautama performed intense penance at this
  site, and that Shiva, moved by his devotion, permitted the descent of
  the Ganga in the form of the Godavari River at Trimbak — giving the
  Godavari its alternate name, the "Ganga of the South" (Dakshin Ganga).
  The present temple structure was built in the 18th century under the
  Peshwa ruler Balaji Baji Rao (Nana Saheb), replacing earlier structures
  at the site; the legend and ritual traditions associated with the
  temple are described in both the Shiva Purana and the Skanda Purana.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Trimbakeshwar's three-faced lingam gives it a distinctive theological
  significance among the twelve Jyotirlingas, understood by many devotees
  as representing the unity of the Hindu trinity (Trimurti) within a single
  Shiva manifestation. The temple's role as the source of the Godavari
  River also connects it to the wider Godavari pilgrimage tradition
  downstream, and Nashik itself, as one of the four Kumbh Mela host
  cities, draws additional pilgrimage significance to the wider region.
