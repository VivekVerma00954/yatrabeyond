overview: |
  Konark Sun Temple, on the Bay of Bengal coast roughly 35 km northeast
  of Puri, Odisha, is a 13th-century monument dedicated to Surya, the Sun
  god, designed in the form of his cosmic chariot. It is one of India's
  most architecturally celebrated temples and a UNESCO World Heritage
  Site, though it now functions primarily as a protected monument rather
  than a site of active daily worship.

history: |
  The temple is attributed to King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga
  dynasty, generally dated to around 1250 CE, and is said to have been
  built with the labour of some 1,200 artisans over a 12-year period
  (1243-1255 CE). The site is understood as a key link in the historical
  diffusion of Surya worship, a cult that scholarship traces as
  originating in Kashmir around the 8th century CE before spreading to
  eastern India, where Konark became one of its most monumental
  architectural expressions.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The temple's entire structure is conceived as Surya's chariot: 24
  elaborately carved stone wheels, each roughly 12 feet in diameter,
  represent the 12 months of the Hindu calendar, with the wheels' eight
  spokes representing the day's eight intervals, and a team of seven
  stone horses representing the seven days of the week. This
  astronomical and calendrical symbolism, worked directly into the
  temple's architecture, gives Konark a distinctive devotional-scientific
  character among Surya temples specifically, reflecting Surya's role in
  governing time itself.
