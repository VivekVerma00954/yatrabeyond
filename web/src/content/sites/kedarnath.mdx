overview: |
  Kedarnath is one of the twelve Jyotirlingas — manifestations of Shiva as a
  pillar of light — and occupies the highest position among the five
  Panch Kedar shrines of Uttarakhand. It is also the final stop in the
  prestigious Char Dham pilgrimage circuit that includes Yamunotri,
  Gangotri, and Badrinath.

  The temple stands at 3,583 metres above sea level in the Rudraprayag
  district of Uttarakhand, on the banks of the Mandakini river, surrounded
  by snow-capped Himalayan peaks. Open only from late April or early May
  to early November (the exact dates are announced annually based on
  auspicious timings), the site is inaccessible in winter due to heavy
  snowfall.

  The presiding deity is Shiva in the form of Kedaresvara — the Lord of the
  Field. The lingam enshrined here is one of the most venerated in the
  Shaivite tradition, with the unusual form of a triangular rock said to
  represent the hump of a bull (Nandi).

history: |
  The origins of the Kedarnath temple are traditionally attributed to the
  Pandavas of the Mahabharata. According to the devotional account, after
  the Kurukshetra war, the Pandavas sought Lord Shiva's blessings and
  absolution. Shiva evaded them by taking the form of a bull and
  submerging into the earth; the hump emerged at Kedarnath, while other
  parts appeared at the four other Panch Kedar sites.

  The current stone structure is widely attributed to Adi Shankaracharya,
  the 8th-century philosopher-saint who is credited with reviving the
  Char Dham pilgrimage circuit. Shankaracharya is said to have
  attained samadhi (liberation) at Kedarnath at the age of 32; a samadhi
  shrine behind the main temple commemorates this.

  Historically, the site was administered by the Rawals — priests
  traditionally from the Veerashaiva community of Karnataka — a tradition
  that continues today under the Badrinath-Kedarnath Temples Committee.

  The temple survived centuries intact but was severely damaged by the
  catastrophic flash floods of June 2013, when the Chorabari glacier lake
  burst. A massive boulder behind the temple is credited by many pilgrims
  with deflecting the worst of the debris flow. Post-2013 reconstruction
  was extensive, and the shrine reopened to pilgrims in 2014. Ongoing
  reconstruction and beautification work has continued since, including a
  redevelopment project initiated around 2019.

spiritualSignificance: |
  For Shaivites, darshan (sacred sight) of the Kedarnath Jyotirlinga is
  considered one of the most potent spiritual acts in human life.
  The Jyotirlinga tradition holds that these twelve sites mark places where
  Shiva manifested as a self-arising column of light (jyoti = light,
  linga = column or symbol), transcending the usual forms of worship.

  Kedarnath holds a particular resonance in the Himalayan pilgrimage
  tradition as a site of liberation. The extreme altitude, difficult trek,
  and harsh climate are themselves understood as a form of tapas (austerity)
  that prepares the devotee's consciousness for encounter with the divine.

  The setting — between the Kedarnath and Bharatkunth peaks, on the banks
  of the Mandakini, with the ancient glacier lake Chorabari nearby —
  is understood in the tradition as a liminal space between the human and
  divine realms.

scriptures: |
  Kedarnath is referenced in several Puranic texts, most prominently the
  **Shiva Purana** (particularly the Kothi Rudra Samhita and Kailash
  Samhita sections) and the **Skanda Purana** (Kedara Khanda). These
  texts describe the origin of the twelve Jyotirlingas and the events
  by which Shiva came to reside at Kedarnath.

  The Mahabharata (Vana Parva / Aranyaka Parva) contains passages relating
  to the Pandavas' pilgrimage through the Himalayas, which form the basis
  of the devotional narrative associating the temple's origins with the
  Pandavas. **Specific chapter and verse citations require expert
  verification before publication.**

  The Kedara Khanda of the Skanda Purana is considered by many scholars
  to be the primary ancient textual authority for the mythology and
  ritual practices of this region. The text likely reached its current
  form over several centuries and is the subject of ongoing scholarly
  study.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit**

  The temple opens annually around late April or early May (on Akshaya
  Tritiya or a nearby auspicious date, announced by the Badrinath-Kedarnath
  Temples Committee) and closes for winter on the auspicious date following
  Diwali — typically in late October or early November. The doors then
  travel to Ukhimath in the Rudraprayag valley, where the deity is
  worshipped through the winter months.

  Peak season is May–June and September–October, when weather is most
  stable. Monsoon (July–August) brings heavy rain, landslide risk, and
  difficult trekking conditions; travel is possible but not recommended for
  first-time pilgrims.

  **Getting there**

  The nearest railhead is Rishikesh (approximately 220 km). The nearest
  airport is Jolly Grant Airport, Dehradun (approximately 240 km).
  Regular bus and shared taxi services operate from Rishikesh and
  Haridwar to Gaurikund (the trek base camp).

  From Gaurikund, the trek to Kedarnath is approximately 16–22 km depending
  on the route taken, with an ascent of around 1,400 metres. Allow 6–8
  hours for the ascent. Helicopter services from multiple helipads (Phata,
  Sersi, Guptkashi, Sitapur) reduce travel time to under 30 minutes —
  booking well in advance is essential.

  **Registration**

  Biometric registration is mandatory for Char Dham yatra. Pilgrims must
  register at authorised centres or via the official Uttarakhand government
  portal before departure. Carry government-issued photo ID.

  **Temple timings (approximate — verify with Badrinath-Kedarnath Temples Committee)**

  - Morning darshan: approximately 6:00 am – 3:00 pm
  - Afternoon break: 3:00 pm – 5:00 pm
  - Evening aarti (Rudrabhishek): 5:00 pm – 7:00 pm

familySuitability: |
  Kedarnath is achievable for families, including older children, with
  careful preparation. The trek is long and at altitude; children under
  8–10 years may find it very challenging. Helicopter services make the
  site accessible for families who would not otherwise be able to complete
  the trek.

  **Key considerations for families:**
  - Acclimatise for 1–2 days at Rishikesh, Haridwar, or Gaurikund before
    the trek.
  - Start the trek early (by 5–6 am) to reach before afternoon weather
    changes.
  - Pony, palanquin (doli), and porter services are readily available.
  - Adequate warm clothing is essential; temperatures can drop sharply
    even in summer.
  - Carry high-energy snacks and water-purification tablets or bottled water.
  - No ATMs are available at Kedarnath; carry sufficient cash.

accommodation: |
  Accommodation at Kedarnath is basic and functional — the site is a
  high-altitude shrine, not a resort. Options include:

  - **Garhwal Mandal Vikas Nigam (GMVN) guesthouses and tent complexes**
    near the temple — basic dormitories and tent accommodation; book in
    advance through the GMVN website.
  - **Private dharamshalas and ashrams** near the temple — basic but clean;
    advance booking strongly recommended during May–June and September–October.
  - **Camp sites** operated by private operators along the trek route.

  For those using helicopter services, it is common to ascend in the morning
  and descend the same day, avoiding the need for overnight accommodation at
  the summit. Most pilgrims spend the night at Phata, Guptkashi, or
  Gaurikund (base camp), which have much wider accommodation options
  including mid-range hotels and dharamshalas.

  **Prices and availability change year to year.** Always book accommodation
  well in advance for peak season (May–June, September–October).

preservation: |
  The 2013 floods caused catastrophic damage to Kedarnath and the surrounding
  valley, killing thousands of pilgrims and residents and destroying large
  portions of the infrastructure. The reconstruction effort has been
  extensive, and the Kedarnath Development Authority was established to
  oversee the redevelopment of the zone.

  Ongoing environmental concerns include:
  - **Glacier recession:** The Chorabari glacier continues to recede due to
    climate change, with implications for the Mandakini's water supply and
    the risk of future glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs).
  - **Over-tourism:** Peak season sees hundreds of thousands of pilgrims in
    a compressed period, placing significant pressure on the fragile
    high-altitude ecosystem.
  - **Solid waste:** Despite improvements, waste management at the site
    remains a challenge.
  - **Construction impact:** The ongoing redevelopment around the temple has
    raised questions among heritage conservationists about the preservation
    of the site's historical character.

  The National Green Tribunal (NGT) and the Supreme Court of India have
  issued guidelines around construction and visitor management in the
  Kedarnath eco-sensitive zone.

givingBack: |
  Pilgrims can support the preservation and community welfare of
  Kedarnath in several ways:

  - **Badrinath-Kedarnath Temples Committee (BKTC):** Donations to the
    official temple trust directly support the shrine's maintenance,
    services for pilgrims, and support for local priests and workers.
    Verify donation channels on the official BKTC website.
  - **Leave No Trace:** The single most impactful thing a pilgrim can do
    is carry all waste back to Gaurikund for proper disposal.
  - **Local economy:** Use locally operated accommodation, guides, and
    service providers rather than large outside operators.
  - **Avoid single-use plastic:** Carry a reusable water bottle and
    use water refill points where available.
  - **Support local NGOs:** Several NGOs work on disaster preparedness,
    ecosystem restoration, and community welfare in the Kedarnath valley.
    Research reputable organisations before donating.
