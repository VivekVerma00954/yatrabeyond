overview: |
  Somnath Temple, at Prabhas Patan on the Arabian Sea coast in Gujarat's
  Gir Somnath district, is traditionally regarded as the first among the
  twelve Jyotirlingas of Shiva. Dedicated to Shiva as Somnath ("Lord of the
  Moon"), the temple has one of the most dramatic and well-documented
  histories of destruction and reconstruction of any major Hindu temple in
  India, spanning roughly a millennium, and its modern reconstruction is
  closely tied to the political history of independent India.

history: |
  Temple structures are believed to have stood at this site since ancient
  times, with recorded reconstructions from as early as the reign of King
  Bhima I of Gujarat in the 11th century. In 1026 CE, the Turkic ruler
  Mahmud of Ghazni raided and plundered the temple, breaking the jyotirlinga
  and carrying away substantial wealth — an event that remains one of the
  most cited episodes in accounts of temple destruction in Indian history.
  The temple was destroyed and rebuilt multiple further times, notably in
  1297 and 1394, and again under the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb in 1706.
  King Kumarapala rebuilt it in stone in the 12th century, and the Maratha
  queen Ahilyabai Holkar funded a further reconstruction in the 18th
  century at a site adjacent to the original.

  The present temple dates from independent India. Sardar Vallabhbhai
  Patel, then India's Deputy Prime Minister and Home Minister, pledged to
  rebuild the temple following Junagadh's accession to India, laying the
  foundation stone in November 1947. Mahatma Gandhi endorsed the plan but
  specified that reconstruction funding should come from public
  contribution rather than the state. Construction proceeded through 1950,
  and the rebuilt temple, in Chalukyan architectural style, was
  consecrated on 11 May 1951 in the presence of India's first President,
  Dr. Rajendra Prasad — a moment widely described as a significant act of
  cultural and national self-assertion in the early republic.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As the traditional first of the twelve Jyotirlingas, Somnath holds a
  foundational place in the Jyotirlinga pilgrimage tradition. Its long
  history of destruction and rebuilding has also given it a distinct
  symbolic weight in modern Indian public life, frequently invoked as an
  emblem of cultural resilience and continuity. The temple sits directly
  on the Arabian Sea coast, with the sea itself forming part of the site's
  devotional and scenic character.
