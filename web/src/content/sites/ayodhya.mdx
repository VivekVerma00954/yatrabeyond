overview: |
  Ayodhya, on the banks of the Saryu River in Uttar Pradesh, is
  traditionally revered as the birthplace of Rama and the capital of his
  kingdom, making it one of the most significant Vaishnava pilgrimage
  cities in India. It contains a dense cluster of major temples spanning
  many centuries of construction and reconstruction, most prominently the
  newly built Ram Mandir (consecrated 2024), the ancient Hanuman Garhi,
  the "Golden Palace" Kanak Bhawan, the Shiva-dedicated Nageshwarnath
  Temple, and the historically significant Treta Ke Thakur (Treta
  Mandir). This page introduces Ayodhya as a pilgrimage city as a whole;
  each of its major temples has its own full page with site-specific
  history and practical information.

history: |
  Ayodhya's identity as Rama's birthplace and capital is rooted in the
  Ramayana and is treated as foundational to Hindu devotional geography.
  The city's individual temples have widely varying, independently
  documented construction and reconstruction histories, spanning from
  claimed ancient origins (Nageshwarnath, attributed to Rama's son Kush)
  through the Vikramaditya-era temple-building tradition referenced at
  several sites (Kanak Bhawan, Treta Ke Thakur), medieval and early modern
  reconstruction under regional Hindu patrons including the Raja of
  Kullu and the Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar, and the most recent Ram
  Mandir, completed in 2024.

  **The Ram Janmabhoomi–Babri Masjid dispute:** the site now occupied by
  the Ram Mandir was, for over four centuries, occupied by the Babri
  Masjid, a mosque built around 1528–1529. Hindus long held the site to be
  the exact birthplace of Rama; the mosque was the subject of an
  extended, multi-decade legal and political dispute, becoming a site of
  intense national controversy. In December 1992, the mosque was
  demolished by a large crowd of activists, an event that triggered
  serious nationwide communal violence and remains one of the most
  significant and contested episodes in modern Indian history. In 2019,
  India's Supreme Court ruled that the disputed site be awarded to Hindu
  litigants for construction of a temple, while allocating separate land
  nearby, at Dhannipur, for a mosque to be built by the Muslim community.
  Construction of the Ram Mandir began in 2020 and the temple was
  consecrated in January 2024, with full construction completion marked
  in a further ceremony (Dhwajarohan) in November 2025.

  This platform presents the dispute's key documented facts and the
  Supreme Court's ruling neutrally, without taking a position on the
  underlying religious or historical claims that were contested; readers
  seeking deeper analysis of the dispute's history and its social and
  political dimensions should consult dedicated historical and journalistic
  sources.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Ayodhya's cluster of temples collectively allow pilgrims to trace a
  devotional narrative of Rama's life — his birthplace (Ram Mandir), his
  father's traditional residence, his protector Hanuman's continued
  presence (Hanuman Garhi), his wife Sita's dedicated residence (Kanak
  Bhawan), and the site of his post-war Ashwamedha Yajna (Treta Ke
  Thakur) — within a single, compact pilgrimage city. Since the 2024
  consecration, Ayodhya has also become one of the most visited pilgrimage
  destinations in India, with several million visitors in the weeks
  immediately following the temple's public opening alone.

scriptures: |

  Kanak Bhawan to Sita, Rama's Ashwamedha Yajna at Treta Ke Thakur) are

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to March offers the most comfortable
  weather; Ram Navami (Rama's birth anniversary, usually March or April)
  and Diwali (closely associated with Rama's return to Ayodhya) are the
  city's most significant annual festivals, drawing exceptionally large
  crowds.

  **Getting there:** Ayodhya has its own airport (Maharishi Valmiki
  International Airport, opened 2024) and railway station with
  significantly expanded connectivity following the Ram Mandir's
  consecration; Lucknow is a further, larger regional air and rail hub.

  **Getting around:** The major temples are within the city and connected
  by road; given very high visitor volumes since 2024, allow extra time
  for queues and crowd management at the Ram Mandir in particular.
