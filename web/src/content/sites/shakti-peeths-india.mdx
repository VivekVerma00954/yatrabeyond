overview: |
  Shakti Peethas are sites across the Indian subcontinent, sacred to the
  goddess (Devi/Shakti), traditionally understood as marking the places
  where parts of the goddess Sati's body or ornaments fell after Vishnu
  dismembered her corpse to end Shiva's grief-stricken cosmic dance. The
  number of Shakti Peethas varies significantly by source — commonly
  cited figures are 51 (per the Devi Bhagavata Purana, the most widely
  followed count), 52 (per the Tantra Chudamani and Markandeya Purana), or
  108 (per an alternate, fuller Puranic enumeration) — and no single
  official, universally agreed list exists.

  This page curates ten of the most widely visited and devotionally
  significant Shakti sites across India and, in two cases, neighbouring
  Pakistan: **Kamakhya** (Assam), **Kalighat** (Kolkata), **Vishalakshi**
  (Varanasi), **Kamakshi Amman** (Kanchipuram), **Ambaji** (Gujarat),
  **Mahalakshmi** (Kolhapur), **Biraja** (Jajpur, Odisha), **Hinglaj
  Mata** (Balochistan, Pakistan), and **Sharada Peeth** (Pakistan-
  administered Kashmir), plus **Baglamukhi** (Nalkheda, Madhya Pradesh).
  Not all ten carry the same classification: eight are widely counted
  among the classical 51 (Kamakhya, Kalighat, Vishalakshi, Kamakshi
  Amman, Ambaji, Biraja, Hinglaj Mata, and Sharada Peeth); **Mahalakshmi
  Kolhapur is more consistently identified as one of the separate 18 Maha
  Shakti Peethas** (per the Ashta Dasa Shakti Peetha Stotram attributed to
  Adi Shankaracharya) rather than the core 51; and **Baglamukhi is not a
  Shakti Peetha in the Sati-dismemberment sense at all** — it is one of
  the ten Mahavidya (Tantric wisdom-goddess) shrines, a related but
  textually distinct devotional category. This platform makes these
  distinctions explicit on each site's own page rather than presenting
  all ten uniformly as "Shakti Peethas."

  Himachal Pradesh's own cluster of major Devi temples (Jwala Ji,
  Chintpurni, Brajeshwari, Naina Devi, and Chamunda Devi) is covered on
  its own separate overview page, and Vaishno Devi — widely regarded as
  one of the four most important Shakti shrines in popular devotion, but
  not part of the classical 51 in the strict Sati-dismemberment sense —
  has its own dedicated page elsewhere on this platform.

history: |
  Each of the ten sites in this cluster has its own independent history,
  detailed on its own page, spanning documented antiquity (Kamakhya,
  8th–9th century construction with earlier origins; Kamakshi Amman,
  5th–8th century Pallava-era founding), well-attested medieval and
  early modern reconstruction (Kalighat, 1809; Naina Devi-style hilltop
  patterns repeated at Ambaji and Biraja), and, in the case of Hinglaj
  Mata and Sharada Peeth, sites whose accessibility and even physical
  condition have been profoundly shaped by 20th-century Partition-era
  and subsequent geopolitical history rather than by any single ancient
  event.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Collectively, these ten sites represent a very wide range of Shakta
  devotional practice — from Kamakhya's tantric tradition and unique
  menstruation-honouring Ambubachi Mela, to Kalighat's central place in
  Bengali Kali worship, to Kamakshi Amman's role as the seat of the Kanchi
  Kamakoti Peetham (a major monastic institution), to Sharada Peeth's
  distinct historical identity as an ancient centre of learning as much
  as a devotional site. No single pilgrimage sequence unites all ten in
  the way the Char Dham or Panch Kedar circuits are structured; each is
  typically visited independently or as part of regional itineraries.

scriptures: |
  The core Sati-dismemberment narrative underlying most of these sites
  draws on the Devi Bhagavata Purana, the Kalika Purana, and various
  Shakti Peetha Stotram texts. Mahalakshmi Kolhapur's more specific
  identification with the Ashta Dasa Shakti Peetha Stotram, and
  Baglamukhi's identity within the separate Dasa Mahavidya tradition, are
  addressed on their own pages. Specific verse-level citation for each
  individual site requires expert verification, addressed site by site.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to March offers the most comfortable weather
  across most of these sites; Kamakhya's Ambubachi Mela (mid-June) is a
  notable exception, timed specifically to the monsoon season.

  **Accessibility varies enormously:** most of the eight India-based
  sites are straightforward road/rail/air destinations; Hinglaj Mata and
  Sharada Peeth, both across the border in Pakistan and Pakistan-
  administered Kashmir respectively, are effectively inaccessible to most
  Indian pilgrims under current travel conditions, and are included here
  for their devotional and historical significance rather than as
  practical near-term travel destinations for an Indian audience.
