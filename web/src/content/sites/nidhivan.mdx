overview: |
  Nidhivan ("Forest of Treasure"), in Vrindavan, is a small, dense sacred
  forest rather than a conventional temple, closely associated with the
  16th-century saint-musician Swami Haridas, who is said to have
  meditated and practised his devotional music here. It is considered one
  of the most significant sites associated with the pastimes (lilas) of
  Radha, Krishna, and their companion gopis, and is closely linked to the
  origin legend of the Banke Bihari deity, now housed in its own separate
  temple nearby.

history: |
  In the 16th century, Vrindavan was still substantially forested, and
  Swami Haridas is said to have chosen this particular secluded spot to
  practise his devotional music and meditation. According to temple
  tradition, it was here that his singing invoked the appearance of Radha
  and Krishna, who — at his request — merged into the single combined
  form later installed as the deity Banke Bihari. Nidhivan itself remains
  a living forest rather than a built monument, distinguished by its
  distinctive Tulsi (holy basil) trees, which grow in unusual paired
  formations with hollow, twisting trunks bending toward the ground.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Nidhivan is widely believed among devotees to be the nightly site of
  Radha and Krishna's Rasleela (their divine dance), even in the present
  day — a belief taken seriously enough that the forest is physically
  closed to visitors after dark. Local tradition holds that the forest's
  paired Tulsi trees transform each night into the gopi companions of
  Radha and Krishna to join the dance, resuming their plant form by dawn,
  and that anyone attempting to witness the nightly Rasleela risks losing
  their sanity, their speech, or their life. Regardless of how literally
  a given visitor holds this specific belief, the forest's daily closure
  ritual and its unusual, undisturbed tree growth are treated with
  serious, consistent devotional respect by the local community.
