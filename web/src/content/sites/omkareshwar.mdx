overview: |
  Omkareshwar Temple sits on Mandhata Island in the Narmada River, in
  Madhya Pradesh's Khandwa district, and enshrines one of the twelve
  Jyotirlingas of Shiva. The island itself is said to be shaped like the
  Devanagari "Om" symbol, giving the site its name. A second, closely
  associated shrine, Mamleshwar (also called Amaleshwar or Amareshwar),
  stands on the mainland directly across the river; the two are often
  described together as a paired Jyotirlinga site, though the Omkareshwar
  shrine on the island is the one most commonly identified as the twelfth
  canonical Jyotirlinga.

history: |
  Tradition holds that King Mandhata, an ancestor of Rama from the
  Ikshvaku dynasty, performed intense worship of Shiva at this site until
  Shiva manifested here as a Jyotirlinga in response. Historical
  inscriptions confirm long-standing patronage of the site: in 1063 CE,
  the Paramara king Udayaditya installed stone inscriptions bearing
  Sanskrit stotras dedicated to the Amaleshwar (Mamleshwar) temple. In the
  18th century, the Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar funded reconstruction
  of both the Omkareshwar and Mamleshwar shrines, work that shaped much of
  the temple architecture visible today.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Omkareshwar's island setting, and its association with the sacred
  syllable "Om" through the shape of the island itself, give the site a
  distinctive symbolic resonance beyond its status as a Jyotirlinga. The
  Narmada River, one of India's most sacred rivers, adds further
  devotional weight, and circumambulation of the river (Narmada
  Parikrama) is a significant separate pilgrimage tradition that passes
  through this site.

scriptures: |
  Omkareshwar is referenced in the Shiva Purana's account of the twelve
  Jyotirlingas. The King Mandhata legend and the Amaleshwar inscriptions
  are documented in a mix of devotional and epigraphic sources; exact
  verse-level scriptural citation requires expert verification before
  publication.
