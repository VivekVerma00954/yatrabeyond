overview: |
  The Char Dham ("four abodes") of India is a set of four major
  pilgrimage sites established as a unified circuit by the 8th-century
  philosopher-saint Adi Shankaracharya: **Badrinath** in the north
  (Uttarakhand), **Dwarka** in the west (Gujarat), **Puri** in the east
  (Odisha), and **Rameshwaram** in the south (Tamil Nadu). Three of the
  four — Badrinath, Dwarka, and Puri — are Vaishnava sites where Vishnu
  is worshipped in different forms; Rameshwaram is uniquely a Shaivite
  site and simultaneously one of the twelve Jyotirlingas, giving the
  circuit a distinctive blend of Vaishnava and Shaivite significance
  rather than belonging exclusively to one tradition.

  This page introduces the pan-Indian Char Dham as a whole; each site has
  its own full page with site-specific history, access, and practical
  information. Note that this circuit is entirely distinct from the
  separate Himalayan "Chota Char Dham" of Uttarakhand (Yamunotri,
  Gangotri, Kedarnath, and Badrinath) — Badrinath alone belongs to both.

history: |
  Adi Shankaracharya is credited with establishing this fourfold
  pilgrimage system as part of a broader project to unify diverse and
  regionally fragmented currents within Hindu religious practice into a
  single, pan-Indian devotional and institutional framework. Alongside
  the four pilgrimage sites themselves, he is credited with founding four
  corresponding monastic institutions (mathas) — headquartered at Dwarka
  in the west, Puri in the east, Badarikashrama (near Badrinath) in the
  north, and Sringeri Sharada Peetham in the south — extending the Char
  Dham's significance beyond pilgrimage into an enduring institutional
  structure of Hindu monastic authority that continues in some form
  today.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Adi Shankaracharya's framework assigns each of the four dhams to one of
  the four cosmic ages (yugas) of Hindu cosmology: Badrinath to Satya
  Yuga, Rameshwaram to Treta Yuga, Dwarka to Dvapara Yuga, and Puri to
  Kali Yuga (the present age). Many Hindus hold that completing pilgrimage
  to all four sites in a single lifetime is a significant step toward
  moksha (liberation from the cycle of rebirth), making the Char Dham one
  of the most consequential pan-Indian pilgrimage undertakings in Hindu
  devotional life, distinct from — though sometimes combined with — the
  physically harder Himalayan Chota Char Dham of Uttarakhand.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **The four sites:** Badrinath (Uttarakhand, north), Dwarka (Gujarat,
  west), Puri (Odisha, east), and Rameshwaram (Tamil Nadu, south) — each
  reached independently by road, rail, or air, with no fixed order
  required, unlike the sequential Himalayan Chota Char Dham circuit.

  **Combining with other circuits:** Because Badrinath is shared with the
  Chota Char Dham of Uttarakhand, and Rameshwaram is also one of the
  twelve Jyotirlingas, pilgrims sometimes combine a Char Dham of India
  journey with other pilgrimage goals at the same sites — check each
  individual site's own page for these overlaps.

  **Season:** Given the geographic spread — from the high Himalaya to
  three very different coastal climates — there is no single best season
  for all four; each site's own page gives location-specific guidance.
  Badrinath in particular is only open roughly May to October/November,
  unlike the other three, which are accessible year-round.
