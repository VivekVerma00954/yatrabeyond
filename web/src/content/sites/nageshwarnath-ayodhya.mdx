overview: |
  Nageshwarnath Temple, at Ram Ki Paidi on the banks of the Saryu River in
  Ayodhya, is a Shiva temple traditionally held to be among the oldest in
  the city, with its establishment attributed to Kush, one of Rama's twin
  sons, tracing the site's devotional origins back to the Treta Yuga in
  Hindu cosmological tradition. Unlike most of Ayodhya's major temples,
  which centre on Rama or Hanuman, Nageshwarnath is distinctly Shaivite,
  adding a further dimension to the city's temple landscape.

history: |
  According to the temple's founding legend, Kush lost an armlet while
  bathing in the Saryu River; the armlet was recovered by a Nag-Kanya (a
  serpent maiden) who subsequently fell in love with him. As she was
  herself a devotee of Shiva, Kush is said to have built this temple in
  her honour, giving the site its name (Nageshwarnath, "Lord of Serpents,"
  referencing the Naga/serpent association of the legend). Tradition holds
  that the temple remained in reasonably good condition through to the
  era of King Vikramaditya, the legendary Ujjain ruler credited with
  temple-building activity at several sites across Ayodhya. The present
  temple structure is dated to 1750 CE, built under the sponsorship of
  Naval Rai, a minister serving under Safdar Jung.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As one of the few prominent Shaivite sites within an otherwise strongly
  Vaishnava pilgrimage city, Nageshwarnath offers Ayodhya's pilgrims a
  distinct devotional experience alongside the city's Rama-centred
  temples. The temple's Maha Shivaratri celebrations are particularly
  significant locally, featuring a Shiv Barat procession that draws large
  numbers of devotees.
