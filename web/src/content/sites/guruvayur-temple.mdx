overview: |
  Guruvayur Sri Krishna Temple, in Thrissur district, Kerala, is one of
  the most revered Krishna temples in India and is popularly known as
  the "Dwarka of the South." Its deity, Guruvayurappan, is worshipped as
  Krishna in a four-armed form carrying the conch, discus, mace, and
  lotus, and the temple is counted among Kerala's most important
  pilgrimage sites, drawing millions of devotees annually.

history: |
  Temple tradition attributes Guruvayur's founding to Brihaspati (guru of
  the Devas) and Vayu (the wind god), giving the town its name and an
  original identity as "Bhooloka Vaikuntha" (Vaikuntha on Earth); some
  devotional accounts describe the site's underlying antiquity as
  extending back several thousand years, though the standing temple
  structure is considerably more recent, generally attributed to a
  rebuilding under the Zamorin rulers of Calicut around the 16th
  century. The temple faced two major historical threats: a 1716 Dutch
  raid that looted treasures and burned the western gopuram (rebuilt in
  1747), and Tipu Sultan's invasion of Kerala in 1789, during which the
  main idol was secretly relocated to the Ambalappuzha Sree Krishna
  Swamy Temple for safekeeping before being returned.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Guruvayur is counted among the 108 Abhimana Kshetrams honoured within
  the Vaishnava devotional tradition, and its Krishna form here is
  understood as especially responsive to devotees, with an extensive
  tradition of miracle narratives associated with the site. The temple's
  elephants (particularly its famous tusker Guruvayur Kesavan) and its
  wedding (vivaham) and tonsure (chowla) ritual services are especially
  well-known features of its devotional life.
