overview: |
  This temple, on Dakini Hill near Guwahati in Assam's Kamrup region, is
  asserted by some, including the Assam state government, to be the true
  Bhimashankar Jyotirlinga of Shiva — a claim that stands in direct
  contrast to the far more widely recognised Bhimashankar temple in Pune
  district, Maharashtra (see that page for the primary listing used on
  this platform). This page presents the Assam claim in its own right, as
  a genuinely asserted alternative rather than dismissing it, while making
  clear that it is not the site this platform treats as the primary
  Bhimashankar Jyotirlinga.

history: |
  The core of this claim rests on the name "Dakini," referenced in the
  Koti Rudra Samhita section of the Shiva Purana as the location of the
  Bhimashankar Jyotirlinga. Advocates of the Assam claim read "Dakini" as
  referring to the Dakini Hills near Guwahati, in what was historically
  the Kamrup region of ancient Assam. This reading came to wide public
  attention in February 2023, when the Assam state government published
  an advertisement asserting the claim, and Assam's Chief Minister,
  Himanta Biswa Sarma, publicly stated that the temple "already exists in
  Assam" and that Assam was not seeking to "shift" the Jyotirlinga from
  Maharashtra, but rather asserting a pre-existing, separate site.

spiritualSignificance: |
  For devotees who accept this reading of "Dakini," the site carries the
  same devotional weight attributed to a Jyotirlinga location. Its
  significance in the wider Hindu pilgrimage landscape, however, is
  considerably more localised and recent in terms of public recognition
  compared to the Maharashtra site, which has centuries of continuous
  pan-Indian devotional recognition and is the site named in most
  historical and devotional literature associated with the Jyotirlinga
  tradition.

scriptures: |
  The claim rests specifically on interpreting "Dakini" in the Koti Rudra
  Samhita of the Shiva Purana as this location rather than the Sahyadri
  hills location associated with the Maharashtra temple. This is a
  genuine textual ambiguity, not a fabricated claim, but it has not
  achieved the same scholarly or devotional consensus as the Maharashtra
  identification, and readers should understand this distinction clearly.
