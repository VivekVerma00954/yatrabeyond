overview: |
  Dwarkadhish Temple, also called Jagat Mandir, in Dwarka on Gujarat's
  Saurashtra coast, is dedicated to Krishna, worshipped here as
  Dwarkadhish ("Lord of Dwarka"). It is the western site of the four
  pan-Indian Char Dham established by Adi Shankaracharya in the 8th
  century, alongside Badrinath, Puri, and Rameshwaram, and is also one of
  the Sapta Puri, the seven holy cities of Hindu tradition. Dwarka is
  traditionally identified as Krishna's own capital city, adding a
  distinct historical-mythological dimension not shared by the other
  three Char Dham.

history: |
  Archaeological findings suggest a temple structure existed at this site
  from as early as 200 BCE, with devotional tradition holding that the
  original temple was established by Vajranabh, Krishna's great-grandson.
  The temple was substantially rebuilt and enlarged in the 15th–16th
  century, and the current image of Dwarkadhish was installed in 1559.
  Adi Shankaracharya is recorded as having visited the shrine during his
  8th-century establishment of the Char Dham system, and the nearby
  Dwarka Pitha is one of the four principal monastic institutions (mathas)
  he founded, with its seat at Dwarka serving the western region of his
  fourfold monastic organisation.

  The main shrine, a five-storied structure supported by 72 pillars, has
  two principal gateways: the northern Moksha Dwara ("Door to Salvation"),
  opening toward the town's main market, and the southern Swarga Dwara
  ("Gate to Heaven"), leading down 56 steps to the ghats of the Gomati
  River.

spiritualSignificance: |
  In the Char Dham symbolism attributed to Adi Shankaracharya, Dwarka
  represents Dvapara Yuga, the age in which Krishna's own life and the
  events of the Mahabharata are traditionally set — giving Dwarka a
  unique status among the four dhams as the one most directly tied to a
  specific incarnation's earthly life and reign, rather than a more
  distant mythological or cosmological association. Dwarka's coastal
  setting and proximity to the submerged remains associated in some
  traditions with the ancient city add a further layer of devotional and
  historical fascination for pilgrims.
