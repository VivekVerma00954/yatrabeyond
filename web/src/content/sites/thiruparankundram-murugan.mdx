overview: |
  Thiruparankundram Murugan Temple, roughly 8 km southwest of Madurai, is
  the first of the Arupadaiveedu, the six traditional abodes of Murugan
  (Kartikeya) in Tamil Nadu. Carved directly into a single rock hillock
  rising 1,050 feet above sea level, it is revered as the site of
  Murugan's marriage to Deivanai, daughter of Indra, following his
  victory over the demon Surapadman.

history: |
  The original rock-cut temple is attributed to the early Pandya rulers
  of the 6th to 8th centuries CE. Historical sources also record a
  distinctive interruption in the temple's Hindu identity: it is said to
  have been converted into a Jain shrine under the Pandya king known as
  Koon Pandiyan, before being restored to Hindu worship in the latter
  part of the 8th century CE under the minister Gajapathy. The later
  Nayak rulers (1559-1736 CE) added the temple's imposing seven-tiered
  Rajagopuram, standing roughly 150 feet high, along with the
  48-pillared Asthaana Mandapam.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Thiruparankundram is revered as the site of Murugan's wedding to
  Deivanai after his defeat of Surapadman, and the deity is depicted here
  in his distinctive Kalyana Murugan ("wedding Murugan") seated form
  alongside his consort, rather than the standing form more typical of
  other Murugan temples. The temple is also unusual for containing five
  rock-cut sanctums within a single shrine complex — dedicated to
  Murugan, Shiva, Vishnu, Ganesha, and Durga — with Shiva and Vishnu
  positioned facing one another, a rare configuration in Hindu temple
  architecture. This is also the only Arupadaiveedu temple where
  abhishekam (ceremonial bathing) is performed on Murugan's vel (spear)
  itself rather than solely on the deity's image.
