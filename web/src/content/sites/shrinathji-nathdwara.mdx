overview: |
  Shrinathji Temple in Nathdwara, Rajasthan, is one of India's most
  important Krishna pilgrimage sites and the primary seat of the
  Pushtimarg ("Path of Grace") Vaishnava tradition founded by the
  16th-century saint Vallabhacharya. Its deity, Shrinathji, depicts
  Krishna as a seven-year-old child in the act of lifting Govardhan Hill
  to shelter the people of Braj, and the temple's arrival in Nathdwara is
  itself tied directly to Mughal-era religious persecution.

history: |
  According to Pushtimarg tradition, the Shrinathji image was originally
  discovered and installed for worship at Govardhan, near Mathura, by
  Vallabhacharya, and the deity was later named "Shri Nath Ji" by his son
  Vitthalnathji (also called Gusainji), who formally established the
  Pushtimarg sect's devotional framework around it. In 1672, amid fears
  of the image's destruction under the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb, the
  deity was moved from Braj for safekeeping; according to temple
  tradition, the chariot carrying the image became stuck in the mud at
  what is now Nathdwara, interpreted as a sign that the deity wished to
  remain there, and Maharana Raj Singh of Mewar granted protection and
  land for the temple that was subsequently built on the spot.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Shrinathji's Govardhan-lifting form directly recalls the Krishna
  legend of protecting the cowherds and cattle of Braj from Indra's
  wrath by holding up the hill on his finger, and Nathdwara has, since
  1672, functioned as the central seat of the Pushtimarg tradition,
  whose distinctive devotional practice (seva) treats the deity as a
  living child requiring detailed daily care, dress, and a changing
  sequence of eight daily darshans (Ashta Yam Seva), rather than the more
  common single or twice-daily darshan pattern seen at most temples.
