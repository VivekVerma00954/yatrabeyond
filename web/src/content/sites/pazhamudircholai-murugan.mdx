overview: |
  Pazhamudircholai Murugan Temple, atop the forested Solaimalai hill on
  the outskirts of Madurai, is the sixth and final of the Arupadaiveedu
  abodes, and the only one among them where Murugan is worshipped
  alongside both of his consorts, Valli and Devayani, together.

history: |
  The site is referenced in the Sangam-era Tirumurukāṟṟuppaṭai by
  Nakkeerar and in the later Cilappatikaram by Ilango Adigal, giving it a
  documented literary history extending back well over a millennium,
  though the temple structure seen today reflects more recent
  construction. The Tamil poet-saint Arunagirinathar's devotional hymn
  collection Tiruppukal includes verses specifically glorifying this
  temple, adding a further layer of devotional-literary significance.

spiritualSignificance: |
  At Pazhamudircholai, Murugan is worshipped in a standing form with a
  single face and four hands, positioned between his two consorts:
  Valli, representing Iccha Shakti (the power of will/desire), and
  Devayani, representing Kriya Shakti (the power of action), with
  Murugan himself embodying Jnana Shakti (the power of knowledge) — a
  distinctive triadic theological framing unique among the six abodes.
  The site is also closely associated with the legend of the Tamil
  poetess Avvaiyar, who was tested by Murugan appearing to her in the
  guise of a young boy near a tree still worshipped at the site today, an
  episode illustrating the deity's role in imparting wisdom and humility.
  A natural spring at the hilltop, Noopura Ganga, said to have originated
  from Vishnu's anklet, is a site of ritual bathing for pilgrims,
  alongside a shrine to Raakkayi Amman.
