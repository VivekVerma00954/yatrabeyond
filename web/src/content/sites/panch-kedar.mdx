overview: |
  The Panch Kedar ("five Kedars") are five Shiva temples scattered across the
  high Garhwal Himalaya in Uttarakhand: **Kedarnath**, **Tungnath**,
  **Rudranath**, **Madhyamaheshwar**, and **Kalpeshwar**. Together they form
  one of the most demanding and revered pilgrimage circuits in Himalayan
  Shaivism — traditionally undertaken in that order, on a route of roughly
  170 km combining road and trek from Rishikesh.

  Each temple is traditionally understood to enshrine a different part of
  Shiva's body, in a single shared origin legend. This page introduces the
  circuit as a whole; each temple has its own full page with site-specific
  history, access, and practical information.

history: |
  All five temples share the same founding legend, recorded with minor
  variations across Puranic and folk sources. After the Kurukshetra war
  described in the **Mahabharata**, the Pandavas sought Shiva's forgiveness
  for the sin of killing their own kin. Shiva, unwilling to grant an easy
  audience, took the form of a bull (Nandi) and hid in the Garhwal
  Himalayas. Bhima recognised and seized the bull near Guptakashi, but the
  bull-formed Shiva sank into the earth and reappeared in five places, in
  five parts: the hump at **Kedarnath**, the arms at **Tungnath**, the face
  at **Rudranath**, the navel and belly at **Madhyamaheshwar**, and the
  matted hair (jata) at **Kalpeshwar**. The Pandavas built a temple at each
  site and were freed from their sin.

  The temples are traditionally attributed to the Pandavas themselves, though
  as with most Himalayan shrines of this antiquity, no firm construction date
  can be verified — "traditionally attributed" should not be read as
  "archaeologically confirmed." Kedarnath, Tungnath, and Madhyamaheshwar
  share a similar North Indian "Katyuri" temple architecture style.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Completing the Panch Kedar circuit is considered one of the most
  spiritually demanding acts available to a Shaivite pilgrim, precisely
  because of the physical difficulty involved — the arduousness of the
  journey is itself treated as a form of tapas (austerity). Tradition holds
  that after receiving Shiva's darshan across all five sites, a pilgrim
  should complete the circuit by visiting Vishnu at **Badrinath**, as final
  confirmation that Shiva's blessing has been received.

  Rudranath carries additional significance as a site for ancestor rites
  (pind daan), owing to its position on the Rudraganga (also called
  Vaitarani), a river name shared with the mythological river separating the
  worlds of the living and the dead.

scriptures: |
  The founding legend draws on the **Mahabharata** (the Pandavas' post-war
  penance narrative) and is elaborated in regional Puranic and folk
  literature associated with the **Kedara Khanda** of the **Skanda Purana**.
  As with Kedarnath's own page, specific verse-level citations for each
  temple's individual legend require expert verification before publication;
  treat the summary above as the commonly told devotional account rather
  than a scripture citation.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **The traditional order:** Kedarnath → Tungnath → Rudranath →
  Madhyamaheshwar → Kalpeshwar, though many modern pilgrims and trekkers
  visit a subset, or approach the more remote sites (Rudranath,
  Madhyamaheshwar) via connecting trek routes rather than back-tracking to
  the road each time.

  **Season:** All five open only outside the Himalayan winter. Kedarnath,
  Tungnath, Rudranath, and Madhyamaheshwar close for roughly six months
  (typically November to April/May) and their symbolic deity images are
  moved to winter shrines in the valley (Ukhimath, for Kedarnath and
  Madhyamaheshwar's tradition; Gopeshwar's Gopinath Mandir, for Rudranath).
  **Kalpeshwar alone remains open all year**, since it sits at a much lower
  altitude.

  **Difficulty varies enormously across the five** — from Kalpeshwar's easy
  2.5 km walk to Rudranath's genuinely demanding multi-day trek through
  Kedarnath Wildlife Sanctuary terrain. Do not assume the whole circuit is
  uniformly difficult, or uniformly easy; check each temple's own page.

familySuitability: |
  Not recommended as a single family itinerary. Kalpeshwar alone is
  genuinely family-friendly. Tungnath is achievable for families with
  reasonably fit older children. Rudranath and Madhyamaheshwar involve
  multi-day treks at altitude, through forest and wildlife-sanctuary
  terrain, better suited to experienced trekkers than a family pilgrimage
  group.

accommodation: |
  Accommodation is basic across the circuit outside of the larger base
  towns (Ukhimath, Gopeshwar, Chopta, Joshimath), which have a wider range
  of guesthouses and hotels. At or near the temples themselves, expect
  GMVN (Garhwal Mandal Vikas Nigam) guesthouses, forest rest houses, and
  simple dharamshalas — see each temple's own page for specifics.

localServices: |
  Base towns (Ukhimath, Gopeshwar, Chopta, Joshimath) have the fuller range
  of services — food, basic medical care, mobile connectivity, porter and
  guide booking. At the temples themselves, expect only the essentials:
  small prasad shops, basic food stalls, and limited or no mobile network.

preservation: |
  The Panch Kedar sites sit within or adjacent to the Kedarnath Wildlife
  Sanctuary (est. 1972, also home to the endangered musk deer), so
  environmental sensitivity applies across the whole circuit, not only at
  Kedarnath. Over-tourism pressure is concentrated at Kedarnath and
  Tungnath (the two most accessible); the more remote sites see far fewer
  visitors and correspondingly less strain, but also less infrastructure
  for waste management.

givingBack: |
  As with Kedarnath specifically, the most direct ways to help are: carry
  out everything you carry in, especially on the Rudranath and
  Madhyamaheshwar treks where waste infrastructure is minimal to
  nonexistent; use local guides, porters, and homestays rather than
  outside operators; and donate through the official Badrinath-Kedarnath
  Temples Committee channels rather than informal collection points.
