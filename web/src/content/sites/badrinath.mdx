overview: |
  Badrinath Temple, on the banks of the Alaknanda River in Chamoli
  district, Uttarakhand, holds a distinctive dual status among India's
  major pilgrimage circuits: it is simultaneously one of the four
  pan-Indian Char Dham established by Adi Shankaracharya (alongside
  Dwarka, Puri, and Rameshwaram) and one of the four sites in the
  Himalayan "Chota Char Dham" of Uttarakhand (alongside Kedarnath,
  Gangotri, and Yamunotri) — the only site that belongs to both circuits.
  Vishnu is worshipped here as Badrinarayan, in the combined form of the
  sage-deities Nar-Narayan, at 3,133 m, with the Nar Parbat peak in front
  of the temple and Neelkanth peak rising behind it.

history: |
  Tradition holds that Adi Shankaracharya, the 8th-century philosopher who
  organised the pan-Indian Char Dham system, discovered a black stone
  image of Badrinarayan (fashioned, in devotional tradition, from
  Shaligram stone) in the Alaknanda River and originally enshrined it in a
  cave near the Tapt Kund hot springs, later re-establishing the temple
  with the support of the Garhwal ruler King Kanak Pal of the Parmar
  dynasty. Some accounts describe Shankaracharya's activity at the site as
  also including the expulsion of Buddhist practice from the region as
  part of re-establishing it as a major Vaishnava pilgrimage centre — a
  claim that reflects devotional and sectarian framing from that period
  and should be treated with appropriate historical caution rather than
  presented as a neutral, settled account.

  As at several other major Himalayan shrines administered under the
  Char Dham system, Badrinath's head priest (Rawal) is traditionally a
  Nambudiri Brahmin from Kerala, a South Indian lineage tradition also
  attributed to Adi Shankaracharya's own South Indian origins.

spiritualSignificance: |
  In the Char Dham symbolism attributed to Adi Shankaracharya, Badrinath
  represents Satya Yuga, the first and most virtuous of the four cosmic
  ages, reflecting the site's status as the northernmost and, in this
  framing, most primordial of the four dhams. Badrinath is also one of
  the 108 Divya Desams, the most sacred Vishnu shrines in the Sri
  Vaishnava tradition, giving it particular weight within that specific
  devotional lineage in addition to its broader Char Dham status.

  Within Uttarakhand's own Panch Kedar tradition, pilgrims who complete
  the five-temple Shiva circuit are traditionally expected to conclude
  their pilgrimage with a visit to Badrinath, receiving Vishnu's darshan
  as confirmation that Shiva's own blessing across the Panch Kedar has
  been received — making Badrinath a common closing destination for
  several distinct Himalayan pilgrimage traditions at once.

scriptures: |

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The temple opens annually around late April or early
  May and closes around October or November, following an
  auspiciously-determined annual calendar announced by the temple
  committee; it is inaccessible in winter due to heavy snow. May–June and
  September–October offer the most stable weather.

  **Getting there:** Reached by road via Joshimath, itself a gateway town
  for several other Garhwal Himalaya pilgrimage and trek routes; the
  nearest railhead is Rishikesh and the nearest airport is Jolly Grant,
  Dehradun, both a considerable road distance away. Unlike Kedarnath, no
  trek is required — the road runs to the temple itself.

  **Registration:** As part of the Char Dham Yatra, biometric registration
  is mandatory for pilgrims travelling through the official Uttarakhand
  government system; carry government-issued photo ID.
