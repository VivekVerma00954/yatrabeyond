overview: |
  Aundha Nagnath Temple, in Hingoli district, Maharashtra, is one of
  three genuine claimants to the Nageshwar Jyotirlinga of Shiva, alongside
  Dwarka in Gujarat (this platform's primary listing — see that page for
  the fuller account of the dispute) and Jageshwar in Uttarakhand. Aundha
  Nagnath's claim rests on identifying the Hingoli district forest as the
  ancient "Darukavana" referenced in the Shiva Purana. The temple is a
  designated Archaeological Survey of India Protected Monument of National
  Importance and a prominent example of Hemadpanthi architecture.

history: |
  The temple's original structure is dated to the 13th century, during
  the reign of the Yadavas of Devagiri, though its mythical founding is
  attributed in popular tradition to the Pandavas, with Yudhishthira said
  to have built a temple here during the period of exile described in the
  Mahabharata. The temple suffered significant damage during Delhi
  Sultanate-era invasions in the medieval period. In the 18th century,
  Ahilyabai Holkar, ruler of the Holkar kingdom of Indore and a major
  patron of Jyotirlinga temple reconstruction across India, is widely
  credited with renovating the temple.

  The temple is a striking example of Hemadpanthi architecture, developed
  under the Yadava minister Hemadri Pandit and characterised by
  interlocking stone construction without mortar. The base structure —
  outer walls and sanctum — dates from the 13th-century Yadava period and
  features intricate carvings of deities, mythological scenes, and
  elephants; the upper portion and spire were rebuilt later, likely in the
  18th century. Distinctively, the sanctum sits in a small basement
  chamber, reached by a narrow flight of stairs, rather than at ground
  level as in most Jyotirlinga temples.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Aundha Nagnath's claim to be the Nageshwar Jyotirlinga rests specifically
  on a geographic identification of Darukavana with this Hingoli district
  forest region, distinguishing its case from the mythological Daruka/
  Supriya legend more closely associated with the Dwarka site. Its status
  as an ASI-protected National Monument gives it a strong basis of
  documented architectural and historical significance independent of the
  Jyotirlinga dispute itself.
