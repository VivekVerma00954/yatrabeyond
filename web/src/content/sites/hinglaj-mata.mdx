overview: |
  Hinglaj Mata Temple, also known as Hinglaj Devi, Hingula Devi, or Nani
  Mandir, is a remote cave shrine on the Makran coast in Lasbela
  district, Balochistan, Pakistan. It is one of the classical 51 Shakti
  Peethas, traditionally marking the spot where Sati's head
  (Brahmarandhra) fell — regarded by many devotees as among the most
  powerful and important of all the Shakti Peethas precisely because of
  this association with the head. There is no built temple structure at
  the site itself; worship centres on a small stone set on a low mud
  altar within a narrow natural gorge.

history: |
  Hinglaj's remoteness and cross-border location have shaped its history
  as much as any single ancient event. Despite the partition of India in
  1947 and Pakistan's subsequent religious and political trajectory, the
  shrine has survived continuously, and — notably — is revered by many
  local Muslims as well, who refer to it as "Nani ki Mandir" and make
  offerings of coloured cloth, incense, candles, and a sweet preparation
  called sirini, reflecting a degree of shared local devotion that
  crosses religious lines in this specific location.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The Hinglaj Yatra, held each spring and drawing as many as 300,000
  pilgrims, is described as the largest Hindu pilgrimage within Pakistan.
  Pilgrim groups traditionally depart from the Nanad Panthi Akhada in
  Karachi, led by a designated staff-bearer (chaadiar) authorised by that
  Hindu religious organisation. For the small but continuing Hindu
  community within Pakistan, Hinglaj Yatra represents a major, actively
  sustained devotional tradition, distinct from — though textually linked
  to — the wider Shakti Peetha tradition followed in India.
