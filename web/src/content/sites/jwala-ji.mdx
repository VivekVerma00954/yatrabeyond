overview: |
  Jwala Ji Temple, in the town of Jawalamukhi, Kangra district, is one of
  the classical 51 Shakti Peethas, marking the spot where Sati's tongue
  is traditionally said to have fallen. Unlike almost every other temple
  on this platform, Jwala Ji has no idol at all: the goddess is worshipped
  directly through nine natural, continuously burning flames emerging
  from a rock fissure in the sanctum, fed by underground natural gas.

history: |
  Jwala Ji is one of the more historically well-documented Shakti Peethas:
  the 14th-century Persian chronicle Tarikh-i-Firuz-Shahi, by Shams-i
  Siraj, records a visit by the Delhi Sultanate ruler Firuz Shah Tughlaq
  during a military campaign against Kangra, making this among the
  earliest datable historical references to any site on this platform's
  Shakti Peetha list. The site is also referenced in the Puranas and in
  the work of the classical Sanskrit poet Kalidasa.

  A well-known historical anecdote describes the Mughal emperor Akbar
  visiting the shrine and, sceptical of its sanctity, attempting to
  extinguish the flames using an iron disk and diverted water — without
  success. Tradition holds that Akbar subsequently offered a golden
  parasol (chattar) at the shrine, which is said to have transformed into
  an unknown, non-gold metal, a story widely repeated in devotional
  accounts of the temple though not independently verifiable.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Jwala Ji's flame-only worship, without any carved or installed image,
  gives it a distinctive theological character among the Shakti Peethas —
  the goddess is understood to be directly, continuously present and
  visible, rather than represented. Each of the nine flames is associated
  with a different form of the goddess, adding a further devotional layer
  to a single visit.
