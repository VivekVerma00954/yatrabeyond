overview: |
  Kalaram Temple, in the Panchavati area of Nashik on the banks of the
  Godavari river, is a Hemadpanthi-style black basalt temple enshrining
  black stone idols of Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana — "Kala" (black) plus
  "Ram" giving the temple its name. Panchavati itself is traditionally
  identified as part of the forest where Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana lived
  during their exile, giving the temple a direct thematic link to the
  Ramayana narrative independent of the temple structure's own more
  recent construction history.

history: |
  An earlier temple at or near the site is thought to date to the
  Rashtrakuta period (roughly 7th-11th century CE), though dedicated to
  an unidentified deity; during early Turkic incursions into the region,
  the original idol is said to have been thrown into the Godavari by
  temple Brahmins to protect it. According to temple tradition, the
  Maratha nobleman Sardar Rangarao Odhekar had a vision of a black Rama
  idol submerged in the Godavari, located it at the exact spot he had
  seen in the vision, and funded the construction of the present temple
  around the 1700s to house it.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Kalaram Temple holds a distinctive place in modern Indian history as
  the site of the 1930 Nashik Satyagraha, led by B. R. Ambedkar, a
  sustained campaign demanding temple entry rights for Dalits, who were
  at the time barred from entering. The campaign, though it did not
  achieve immediate entry, became one of the landmark actions of the
  wider anti-untouchability movement and is an integral part of how this
  temple is remembered in modern India, alongside its devotional
  significance as a Panchavati Ramayana site.

scriptures: |
  Panchavati's identification as part of the Dandaka forest where Rama,
