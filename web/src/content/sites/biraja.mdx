overview: |
  Biraja Temple, in Jajpur, Odisha, is one of the classical 51 Shakti
  Peethas — and, distinctively, is also counted among the separate
  eighteen Ashtadasha Shakti Peethas — traditionally marking the spot
  where Sati's navel fell. Considered the oldest Tantric Peetha in
  Odisha, the temple gives Jajpur its alternate names, Biraja Kshetra
  and Biraja Pitha.

history: |
  The present temple structure is dated to the 11th century, with Jajati
  Keshari of the Kesari dynasty credited with building the temple in
  Jajati Nagar (the historical name of present-day Jajpur) during the
  Kalinga era, some sources placing this construction as late as the 13th
  century. Historical references to the wider region are documented from
  the 7th–8th centuries onward, during the rule of the Bhaumakara
  dynasty, though the Puranas themselves reference the site in various
  forms well before this more securely documented historical period.

spiritualSignificance: |
  A distinctive regional legend links Biraja to a broader "Gaya" tradition
  of body-part sacred geography, separate from the Sati narrative: this
  legend holds that when Vishnu killed the demon Gayasura via a yajna
  performed on the demon's own body, the demon's feet came to rest at
  Pada Gaya (identified with Pithapuram, Andhra Pradesh), his head at
  Siro Gaya (Bihar's Gaya), and his navel here at Jajpur's Nabhi Gaya —
  giving Biraja a second, independent devotional narrative layered
  alongside its core Shakti Peetha identity. The principal deity, worshipped
  as Virāja/Birājā, is depicted in a distinctive two-armed form spearing
  the demon Mahishasura with one hand while pulling his tail with the
  other, one foot on a lion and the other on the demon's chest.

scriptures: |
  Biraja's identification with Sati's navel is documented across the
  Devi Bhagavata Purana and Shakti Peetha Stotram literature, while its
  place among the Ashtadasha Shakti Peethas draws on the separate Ashta
  Dasa Shakti Peetha Stotram tradition. The Gayasura/Nabhi Gaya legend is
  documented in regional Odisha devotional tradition. Specific
  verse-level citation requires expert verification before publication.
