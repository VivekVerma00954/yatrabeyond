overview: |
  Banke Bihari Temple, in Vrindavan, is dedicated to Banke Bihari, worshipped
  as the combined form of Radha and Krishna in a single black idol. It is
  widely regarded as Vrindavan's single most visited temple, drawing very
  large daily crowds, and is closely associated with the 16th-century
  saint-musician Swami Haridas, whose devotional practice at nearby
  Nidhivan is directly connected to the deity's own origin legend.

history: |
  Banke Bihari was originally worshipped at Nidhivan, the sacred forest
  elsewhere in Vrindavan associated with Swami Haridas's devotional
  practice, before the icon was moved to its present, purpose-built
  temple around 1864. The deity's origin legend holds that Swami Haridas,
  singing a devotional bhajan at Nidhivan at his followers' request,
  invoked the appearance of Radha and Krishna before him; asked by
  Haridas to combine into a single form — likened to the union of a dark
  cloud (Krishna) and lightning (Radha) — the couple is said to have
  granted his request, manifesting as the single dark idol worshipped in
  the temple today.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Banke Bihari's darshan follows a distinctive practice unlike most Hindu
  temples: the curtain before the deity is repeatedly opened and closed
  throughout the day, rather than left open continuously, based on a
  belief that the deity's direct, sustained gaze is so powerful that
  looking upon him continuously could be overwhelming even for devotees.
  This makes each darshan at Banke Bihari a series of brief but intensely
  anticipated moments rather than a continuous viewing, a practice unique
  among the major Braj temples.
