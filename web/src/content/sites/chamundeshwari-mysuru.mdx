overview: |
  Chamundeshwari Temple, atop Chamundi Hills roughly 13 km from Mysuru,
  Karnataka, is one of the Ashtadasha (18) Maha Shakti Peethas,
  traditionally marking the spot where the hair of Sati's head fell, in
  what was then known as Krouncha Puri. The temple gave its name to the
  Mysuru royal family's tutelary goddess, Chamundeshwari (a fierce form
  of Durga/Shakti), held in reverence by the Wodeyar Maharajas of Mysuru
  for centuries, and remains the ceremonial focal point of Mysuru
  Dasara, one of Karnataka's largest annual festivals.

history: |
  The temple is estimated to be over 1,000 years old, beginning as a
  smaller shrine next to the Mahabaleshwara Temple on the same hill,
  identified in the Skanda Purana and other ancient texts as one of
  eight hills surrounding the Trimuta Kshetra sacred landscape. The
  temple grew substantially in scale and prominence with the rise of the
  Wodeyar dynasty as rulers of Mysuru from 1399 CE onward, becoming the
  large complex seen today under sustained royal patronage across
  several centuries.

spiritualSignificance: |
  According to the temple's central legend, the demon king Mahishasura
  ruled from this hill before being slain by Goddess Durga at this exact
  site, an episode celebrated annually and elaborately during Mysuru
  Dasara, the state festival of Karnataka (Nada Habba). This gives
  Chamundeshwari a dual significance as both a Maha Shakti Peetha in the
  Sati-dismemberment tradition and the specific site of the
  Mahishasura-slaying narrative central to Navratri celebrations
  nationwide.

scriptures: |
  Chamundeshwari's identification with Sati's hair is documented in the
  Ashta Dasa Shakti Peetha Stotram attributed to Adi Shankaracharya; the
  Mahishasura-slaying legend draws on the Devi Mahatmyam and the wider
  Skanda Purana tradition associated with the hill. Specific verse-level
