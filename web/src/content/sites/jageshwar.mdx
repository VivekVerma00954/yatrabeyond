overview: |
  Jageshwar, near Almora in Uttarakhand's Kumaon region, is a cluster of
  roughly 125 ancient Hindu temples set within a deodar (Himalayan cedar)
  forest along the Jataganga river valley. It is one of three genuine
  claimants to the Nageshwar Jyotirlinga of Shiva, alongside Dwarka in
  Gujarat (this platform's primary listing — see that page for the fuller
  account of the dispute) and Aundha Nagnath in Maharashtra. Jageshwar's
  claim rests on identifying the deodar ("daru") forest here as the
  ancient "Darukavana" of the Shiva Purana. Independent of the Jyotirlinga
  dispute, the site is one of the most architecturally and historically
  significant temple clusters in the Himalaya, protected by the
  Archaeological Survey of India.

history: |
  The origins of the Jageshwar temples are not fully settled by scholars.
  The site shows evidence of different architectural styles and building
  periods spanning the 7th to 14th centuries, with some temples and stone
  steles dated to the post-Gupta era (second half of the 1st millennium)
  and others to the 2nd millennium. Some colonial-era writing attributed
  the temples to the Katyuri or Chand hill dynasties, but the ASI notes
  there is no firm textual or epigraphical evidence to confirm or refute
  this. Of the roughly 125 temples across the valley, 151 individual
  structures (across the Jageshwar and neighbouring Dandeshwar groups)
  have been numbered and protected by the ASI as pre-12th-century
  monuments.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Jageshwar's claim to be the Nageshwar Jyotirlinga rests on the deodar
  forest identification with Darukavana, distinct from the mythological
  Daruka/Supriya legend associated with Dwarka. Independent of that
  specific claim, the sheer scale and antiquity of the temple cluster —
  one of the largest concentrations of ancient stone temples anywhere in
  the Himalaya — gives Jageshwar major standing in Himalayan Shaivism in
  its own right, reinforced by an on-site ASI Archaeological Museum
  housing over 150 sculptures recovered from the valley, dated from the
  9th to 13th centuries.

scriptures: |
  The claim rests on identifying the deodar forest here with
  "Darukavana," rather than a direct citation naming Jageshwar in the
  Dwadasha Jyotirlinga Stotra. As with the other two Nageshwar claims,
  this identification has not achieved universal scholarly consensus.
