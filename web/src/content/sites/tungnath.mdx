overview: |
  Tungnath is the highest of the five Panch Kedar temples and one of the
  highest Shiva temples in the world, standing at 3,680 m, just below the
  summit of Chandrashila peak in the Rudraprayag district of Uttarakhand.
  It is traditionally held to enshrine the **arms (bahu)** of Shiva, in the
  shared Panch Kedar origin legend (see the Panch Kedar overview page for
  the full story).

  Tungnath is also, by a wide margin, the most accessible of the five —
  the trek from Chopta is only about 5 km, compared to the multi-day
  approaches required for Rudranath and Madhyamaheshwar. This accessibility
  makes it one of the more visited high-altitude Shiva temples in India,
  popular with both pilgrims and trekkers.

history: |
  Tungnath is traditionally attributed to the Pandavas, as part of the
  shared Panch Kedar founding legend: after Shiva evaded the Pandavas by
  taking the form of a bull and sinking into the earth near Guptakashi, his
  arms are said to have surfaced here. Tradition holds the temple to be
  roughly 1,000 years old, though as with most Himalayan shrines of this
  age, no firm construction date has been established through
  archaeological evidence — this figure should be read as a devotional
  estimate rather than a confirmed date.

  A distinctive feature of Tungnath's priestly lineage: unlike the other
  Panch Kedar and Char Dham shrines, where priests traditionally come from
  South India (a practice attributed to Adi Shankaracharya), Tungnath's
  priests are local Brahmins from Makkumath village. During the winter
  closure, the temple's symbolic deity image and its priests relocate to
  the Markandeshwar Temple in Makkumath, about 29 km away.

  The site also carries a Ramayana-linked legend: Chandrashila peak, just
  above the temple, is where Rama is said to have meditated after killing
  Ravana, to be freed from the sin of killing a Brahmin (Ravana was a
  Brahmin by birth). A separate tradition holds that Ravana himself once
  performed penance to Shiva here.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As the highest of the Panch Kedar, and one of the most physically
  accessible, Tungnath occupies a distinctive place in Himalayan Shaivism:
  a genuinely high-altitude, arduous pilgrimage that is nonetheless
  achievable within a single day by a reasonably fit visitor, making it a
  common entry point into Panch Kedar and high-Himalaya pilgrimage more
  broadly.

  The adjoining Chandrashila summit, associated in tradition with Rama's
  penance, adds a Vaishnava dimension to what is otherwise a firmly
  Shaivite site — visitors often treat the temple and the summit as a
  single combined pilgrimage.

scriptures: |
  As with the other Panch Kedar sites, the founding legend draws on the
  Pandavas' post-Kurukshetra penance narrative associated with the
  **Mahabharata** and elaborated in regional Puranic and folk material tied
  to the **Kedara Khanda** of the **Skanda Purana**. The Ramayana-linked
  Chandrashila legend does not appear to have a settled scriptural
  citation and should be treated as popular/regional tradition pending
  expert verification.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The temple is open roughly April/May to October/November, closing for about six months in winter due to heavy snow.
  Best conditions are April–June and September, when the trail is clear and
  weather is most stable; March brings vivid rhododendron blooms to the
  approach trail.

  **Getting there:** Chopta, the trailhead, sits on NH-58 about 24 km from
  Rudraprayag towards Karnaprayag, reached from Rishikesh via Devprayag,
  Srinagar (Uttarakhand), and Rudraprayag. Nearest airport: Jolly Grant,
  Dehradun (~258 km). Nearest railway station: Rishikesh (~241 km).

  **The trek:** 5 km from Chopta (2,926 m) to the temple (3,680 m), roughly
  4–5 hours on a stone-paved path with rest benches at intervals. From the
  temple, a further, steeper 2 km climb reaches Chandrashila peak.

familySuitability: |
  The most family-achievable of the Panch Kedar treks. The path is short,
  well-maintained, and paced with rest points, though it is still a steep
  high-altitude climb, not a casual walk. Families with older children in
  reasonable fitness generally manage it in a day. The additional climb to
  Chandrashila is steeper and better suited to fitter members of a group.

accommodation: |
  Chopta itself has a growing range of guesthouses, budget hotels, and
  camping options, popular with trekkers. GMVN (Garhwal Mandal Vikas Nigam)
  accommodation and a forest resthouse at Duggalbitta (near the trail) are
  also available. Most visitors day-trip the temple from Chopta rather than
  staying overnight at altitude.

localServices: |
  Chopta has food stalls, dhabas, and basic shops; mobile connectivity is
  limited but present in the town itself, and unreliable higher up the
  trail. A high-altitude botanical research station (Garhwal University)
  is located along the route. No ATMs at the temple or immediately
  surrounding area — carry cash from Rudraprayag or Ukhimath.

preservation: |
  Tungnath's relative accessibility compared to the other Panch Kedar sites
  means it sees significantly higher visitor volumes, placing more pressure
  on the alpine meadow and rhododendron-forest ecosystem along the Chopta
  trail than the more remote sites experience. The temple lies within the
  broader Kedarnath Wildlife Sanctuary (est. 1972) landscape, home to the
  endangered musk deer, and visitor behaviour on this popular trail has a
  proportionally larger conservation impact than at the harder-to-reach
  Panch Kedar sites.

givingBack: |
  Carry out all waste, this trail is one of the more heavily used in the
  region and waste management infrastructure is limited. Use local guides
  and Chopta-based accommodation and food services rather than outside
  operators. Support conservation efforts tied to the Kedarnath Wildlife
  Sanctuary where reputable local or government channels are identified.
