overview: |
  Jagannath Temple, in Puri, Odisha, is dedicated to Jagannath
  ("Lord of the Universe"), worshipped alongside his siblings Balabhadra
  and Subhadra as a distinctive form of Krishna/Vishnu found almost
  nowhere else in this exact configuration. It is the eastern site of the
  four pan-Indian Char Dham established by Adi Shankaracharya, alongside
  Badrinath, Dwarka, and Rameshwaram, and is world-famous for its annual
  Rath Yatra (chariot festival), among the largest and most widely
  recognised Hindu festivals held anywhere.

history: |
  Temple records attribute the original founding of a shrine at this site
  to a legendary King Indradyumna of Avanti, though the present temple
  structure is historically dated to the 12th century, built under Ananta
  Varman Chodaganga Deva of the Eastern Ganga dynasty and completed under
  his successor, Anangabhima Deva III, with construction beginning around
  1136 CE. The temple is a major exemplar of the Kalinga architectural
  style, its four structural units — Vimana, Jagamohana, Natamandira, and
  Bhogamandapa — aligned along a single axis.

  The Rath Yatra itself has been celebrated at Puri for over a thousand
  years, and the wooden images of Jagannath, Balabhadra, and Subhadra are
  themselves ritually replaced on a periodic cycle (Nabakalebara), a
  practice distinct from the continuously worshipped stone or metal
  images found at most other major Hindu temples.

spiritualSignificance: |
  In the Char Dham symbolism attributed to Adi Shankaracharya, Puri
  represents Kali Yuga, the current and final of the four cosmic ages —
  positioning Jagannath's temple as the dham most closely associated with
  the present era. The annual Rath Yatra, in which enormous newly-built
  wooden chariots (the largest, Jagannath's own, standing roughly 14 m
  tall) are pulled through Puri's Grand Road (Bada Danda) by thousands of
  devotees to the Gundicha Temple roughly 3 km away, is unique in Hindu
  practice for bringing the deities out of the sanctum and into full public
  view and physical reach, a marked departure from typical temple
  darshan conventions.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to February offers the most comfortable
  coastal weather. The Rath Yatra, usually falling in June or July during
  the month of Ashadha, is the temple's largest annual event by a wide
  margin, drawing enormous crowds — a major experience in its own right,
  but also the most crowded and logistically demanding time to visit.

  **Getting there:** Puri has its own railway station with good
  connectivity, and Bhubaneswar, roughly 60 km away, has the nearest
  airport.

  **Entry:** Non-Hindus are not permitted to enter the main temple; a
  raised platform (Raghunandan Library building, opposite the temple) is
  commonly used by non-Hindu visitors to view the temple from outside.

  a major, safe, and well-organised public event overall.
