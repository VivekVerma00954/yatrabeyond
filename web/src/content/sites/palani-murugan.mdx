overview: |
  Palani Murugan Temple, atop the Palani Hills, is the third of the six
  Arupadaiveedu abodes and one of the most visited Murugan temples in
  India. Here, Murugan is worshipped as Dhandayuthapani Swamy, depicted
  as a renunciate ascetic having given up worldly attachment, a
  distinctive devotional form among the six abodes.

history: |
  The site is referenced in Sangam-era literature, including the
  Tirumurukāṟṟuppaṭai (under the older name Thiruaavinankudi), giving
  it a documented history extending back more than 2,000 years. The
  temple structure itself is generally dated to between the 2nd and 5th
  centuries CE, built by the Cheras, with the Cholas and Pandyas
  expanding it considerably between the 8th and 13th centuries through
  additional mandapams and gopurams. Temple inscriptions record specific
  royal grants, including from Pandya kings Jatavarman Sundara Pandyan I
  and Jatavarman Vira Pandyan II in the 13th century, and a village grant
  from Maravarman Kulasekara Pandyan I in 1300 CE.

spiritualSignificance: |
  According to legend, the sage Narada presented Shiva with the
  gnana-palam (fruit of knowledge) and declared it would go to whichever
  of his two sons, Ganesha or Murugan, could first circle the world
  three times; Murugan, feeling the contest unfairly favoured his
  brother, renounced his claim and retreated to Palani Hills to live as
  a hermit, giving the site its distinctive renunciate devotional
  character. The temple's central idol is traditionally said to have
  been consecrated by the siddhar (alchemist-saint) Bogar from an
  amalgam of nine medicinal herbs known as Navapashanam, giving the
  murti a unique material history among the six Arupadaiveedu images.
  Palani is also closely associated with Panchamritam, a five-ingredient
  sweet prasadam offering that holds Geographical Indication status,
  reflecting the temple's deep cultural as well as devotional
  significance.
