overview: |
  Vishnupad Temple, on the banks of the Phalgu river in Gaya, Bihar, is
  the central shrine of one of Hinduism's most significant cities for
  ancestral rites. It enshrines a footprint held to be Vishnu's own,
  incised into a block of basalt known as the Dharmashila, and is the
  primary site at which pilgrims perform Pind Daan, offerings made for
  the salvation of one's ancestors.

history: |
  The present temple structure was commissioned around 1787 by Maharani
  Ahilyabai Holkar of Indore, part of the same wide-ranging programme of
  temple restoration she funded at several other major Hindu pilgrimage
  sites across India. Skilled artisans built the structure from large
  blocks of dark grey/black stone, reportedly sourced from quarries near
  Patharkatti, joined with iron clamps for durability. The octagonal,
  east-facing shrine rises roughly 30 metres, topped by a pyramidal
  tower reaching about 100 feet, with sloping sides in alternating
  indented and plain sections forming a series of joined peaks; eight
  rows of carved pillars support the surrounding pavilion.

spiritualSignificance: |
  According to legend, the demon Gayasura performed intense penance and
  was granted a boon that anyone who merely saw him would attain
  moksha (liberation), a power so disruptive to the moral order that
  people began attaining salvation without righteous conduct. To
  restore balance, Vishnu asked Gayasura to submit to being pinned
  beneath the earth, pressing his right foot on the demon's head (or
  chest, in some tellings) to hold him down permanently; Vishnu's
  footprint, left on the Dharmashila at this moment, is the object of
  worship at the temple today. This legend underlies Gaya's standing as
  the pre-eminent site for Pind Daan, since the site itself is
  associated with securing liberation through Vishnu's own act, and
  pilgrims from across India travel here specifically to perform these
  ancestral rites, particularly during the Pitru Paksha period.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to February offers the most comfortable
  weather; Pitru Paksha (a roughly two-week period in
  September-October) is the temple's most significant annual period,
  drawing very large numbers of pilgrims specifically to perform Pind
  Daan, though the rite may be performed here on any day of the year.

  **Getting there:** Gaya has its own airport and railway station, well
  connected to Patna and the wider region; the temple is within the
  city itself, on the Phalgu riverbank.
