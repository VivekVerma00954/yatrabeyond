overview: |
  Udupi Sri Krishna Matha, in coastal Karnataka, is one of South India's
  most important Krishna pilgrimage sites and the seat of the Dvaita
  ("dualist") school of Vedanta philosophy founded by the 13th-century
  saint-philosopher Madhvacharya. Its central image of Bala Krishna, and
  the distinctive tradition of viewing the deity through the "Kanakana
  Kindi" (Kanaka's Window), give the temple a devotional history unlike
  any other major Krishna site in India.

history: |
  The Krishna Matha was founded by Madhvacharya, who according to temple
  tradition recovered the deity's image, encased within a block of
  sacred gopichandana clay, from a merchant ship he had rescued during a
  storm, before carrying it to Udupi and consecrating the matha around
  it in the 13th century. Madhvacharya subsequently established eight
  affiliated mathas (the Ashta Mathas of Udupi) to maintain worship at
  the temple on a rotating basis, an administrative structure that
  continues to this day.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The temple's best-known feature, the Kanakana Kindi (Kanaka's Window),
  is linked to the Haridasa saint Kanakadasa, a former Vijayanagara army
  chieftain turned devotee who, according to legend and as recorded by
  the scholar Mogling, was barred from the temple as a non-Brahmin and
  worshipped from outside its walls; the temple's Krishna idol is said
  to have miraculously turned to face him, and a crack that later
  appeared in the temple wall during an earthquake was, on the guidance
  of the pontiff Vadiraja, widened into a permanent viewing window rather
  than sealed. Devotees continue to view the deity through this window
  to this day, making it a distinctive and unusually democratic feature
  of the temple's devotional practice.
