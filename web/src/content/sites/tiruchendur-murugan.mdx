overview: |
  Tiruchendur Murugan Temple, in Thoothukudi district, is the second of
  the six Arupadaiveedu abodes of Murugan (Kartikeya) and is unusual
  among Murugan temples for standing directly on the seashore facing the
  Bay of Bengal, rather than atop a hill as most of the other five
  abodes do. It commemorates the site where Murugan is believed to have
  defeated the demon Surapadman.

history: |
  References to the site appear in Tamil Sangam literature dating
  roughly from the 2nd century BCE to the 2nd century CE, and temple
  tradition holds that it was founded not by kings but by three holy
  saints, with the temple gradually remodelled and expanded over roughly
  a thousand years under the Chera and Chola rulers. In a distinctive
  episode of colonial-era history, the temple complex was occupied by
  the Dutch East India Company from 1646 to 1648 CE during their
  conflict with the Portuguese; local resistance, together with an
  intervention by Thirumalai Nayak, the Nayak ruler of the region,
  eventually forced the Dutch out, and the original idol, which had been
  removed for safekeeping, was recovered and reinstalled by Vadamalaiappa
  Pillaiyyan in 1653.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Tiruchendur is revered as the site of Murugan's defeat of the demon
  Surapadman, an event commemorated annually through Surasamharam, a
  major festival re-enactment of the battle that draws large crowds. The
  temple's nine-storeyed, 137-foot Mela-Gopuram, believed to have been
  constructed roughly 300 years ago, is among its most recognisable
  features, and the temple's overall scale makes it one of the largest
  in India by total temple area.
