overview: |
  Parli Vaijnath Temple, in Maharashtra's Beed district, is one of three
  genuine claimants to the Vaidyanath Jyotirlinga of Shiva, alongside
  Deoghar in Jharkhand (listed as this platform's primary site — see that
  page for the fuller account of the underlying dispute) and Baijnath in
  Himachal Pradesh. Parli's claim is considered strong by many, resting
  partly on being named directly in some readings of the Dwadasha
  Jyotirlinga Stotra, and the temple maintains a large, independent
  pilgrimage following that treats it as the genuine Jyotirlinga
  regardless of Deoghar's own claim.

history: |
  The temple is generally dated to construction under the Yadava dynasty
  of Devagiri around the 12th–13th century, under the guidance of
  Hemadpant, the Yadava chief minister credited with the distinctive
  "Hemadpanthi" architectural style seen at several Deccan-region temples.
  The temple fell into disrepair over subsequent centuries before being
  substantially rebuilt in the 18th century (commonly dated to 1706, and
  separately described as receiving major renovation funding) by the
  Maratha queen Ahilyabai Holkar — the same patron responsible for major
  reconstructions at Somnath, Omkareshwar/Mamleshwar, Kashi Vishwanath,
  and Aundha Nagnath, making her contribution to the physical fabric of
  the Jyotirlinga tradition unusually extensive.

  The name "Vaidyanath"/"Vaijnath" ("Lord of Physicians") is explained
  through a legend in which Shiva descended to heal the injured Ravana
  after the latter's battles, acting in the role of a divine physician. A
  further legend closely associated specifically with Parli holds that
  Ravana brought the lingam here from the heavens himself.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Parli's sanctum houses a smooth black-stone (Shaligram) Shiva Linga
  within a Hemadpanthi-Indo-Aryan hybrid architectural setting distinctive
  among the Jyotirlingas. The temple's large courtyard, brass-plated
  entrance, and continued independent devotional following, regardless of
  Deoghar's own claim, reflect its status as a fully self-sufficient
  pilgrimage destination rather than a secondary site.

scriptures: |
  Parli's claim rests on being named in some readings of the Dwadasha
  Jyotirlinga Stotra verses concerning Vaidyanath, and on the Ravana
  physician legend recorded in regional devotional tradition. As with
  Deoghar, this claim has not achieved universal scholarly consensus and
  should be presented as contested rather than settled.
