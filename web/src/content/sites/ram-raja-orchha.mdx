overview: |
  The Ram Raja Temple in Orchha, Madhya Pradesh, is widely described as
  the only temple in India where Rama is worshipped not as a deity in a
  sanctum but as a reigning king, complete with a daily police Guard of
  Honour. The temple occupies a former royal palace rather than a
  purpose-built shrine, a distinction that grew directly out of the
  legend recounted below.

history: |
  According to the temple's traditional account, Orchha's 16th-century
  king Madhukar Shah was a devotee of Krishna (in the form of Banke
  Bihari), while his queen, Ganesh Kunwari, was devoted to Rama. When
  the king challenged her to bring Rama himself from Ayodhya to Orchha
  if her devotion was genuine, she travelled to Ayodhya and, in despair
  at being unable to bring the deity back, is said to have attempted to
  end her life in the Saryu river — at which point Rama appeared to her
  and agreed to accompany her back to Orchha, on the condition that he
  would settle wherever he was first set down. On returning exhausted to
  her palace, the queen set the deity down there before completing the
  planned temple elsewhere, and Rama is said to have honoured his own
  condition by remaining in her palace permanently. That palace has been
  the Ram Raja Temple ever since.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Because Rama is understood here to reside as the reigning monarch of
  Orchha rather than as a temple deity, the shrine maintains protocols
  associated with royalty: an armed police Guard of Honour is presented
  daily, and the deity is offered a royal-style repast. The image is
  depicted holding a sword in one hand and a shield in the other, again
  consistent with a sovereign rather than a conventional temple murti.
