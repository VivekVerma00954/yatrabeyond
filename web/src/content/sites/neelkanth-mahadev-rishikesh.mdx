overview: |
  Neelkanth Mahadev Temple, roughly 32 km from Rishikesh in Pauri
  Garhwal district, Uttarakhand, marks the site traditionally
  identified as where Shiva consumed the Halahala poison produced
  during the Samudra Manthan (churning of the cosmic ocean), an act
  that turned his throat blue and gave him the name Neelkanth
  ("blue-throated"). The temple sits at the confluence of the Pankaja
  and Madhumati rivers, between the Manikoot, Brahmakoot, and
  Vishnukoot valleys.

history: |
  According to legend, after consuming the Halahala poison to save the
  Devas and Asuras during the Samudra Manthan, Shiva meditated at this
  spot for 60,000 years beneath a Panchpani tree to withstand its
  effects; the temple's Garbha Griha (inner sanctum) is said to be
  built at the exact location of that meditation. The temple's
  architecture shows a Dravidian-style influence, distinct from the
  more typically Garhwal stone-and-wood temple style found elsewhere in
  the immediate region.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Neelkanth Mahadev draws large numbers of Kanwariyas, pilgrims who
  carry Ganga water on foot from Haridwar to offer at the temple,
  particularly during the Shravan month (July-August) and around Maha
  Shivratri (February-March), when the temple holds two major annual
  fairs. Its Samudra Manthan association gives it a distinctive
  devotional identity tied to one of Shiva's most widely known acts of
  cosmic self-sacrifice.

scriptures: |
  The Samudra Manthan and Shiva's consumption of the Halahala poison are
  widely documented across Puranic literature; the specific
  identification of this exact site as the location of that event is a
  matter of regional pilgrimage tradition rather than direct scriptural
  citation, and should be presented as such.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** March to May and September to November offer the
  most comfortable weather; Maha Shivratri and Shravan Shivratri are the
  temple's two major annual fairs, with Shravan drawing especially
  large Kanwariya crowds walking from Haridwar.

  **Getting there:** The temple is reached by road, roughly 32 km from
  Rishikesh, via winding hill roads through forested terrain.
