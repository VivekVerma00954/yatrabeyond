overview: |
  **This page describes the most disputed and physically unresolved
  site among all the Shakti Peethas covered on this platform; it is
  presented here clearly flagged, with all available information, so
  the full 18 Maha Shakti Peetha list can be shown.** Shrinkhala Devi
  Shakti Peetha, traditionally located in Pandua (also called
  Praddyumnam), Hooghly district, West Bengal, is one of the Ashtadasha
  (18) Maha Shakti Peethas, traditionally marking the spot where Sati's
  stomach fell. Sources describe this as, by some accounts, the only one
  of the full Shakti Peetha network to which Hindus currently have no
  physical access at its traditional location: the original temple was
  destroyed, and an Islamic minaret has stood at the site since 1340 CE,
  now administered by the Archaeological Survey of India as a protected
  monument rather than an active temple.

history: |
  According to the sources available for this page, the original
  Shrinkhala Devi temple was destroyed during a period of medieval
  conflict, and Shah Sufiuddin built the standing minaret in 1340 CE, said
  to commemorate a military victory at the site. This platform found no
  source able to independently confirm the exact circumstances or precise
  date of the original temple's destruction, and presents this account
  with that limitation explicit.

spiritualSignificance: |
  This platform found at least four distinct, competing claims regarding
  where Shrinkhala Devi's tradition now continues in devotional practice:
  some accounts hold that her presence has moved to the Chotila Mata
  temple in Gujarat; others to Ganga Sagar, in the Adinath Kshetra
  tradition; others to Sringeri in Karnataka, connected to the sage
  Rishyasringa, though this platform notes that Sringeri's own Sharada
  Peetham, a separate and independently well-documented institution, is
  not itself considered a classical Shakti Peetha, and no corroborating
  evidence was found in the sources consulted linking it directly to
  Shrinkhala. This platform presents all of these claims transparently
  rather than endorsing any one as settled. Despite the unresolved status
  of the original temple, an annual Mela Tala festival, drawing over
  100,000 local Hindus and Muslims, is held for 30 days each Magha Masa
  (January-February) at the Pandua site itself.
