overview: |
  Sita Ramachandraswamy Temple in Bhadrachalam, on the banks of the
  Godavari river in eastern Telangana, is revered as one of the major
  Godavari-basin Divya Kshetrams and is popularly called "Dakshina
  Ayodhya" (the Ayodhya of the South). Its self-manifested idols and the
  dramatic 17th-century story of its principal builder, the devotee-
  official Bhakta Ramadasu, together give the temple one of the most
  distinctive origin narratives among India's Rama temples.

history: |
  According to temple tradition, a tribal woman named Pokala Dhammakka
  discovered self-manifested (svayambhu) idols of Rama, Sita, and
  Lakshmana inside an anthill and began worshipping them, laying the
  spiritual foundation for what became the temple. The structure itself
  was built in 1630 CE by Kancherla Gopanna, a local tax official under
  the Qutb Shahi (Golconda) administration, who became known as Bhakta
  Ramadasu. Ramadasu financed much of the reconstruction using land-
  revenue funds he had collected on behalf of the state, initially with
  local villagers' promise to repay the amount after harvest, but
  without the Sultan's own authorisation; when the funds could not be
  repaid, this led to his imprisonment by Sultan Abul Hasan Qutb Shah
  ("Tanisha"). Temple tradition holds that Rama and Lakshmana then
  appeared to the Sultan in a dream and repaid the debt on Ramadasu's
  behalf, securing his release — an episode still central to how the
  temple's founding is remembered.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Ramadasu's devotional compositions to Rama, written largely in Telugu
  in the classical pallavi-anupallavi-charanam song form, are known
  collectively as the Ramadasu Keertanalu and remain an influential body
  of work within South Indian classical (Carnatic) music, giving
  Bhadrachalam a devotional-musical legacy that extends well beyond the
  temple site itself. The temple continued to receive grants from both
  the Qutb Shahi and later Asaf Jahi (Nizam) rulers of Hyderabad, a
  pattern of cross-community royal patronage that is itself part of the
  site's historical significance, and one still referenced by the
  present-day Telangana state government's continued support for the
  temple.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to March offers the most comfortable
  weather; the temple's annual Sita Rama Kalyanam (celestial wedding
  celebration), held around Rama Navami, is its single largest festival.

  **Getting there:** Bhadrachalam is reachable by road from Hyderabad,
  Vijayawada, and Visakhapatnam; the nearest major railway stations
  require a further road journey to reach the town itself.
