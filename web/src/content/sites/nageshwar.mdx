overview: |
  Nageshwar Jyotirlinga Temple, on the road between Dwarka city and Beyt
  Dwarka island in Gujarat, is one of three sites claimed as the
  Nageshwar Jyotirlinga of Shiva, alongside Aundha Nagnath in Maharashtra
  and Jageshwar in Uttarakhand. This platform lists Dwarka as the primary
  site, reflecting its status as the location of "popular faith" and the
  one most commonly included in mainstream pilgrim circuits, reinforced
  by its proximity to Dwarka's own major Char Dham significance, while
  presenting Aundha Nagnath and Jageshwar as separately documented,
  genuinely contested alternate claims, each with its own page.

history: |
  The Shiva Purana places the Nageshwar Jyotirlinga in "Darukavana," an
  ancient forest referenced across multiple Indian epics (alongside other
  named forests such as Kamyakavana, Dvaitavana, and Dandakavana). The
  ambiguity of exactly which historical forest this refers to is the root
  of the three-way dispute: some identify it with the coastal Gujarat
  region near Dwarka, others with a forest in Maharashtra's Hingoli
  district (associated with Aundha Nagnath), and others with the Deodar
  ("daru") forest near Almora in Uttarakhand (associated with Jageshwar).

  The presiding legend at the Dwarka site describes a demon named Daruka
  terrorising the region and imprisoning devotees, including a steadfast
  Shiva devotee named Supriya, who continued chanting even in captivity.
  Shiva is said to have responded to this devotion by appearing, defeating
  Daruka, and choosing to reside permanently at the site as a Jyotirlinga.
  Archaeological excavations at the site are reported to have found
  evidence of as many as five earlier settlements, though this platform
  has not independently verified the excavation reports.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Nageshwar's location directly on the route to Beyt Dwarka, and its
  proximity to Dwarka city itself (one of the four Char Dham of India and
  a major Krishna pilgrimage centre), means it is very commonly visited as
  part of a combined Dwarka pilgrimage, reinforcing its status as the most
  visited of the three Nageshwar claims by a wide margin. A notable modern
  feature at the site is a 25-metre tall seated Shiva statue, alongside a
  garden and pond, which has become a significant visual landmark for the
  temple in its own right.

scriptures: |
  Nageshwar's identification with Dwarka rests on associating "Darukavana"
  with this coastal region, and on the Daruka/Supriya legend recorded in
  Puranic literature on the twelve Jyotirlingas. The competing Aundha
  Nagnath and Jageshwar claims rest on alternate readings of the same
  "Darukavana" reference. None of the three has achieved universal
  scholarly consensus, and this platform should not present the Dwarka
  identification as definitively settled, despite its being the most
  popularly followed.
