overview: |
  Baijnath Temple, on the banks of the Binwa river in Himachal Pradesh's
  Kangra valley, is the third of three genuine claimants to the Vaidyanath
  Jyotirlinga of Shiva, alongside Deoghar in Jharkhand (this platform's
  primary listing — see that page for the fuller account of the dispute)
  and Parli in Maharashtra. Of the three, Baijnath is generally the least
  widely recognised in popular devotional consensus, though it has strong
  independent historical documentation through its own temple
  inscriptions.

history: |
  The present temple structure was built in 1204 CE by two local
  merchants, Ahuka and Manyuka, according to inscriptions engraved on
  stone slabs within the temple's main hall — written in Sanskrit using
  the Sharada script, alongside local Pahari-language text in the Takri
  script. These inscriptions record the temple's construction under the
  reign of a king named Jaya Chandra, and name both the architects and the
  donor merchants. Notably, the inscriptions themselves suggest that a
  Shiva temple existed at the site even before this 1204 CE structure was
  built, indicating older origins for worship here.

  In 1786, Kangra ruler Sansar Chand undertook renovation and restoration
  work at the temple. It survived the major 1905 Kangra earthquake with
  comparatively minor damage, though several smaller shrines within the
  temple yard (dedicated to Jamadagni, Bhairava, Narmadeshwar, and
  Murlimanohar) and part of the main temple's roof sustained some damage
  at that time.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As with the other two Vaidyanath sites, the name "Vaidyanath" ("Lord of
  Physicians") here draws on the legend of Shiva healing Ravana. A
  Himachal-specific telling of this legend describes Ravana undertaking
  extreme penance at Kurah seeking immortality from Shiva at Kailash; when
  the Devas grew concerned at Ravana's growing power, Shiva intervened,
  halted the rites, restored Ravana's ten heads, and earned the title
  Vaidyanath in the process — with a further tradition holding that Shiva
  granted Ravana's request to be carried to Lanka in linga form,
  paralleling similar Ravana-linga legends recorded at both Parli and
  elsewhere in the wider Vaidyanath tradition.

scriptures: |
  Baijnath's claim rests on its documented antiquity (the 1204 CE
  inscriptions, and evidence of an even older temple predating them) and
  its own version of the Ravana/Vaidyanath legend, rather than on a
  citation in the Dwadasha Jyotirlinga Stotra comparable to those claimed
  for Deoghar or Parli. This should be understood as the weakest of the
  three claims in terms of textual citation, though its architectural and
  epigraphic documentation is strong in its own right.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** March–May and September–November offer the most
  comfortable weather in this Himalayan-foothill setting; winter can be
  cold, and monsoon brings heavy rain to the region.

  **Getting there:** Baijnath is in the Kangra valley, reached by road from
  Dharamshala or Pathankot; Gaggal Airport (near Dharamshala) is the
  nearest airport, and Baijnath has its own narrow-gauge railway station
  on the Kangra Valley line.
