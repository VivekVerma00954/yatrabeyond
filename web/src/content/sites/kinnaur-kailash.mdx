overview: |
  Kinnaur Kailash is a Himalayan peak in Kinnaur district, Himachal
  Pradesh, best known for a striking natural rock pillar near its summit,
  venerated as a self-manifested (swayambhu) Shivling. Reports of the
  formation's exact height vary — commonly cited figures range from around
  75 to 79 feet — and it is popularly said to change colour through the
  course of the day, taken by devotees as a reflection of Shiva's changing
  moods. The peak sits above Kalpa, a well-known Kinnaur town, and forms a
  dramatic backdrop to that region's landscape.

  **A note on altitude figures:** sources are inconsistent on both the true
  summit height and the height of the trekking route's high point. This
  page uses approximately 4,800 m as the altitude reached by pilgrims at
  the Shivling viewpoint, consistent with the roughly 15,700 ft figure
  given in trekking literature, but this should be cross-checked against
  an authoritative source, along with the peak's true summit elevation,
  before publication.

history: |
  Local Kinnauri tradition holds that Shiva and other deities gather at
  this peak, and that the natural rock formation is itself a manifestation
  of Shiva rather than a carved or installed image. As with the other Panch
  Kailash sites, no built temple marks the sacred point — the mountain and
  the rock formation are themselves the object of worship. Kinnaur more
  broadly has a distinct religious character within Himachal Pradesh,
  blending Hindu and Tibetan Buddhist practice in ways not found in most of
  the rest of the state, and this peak sits within that broader Kinnauri
  religious landscape.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The annual Kinnaur Kailash Yatra draws pilgrims from across the region
  during the July–September window, when weather conditions make the trek
  viable. As with the other Panch Kailash sites, the true summit is not
  climbed; the pilgrimage culminates in darshan of the Shivling formation
  from a fixed vantage point. The trek's difficulty is itself treated as
  part of its spiritual value, consistent with the broader Panch Kailash
  and Panch Kedar tradition of treating physical hardship as tapas.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** The yatra season runs July to September, when snow has
  cleared sufficiently for the route to be passable; outside this window the
  trek is not advisable.

  **Getting there:** Kalpa and the broader Kinnaur valley, reached via
  Shimla and Rampur along the Hindustan-Tibet Road (NH-5), serve as the
  gateway. The trek itself typically begins from villages in the vicinity
  of Kalpa and climbs through varied terrain to the high-altitude viewpoint
  for the Shivling formation, over multiple days.
