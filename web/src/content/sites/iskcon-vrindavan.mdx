overview: |
  ISKCON Vrindavan's Sri Sri Krishna Balaram Mandir, in the Raman Reti
  area of Vrindavan, was the first temple built in India by the
  International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON), founded by
  A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada. Dedicated to Krishna and his
  brother Balaram, the temple represents a comparatively recent addition
  to Vrindavan's temple landscape (opened 1975) but has become one of the
  town's most visited sites, drawing large numbers of both Indian and
  international devotees.

history: |
  Srila Prabhupada, ISKCON's founder-acharya, had long envisioned
  building a temple of exceptional beauty for Krishna and Balaram in the
  very village where the two brothers are traditionally said to have
  played more than five thousand years ago. Prabhupada, whose own guru
  Bhaktisiddhanta Sarasvati Goswami had instructed him to spread Krishna
  consciousness to the English-speaking West, travelled to the United
  States in 1965 and subsequently founded ISKCON there before returning to
  oversee construction of this temple in Vrindavan, personally directing
  its design and construction. The temple opened during the Ram Navami
  festival on 20 April 1975.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As ISKCON's first Indian temple, Sri Sri Krishna Balaram Mandir holds
  particular significance within the global ISKCON movement as a kind of
  spiritual homecoming — a Western-founded but authentically Vrindavan-
  rooted institution bringing large numbers of international devotees
  into direct contact with Braj's own devotional landscape. The temple
  complex also includes Srila Prabhupada's own samadhi (memorial shrine),
  a significant pilgrimage point for ISKCON devotees specifically.

scriptures: |
  Krishna and Balaram's shared childhood in Vrindavan is described in the
  Bhagavata Purana's tenth canto and the Harivamsa. The temple's own
  founding history is well documented through ISKCON's own institutional
  records rather than requiring the same devotional-legend verification
  needed for many of Braj's older temples.
