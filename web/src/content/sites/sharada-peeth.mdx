overview: |
  Sharada Peeth, in the Neelum Valley of Pakistan-administered Azad
  Jammu and Kashmir, is a ruined temple and, historically, one of the
  most important centres of learning in the ancient Indian subcontinent.
  It is one of the Maha Shakta Pithas, associated with the goddess
  Sati's fallen right hand, and is dedicated to Saraswati in her form as
  Sharada — a name that became so closely associated with Kashmir's own
  scholarly and religious identity that "Sharada script" became the name
  of the historical writing system used across the region for centuries.

history: |
  Between roughly the 6th and 12th centuries CE, Sharada Peeth was among
  the most prominent temple-universities in the Indian subcontinent,
  drawing scholars not only from Kashmir but from Central Asia,
  northwest India, and reportedly from as far as Tibet, Greece, and
  Mesopotamia. Its library and its role as a centre for the study of the
  Vedas, philosophy, grammar, logic, mathematics, astrology, and
  astronomy placed it, in scholarly reputation, alongside the great
  ancient universities of Takshashila and Nalanda. Sharada Peeth is
  considered the earliest known shrine dedicated to Shaktism within
  Kashmir specifically, with later, better-known Kashmiri Shakta shrines
  — including Kheer Bhawani and, indirectly, the broader devotional
  tradition that includes Vaishno Devi — following in its wake.
