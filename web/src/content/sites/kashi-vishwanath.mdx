overview: |
  Kashi Vishwanath — the temple of the Lord of the Universe — is one of
  the holiest shrines in all of Hinduism and the most sacred site in
  the ancient city of Varanasi (also known as Kashi, Banaras, and
  Benares), on the banks of the Ganga in Uttar Pradesh.

  It is the prime among the twelve Jyotirlingas — the self-arising
  manifestations of Shiva — and holds a singular place in the Shaivite
  tradition as the site where Shiva himself is said to eternally reside.
  Varanasi is considered by many Hindus to be the oldest continuously
  inhabited city in the world, and Kashi has been a centre of religious
  learning, pilgrimage, and culture for at least three thousand years.

  The present temple, with its iconic golden spires, was built in the
  18th century and recently surrounded by the landmark Kashi Vishwanath
  Corridor, inaugurated in 2021, which connects the temple directly to
  the Ganga ghats and transformed the visitor experience.

history: |
  The original temples on this site are believed to date back to ancient
  times — with references to Kashi as a sacred city appearing in the
  Rigveda, the Atharvaveda, and numerous Puranas. The site has a turbulent
  recorded history of destruction and reconstruction.

  The most significant destruction occurred in 1669 CE under the Mughal
  emperor Aurangzeb, who demolished the then-standing temple and
  constructed the Gyanvapi Mosque partially over its ruins — a site that
  remains adjacent to the current temple and is a subject of ongoing
  legal disputes in Indian courts at the time of writing.

  The current Kashi Vishwanath temple was rebuilt in 1780 by Ahilyabai
  Holkar, the Maratha queen of Indore, renowned across India as a
  builder of temples and a patron of dharmic institutions. The golden
  spires and dome — now an iconic feature of the Varanasi skyline —
  were added later with gold donated by Maharaja Ranjit Singh of the
  Sikh kingdom in the 19th century.

  The 2019–2021 Kashi Vishwanath Corridor project was the most
  significant redevelopment since the 18th-century reconstruction,
  clearing a large area between the temple and the Ganga ghats and
  creating a grand pilgrimage promenade.

spiritualSignificance: |
  In the Hindu cosmological tradition, Kashi holds a unique status as
  the city that is said to rest on the trident of Shiva — a city that
  exists outside the ordinary cycle of creation and destruction.
  Those who die in Kashi are traditionally believed to receive from
  Shiva the gift of Taraka Mantra (the liberating mantra whispered in
  the ear at the moment of death), ensuring moksha (liberation from the
  cycle of rebirth) regardless of their karma.

  This belief in Kashi as the city of liberation is elaborated in the
  **Kashi Khanda** of the Skanda Purana and has shaped the city's
  unique role as a place where Hindus from across India and the diaspora
  seek to die. The burning ghats — particularly Manikarnika — are
  therefore sacred not as places of grief but of liberation.

  For Shaivites, darshan of the Kashi Vishwanath Jyotirlinga is among
  the highest acts of devotion. The tradition holds that pilgrimage to
  all other tirthas (sacred fords) equals only a fraction of the merit
  gained by a single darshan at Kashi.

scriptures: |
  Varanasi and the Kashi Vishwanath temple are among the most extensively
  described sacred sites in Sanskrit literature.

  The **Kashi Khanda** (section on Kashi) of the **Skanda Purana** is the
  primary scriptural source for the mythology, sacred geography, and
  ritual significance of Varanasi. It describes the city's origin,
  its sacred tirthas, and the promise of liberation for those who die
  here. **Specific verse citations require expert verification.**

  The **Linga Purana** and **Shiva Purana** include chapters describing
  the twelve Jyotirlingas, with Kashi (Vishveshvara) typically listed
  among the most pre-eminent.

  The city also features in the Mahabharata (Anushasana Parva and other
  sections) and numerous Upanishads and early Vedic texts as a site of
  learning and liberation.

  Varanasi has been the home of many significant philosophical and
  poetic traditions: Kabir (15th century), Tulsidas (16th century, who
  composed the Ramcharitmanas here), and many others.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit**

  Kashi Vishwanath is open year-round. The most popular pilgrimage
  times are:
  - **Maha Shivaratri** (February/March) — the most important festival;
    massive crowds and all-night vigils.
  - **Shravan (July–August)** — the holy month of Shiva; Kanwariyas
    (devotees carrying Ganga water for abhishek) arrive in enormous numbers.
  - **Dev Deepawali (Kartik Purnima, November)** — the ghats are lit
    with lakhs of lamps in a spectacular event.
  - **Winter (October–March)** — most comfortable climate for exploring
    the city; avoid the monsoon for sightseeing.

  **Getting there**

  Varanasi is exceptionally well connected:
  - **By air:** Lal Bahadur Shastri International Airport, Varanasi
    (BHU/VNS), approximately 25 km from the city centre; direct flights
    from most major Indian cities.
  - **By rail:** Varanasi Junction (BSB) is a major railway hub with
    connections from Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, and across India.
  - **By road:** National highways connect Varanasi to Lucknow,
    Allahabad (Prayagraj), and Patna.

  **Temple access**

  The Kashi Vishwanath Corridor allows direct pedestrian access from
  Dashashwamedh Ghat to the temple. Entry is managed through security
  checkpoints; expect queues during peak hours and festival times.
  Separate queues operate for general darshan and special/VIP darshan.
  Non-Hindus may or may not be permitted to enter the inner sanctum —
  policies vary and should be verified before visiting.

  **Temple timings (approximate — verify with the official temple trust)**

  - Mangala Aarti: 3:00 am – 4:00 am
  - Darshan opens: 4:00 am
  - Bhog (midday): 11:00 am – 12:00 pm
  - Sandhya Aarti: 7:00 pm – 8:15 pm
  - Shringar / Shayan Aarti: 10:30 pm – 11:00 pm

familySuitability: |
  Kashi Vishwanath and Varanasi as a whole are very family-friendly
  destinations. The new Corridor has significantly improved the experience
  for families, elderly pilgrims, and those with mobility challenges.

  **Key considerations:**
  - The narrow galis (lanes) of the old city are chaotic and interesting
    for older children but can be overwhelming for young children and toddlers.
  - Manikarnika and Harishchandra cremation ghats are operational 24 hours
    a day — families with young children should exercise discretion when
    visiting and explain the significance respectfully in advance.
  - The Ganga aarti at Dashashwamedh Ghat is spectacular and highly
    recommended for families — arrive early for a good vantage point.
  - Boat rides on the Ganga at dawn are one of the most memorable
    experiences in Varanasi and are suitable for all ages.
  - Accommodation ranges from budget guesthouses to heritage hotels; the
    better options are within walking distance of the ghats.

accommodation: |
  Varanasi offers accommodation across all budget levels, from
  guesthouses directly on the ghats to five-star heritage hotels.

  - **Ghat-facing guesthouses:** Numerous family-run guesthouses line the
    riverfront, particularly around Assi Ghat and Dashashwamedh Ghat —
    ideal for experiencing the dawn rituals and Ganga aarti on foot.
  - **Heritage hotels:** Several haveli-style heritage properties in the
    old city and near the ghats offer a premium, atmospheric stay.
  - **Larger hotels:** More modern hotels are available in the Cantonment
    area and near the airport, away from the old city.
  - **Ashrams and dharamshalas:** Several provide pilgrimage-oriented
    accommodation at modest cost; standards vary widely.

  Booking in advance is essential during Maha Shivaratri, Shravan, and
  Dev Deepawali.

    (the main lane leading to the temple) has many options. The city is
    predominantly vegetarian near the temple precincts.
  - **Medical:** Good private hospitals and clinics are available. Carry

  - **ATMs and banking:** Widely available throughout the city.
  - **Mobile connectivity:** Major Indian networks all operate well.
  - **Rickshaws and e-rickshaws:** The primary transport within the
    old city; agree the fare in advance.

preservation: |
  Varanasi faces profound preservation challenges as a living, densely
  inhabited ancient city:

  - **The Ganga:** Water quality and the ecological health of the river
    remains a critical concern despite significant investment under the
    Namami Gange programme. Ghats and riverfront environments continue
    to be affected by urban pollution, sewage discharge, and industrial
    effluents from upstream.
  - **Heritage buildings:** The galis (lanes) and ancient havelis of
    Varanasi contain irreplaceable architectural heritage, much of it
    in a fragile state due to unregulated development, neglect, and
    population pressure.
  - **The Kashi Vishwanath Corridor:** The 2021 development was
    controversial among heritage conservationists, as it involved the
    demolition of hundreds of buildings. Proponents argue it vastly
    improved pilgrimage access and safety; critics raised concerns
    about the loss of the historic urban fabric.
  - **Over-tourism:** Mass tourism — both domestic and international —
    places significant strain on the ancient infrastructure.

  The Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) maintains several protected
  monuments in Varanasi and Sarnath; the National Mission for Clean Ganga
  (NMCG) coordinates river restoration efforts.

givingBack: |
  Pilgrims and visitors can support Varanasi in several meaningful ways:

  - **Kashi Vishwanath Temple Trust:** The official trust accepts donations
    for the temple's maintenance, charitable activities, and support for
    local priests and workers.
  - **Namami Gange / Ganga cleaning:** Support the Ganga restoration effort
    by following strictly plastic-free practices on and near the ghats.
  - **Heritage organisations:** Several organisations work on documenting
    and restoring Varanasi's architectural and cultural heritage — research
    reputable bodies before contributing.
  - **Local artisans:** Varanasi is famous for its Banarasi silk weaving,
    brasswork, and other crafts. Purchasing directly from artisans' workshops
    rather than large commercial outlets supports traditional livelihoods.
  - **Local guides:** Engage licensed local guides rather than large
    tour operators to keep tourism revenue within the community.
