overview: |
  Baidyanath Temple in Deoghar, Jharkhand, is one of three genuine
  claimants to the Vaidyanath (or Baidyanath/Vaijnath) Jyotirlinga of
  Shiva — the most contested of the twelve, with no official government
  notification identifying which of the three (this site, Parli in
  Maharashtra, or Baijnath in Himachal Pradesh) is the canonical location.
  This platform lists Deoghar as the primary site, based on its
  identification with "Chidabhoomi" in some scriptural readings and its
  reported endorsement by the Shankaracharyas of the Dwarka and Puri
  peethas, while presenting Parli and Baijnath as separately documented,
  genuinely contested alternate claims, each with its own page. Deoghar is
  also separately revered as one of the 51 Shakti Peethas, said to mark
  where Sati's heart fell — giving it the additional name "Hriday Peeth"
  (Heart Shrine).

history: |
  The site's origins are described as lost in antiquity, referred to in
  Sanskrit texts by older names including Haritakivan or Ketakivan; the
  name "Deoghar" is thought to be comparatively recent, likely dating from
  the construction of the main Baidyanath temple itself. The earliest
  documented reference identifiable as a Kanwar Yatra — pilgrims carrying
  Ganges water from Sultanganj to pour at the Vaidyanath shrine — traces
  to the 1700s, according to research cited from the University of
  Allahabad.

  The dispute over which of the three Vaidyanath sites is the canonical
  Jyotirlinga has never been resolved by any single central authority.
  Some sources report that the Shankaracharyas of the Dwarka and Puri
  peethas have endorsed Deoghar as the original site, though this
  endorsement is itself not universally accepted, and Parli in particular
  retains a large, independent devotional following that treats its own
  site as the genuine Jyotirlinga.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Deoghar's Shravani Mela, held during the Hindu month of Shravan
  (roughly July–August), is one of the largest annual pilgrimages in
  eastern India: an estimated 7 to 8 million devotees walk the
  approximately 108 km route from Sultanganj (on the Ganges) to Deoghar
  each year, carrying kanwars (decorated pots of Ganges water) barefoot,
  in a line of saffron-clad pilgrims that reportedly stretches the full
  distance — described as the longest annual pilgrimage gathering in
  Asia. The symbolic act of carrying water echoes the broader Shiva
  legend of carrying Sati's remains, reinforcing Deoghar's dual identity
  as both Jyotirlinga and Shakti Peetha.

scriptures: |
  Deoghar's claim rests partly on identifying the site with "Chidabhoomi"
  ("cremation ground," referenced in some readings of the Dwadasha
  Jyotirlinga Stotra's description of Vaidyanath's location in the
  north-eastern part of the country) and partly on reported Shankaracharya
  endorsement. Parli's claim rests on being named directly in some
  versions of the same stotra's verses. Neither claim has achieved
  universal scholarly consensus, and this platform should not present
  either as definitively settled.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to February offers the most comfortable
  weather outside the main pilgrimage season. The Shravani Mela
  (July–August) is the temple's single largest event, drawing millions of
  pilgrims — an extraordinary experience, but also the most crowded and
  logistically demanding time to visit by a wide margin.

  **Getting there:** Deoghar has its own airport and railway station with
  reasonable connectivity, and is also reachable by road from Ranchi,
  Patna, and Kolkata.
