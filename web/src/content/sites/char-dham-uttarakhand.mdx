overview: |
  The Char Dham of Uttarakhand — often called the "Chota Char Dham"
  ("small four abodes") to distinguish it from the separate, pan-Indian
  Char Dham of India — is a circuit of four Himalayan pilgrimage sites in
  Uttarakhand's Garhwal region: **Yamunotri**, **Gangotri**,
  **Kedarnath**, and **Badrinath**, traditionally visited in that order.
  The circuit combines devotion to two sacred rivers (Yamuna at
  Yamunotri, Ganga at Gangotri) with devotion to Shiva (at Kedarnath) and
  Vishnu (at Badrinath), making it distinctly cross-traditional compared
  with the pan-Indian Char Dham of India, which is exclusively
  Vaishnava apart from Rameshwaram.

  **Note on shared sites:** Badrinath belongs to both this circuit and
  the pan-Indian Char Dham of India; Kedarnath is also one of the twelve
  Jyotirlingas and the first stop of the separate Panch Kedar circuit.
  This page introduces the Uttarakhand circuit as a whole; each site has
  its own full page with site-specific history, access, and practical
  information.

history: |
  Unlike the pan-Indian Char Dham, which is attributed as a single
  unified system to Adi Shankaracharya, the Chota Char Dham of
  Uttarakhand is better understood as a regional Himalayan pilgrimage
  tradition that developed around four independently significant sacred
  sites, later organised into a common circuit and travel sequence for
  pilgrims, rather than as a single founding act at one point in time.
  Each site's own history — Yamunotri's 1839 temple, Gangotri's
  18th-century Gorkha-era construction, Kedarnath's Pandava/Shankaracharya
  associations, and Badrinath's own Shankaracharya-linked founding — is
  detailed on its own page.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Completing the full Chota Char Dham circuit, traditionally undertaken in
  the sequence Yamunotri → Gangotri → Kedarnath → Badrinath, is considered
  a highly significant devotional undertaking, combining river-goddess
  worship, Shaivite pilgrimage to one of the twelve Jyotirlingas, and
  Vaishnava pilgrimage to a Divya Desam within a single circuit. The
  physical demands of the circuit — particularly the Kedarnath trek — are,
  as with the Panch Kedar tradition, understood as a form of tapas
  (austerity) integral to the pilgrimage's spiritual value.

scriptures: |
  Each site's own scriptural associations are addressed on its individual

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **The traditional order:** Yamunotri → Gangotri → Kedarnath → Badrinath,
  broadly following a west-to-east geographic sequence across the Garhwal
  Himalaya, though many modern pilgrims visit a subset or reorder the
  sequence based on road conditions and personal itinerary.

  **Season:** All four open only outside the Himalayan winter, typically
  late April/May to October/November, following an annual calendar
  announced separately for each site; none is accessible in winter due to
  heavy snow.

  **Registration:** Biometric registration is mandatory for the Char Dham
  Yatra of Uttarakhand as a whole, via the official Uttarakhand government
  portal or authorised registration centres; carry government-issued
  photo ID throughout.

  **Difficulty varies enormously across the four** — from Badrinath and
  Gangotri's motorable road access to Yamunotri's short trek and
  Kedarnath's genuinely demanding multi-day-effort climb. Do not assume
  the whole circuit is uniformly difficult; check each site's own page.

preservation: |
  The full circuit crosses genuinely fragile high-altitude Himalayan
  terrain, with Kedarnath in particular carrying well-documented
  environmental and disaster-risk considerations (see its own page for
  detail on the 2013 floods). Over-tourism during the compressed
  May–October season is a shared challenge across all four sites, managed
  in part through the mandatory biometric registration system.
