overview: |
  Sree Padmanabhaswamy Temple in Thiruvananthapuram, the capital of
  Kerala, is one of the wealthiest religious sites in the world following
  the 2011 discovery of vast treasure held in its sealed vaults. The
  temple is dedicated to Vishnu in his Padmanabha ("lotus-navel") form,
  depicted reclining on the serpent Adishesha with Brahma seated on a
  lotus emerging from his navel, flanked by his consorts Lakshmi and
  Bhudevi.

history: |
  The earliest recorded reference to the temple dates to around the 8th
  or 9th century CE, though the site is believed to be considerably
  older still. The temple was substantially reconstructed in the 18th
  century by Martanda Varma, king of Travancore, who in 1750 formally
  dedicated his entire kingdom to the deity, ruling thereafter as the
  deity's steward (Padmanabha Dasa) — a distinctive constitutional
  arrangement that shaped Travancore's, and later Kerala's, royal
  identity for the following two centuries.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The temple's main image is said to be constructed from 12,008
  shaligrams (fossilised ammonites considered sacred, aniconic
  representations of Vishnu), brought from the Gandaki River in Nepal, a
  detail that gives the deity's very physical form a trans-regional
  devotional history. The 2011 Supreme Court-ordered inventory of the
  temple's vaults revealed gold, gems, and artefacts estimated by various
  reports at tens of billions of dollars in value, making global
  headlines and reframing the temple, alongside its long-standing
  religious significance, as one of the most materially significant
  religious sites on earth. One vault (designated "Vault B") has, per
  most reporting, remained unopened by court order.
