overview: |
  Puj Chaliha Sahib Jhulelal Mandir, in Ulhasnagar 5 (also called
  Sindhunagar), near Kalyan, Maharashtra, is recorded as the first
  Jhulelal temple built in India following the 1947 Partition, when the
  Sindhi Hindu community was displaced from Sindh (in present-day
  Pakistan) and its members carried elements of their devotional
  tradition, including a sacred flame from their original Sindh temple,
  into a newly established diaspora community.

history: |
  Following Partition, Sindhi Hindus who settled in the Kalyan Camp area
  built this temple to sustain their community's devotional and cultural
  identity in a new home. Its Akhand Jyot (perpetual flame) is recorded
  as having been carried during the 1947 migration from the original Puj
  Chalihasahib Mandir at Peergoath in Sindh, where the flame itself is
  said to have first been lit around 1897 CE; it has reportedly burned
  without interruption since, an achievement recognised in the India
  Book of Records after 75 years of continuous burning at the Ulhasnagar
  site.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Jhulelal is venerated by the Sindhi community as a deity of water,
  traditionally associated with the river Indus and identified by some
  devotees with Varuna or with an incarnation of Vishnu; he is regarded
  as a unifying patron figure for Sindhi Hindus both in India and across
  the global diaspora following Partition. Cheti Chand, marking
  Jhulelal's traditional birth anniversary, is the community's most
  significant annual festival, and the temple's continuously burning
  Akhand Jyot is a particularly potent symbol of cultural and devotional
  continuity carried across the 1947 displacement.
