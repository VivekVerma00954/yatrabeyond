overview: |
  Radha Rani Temple, popularly known as Ladli Ji Temple, stands atop
  Bhanugarh hill in Barsana, traditionally revered as Radha's own home
  village. Radha and Krishna are worshipped together here as Ladli Lal
  ("the beloved daughter and son"), with Radha herself lovingly addressed
  as Ladli Ji ("the most cherished one") — one of relatively few major
  Braj temples where Radha, rather than Krishna, is the presiding deity.

history: |
  Temple tradition attributes an original shrine at this site to King
  Vajranabh, traditionally Krishna's great-grandson, some 5,000 years ago
  in devotional reckoning, though the site is said to have fallen into
  ruin over subsequent centuries, with its original sacred images lost.
  The site's more recent, better-documented restoration is credited to
  the 16th-century saint Narayan Bhatt, a key figure in reviving and
  systematising the sacred geography of the wider Braj Mandal region, and
  a further temple was built at the site in 1675 by Raja Bir Singh Deo.
  The present palace-like red sandstone structure, richly carved with
  arches, domes, and painted interiors, reflects this later, better-
  documented phase of construction.

spiritualSignificance: |
  As the presiding temple of Radha's own traditional home village,
  Barsana's Radha Rani Temple occupies a distinctive place within Braj's
  broader devotional geography, which otherwise centres more heavily on
  Krishna. The temple is the focal point of two of Braj's most
  distinctive annual celebrations: Radhashtami (Radha's birth
  anniversary) and, especially, Lathmar Holi, in which women from Barsana
  traditionally strike men from neighbouring Nandgaon (Krishna's own
  traditional home village) with sticks (lathis) in a good-natured,
  ritualised re-enactment of Radha and her companions' playful, teasing
  response to Krishna's own Holi mischief.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to March offers the most comfortable
  weather; Radhashtami and Lathmar Holi (celebrated a few days before the
  main Holi festival) are the temple's most distinctive and heavily
  attended annual events.

  **Getting there:** Barsana is roughly 45 km from Mathura and Vrindavan,
  reached by road; most pilgrims visit as a day trip from one of those two
  towns rather than staying overnight in Barsana itself.

  **The climb:** The temple sits atop Bhanugarh hill, reached by a
  stepped climb from the town below — a moderate ascent, considerably
  easier than any Himalayan trek on this platform, but still requiring
  reasonable mobility.
