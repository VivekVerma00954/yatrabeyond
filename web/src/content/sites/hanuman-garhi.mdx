overview: |
  Hanuman Garhi, in the Ramkot area of Ayodhya, is one of the city's most
  ancient and important temples, dedicated to Hanuman. Built in the shape
  of a four-sided fort with circular ramparts at each corner, the temple
  is traditionally visited by pilgrims before the Ram Mandir, as part of
  the standard sequence of an Ayodhya pilgrimage. Within the fort, 76
  stairs lead to the main sanctum, where a six-inch image of Hanuman in
  his youthful (Bal) form is depicted seated on the lap of his mother,
  Anjani.

history: |
  Devotional tradition, drawing on the Skanda Purana, attributes an
  original temple at this site to King Vikramaditya, alongside 360 other
  temples said to have been built across Ayodhya during the same era — a
  tradition, like similar Vikramaditya-era attributions at Kanak Bhawan
  and Treta Ke Thakur, that should be understood as devotional and
  regional historical tradition rather than independently verified
  archaeological fact. Some accounts describe the temple being destroyed
  and a mosque built in its place under the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb; the
  temple's precise history through the following centuries is disputed
  and unevenly documented across different sources. The temple as it
  broadly appears today is dated to 1799 CE, built under the supervision
  of Diwan Tikait Rai during the rule of Asaf-ud-Daulah, the Nawab of
  Awadh.

  Mythologically, the site is associated with the tradition that Hanuman
  took up residence in a cave here to protect Ramkot (the fortified area
  understood to be Rama's own citadel) after returning to Ayodhya
  following the defeat of Ravana — establishing Hanuman's enduring role
  as the guardian of the city.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Hanuman Garhi's status as Ayodhya's traditional "first stop" for
  pilgrims, visited before the Ram Mandir itself, reflects Hanuman's role
  as protector and gatekeeper of the city in devotional practice — seeking
  his blessing and permission, in effect, before proceeding to Rama's own
  birthplace. This sequencing is a well-established local pilgrimage
  custom rather than a formal religious requirement.
