overview: |
  Himachal Pradesh's five most widely visited Devi temples — **Jwala
  Ji** (Kangra), **Chintpurni** (Una), **Brajeshwari/Vajreshwari Devi**
  (Kangra), **Naina Devi** (Bilaspur), and **Chamunda Devi** (Kangra) —
  are commonly grouped together in regional pilgrimage tradition and
  tourism material as the state's primary Shakti circuit. **Four of
  these five are counted among the classical 51 Shakti Peethas** (Jwala
  Ji, Chintpurni, Brajeshwari, and Naina Devi); **Chamunda Devi is not**
  part of that classical list, despite being a major, widely revered
  Siddha Peetha in its own right. This platform presents that distinction
  clearly rather than treating all five as equally "Shakti Peethas" in
  the strict, textual sense.

history: |
  Each of the five temples has its own independent founding history,
  detailed on its own page — spanning claimed antiquity to the Vedic era
  (Naina Devi), Mughal-era historical documentation (Jwala Ji, mentioned
  by name in a 14th-century chronicle), Pandava-era legend layered with a
  well-documented 1905-earthquake reconstruction (Brajeshwari), a
  founding attributed to a Brahmin priest roughly twelve generations ago
  (Chintpurni), and an 18th-century relocation of an older hilltop shrine
  to a more accessible site (Chamunda Devi). Despite this varied history,
  all five are grouped together in contemporary Himachal pilgrimage
  tourism as a common regional circuit.

spiritualSignificance: |
  Each temple in this group centres on a distinct aspect or manifestation
  of Devi: Jwala Ji as pure elemental fire, Chintpurni as the fierce
  Chinnamastika (the "headless" Mahavidya), Brajeshwari/Vajreshwari as
  the thunderbolt-wielding form who defeated the demon Kalikala, Naina
  Devi as the eyes of the goddess and slayer of Mahishasura, and Chamunda
  as the combined destroyer of the demons Chanda and Munda. Completing
  all five is a popular regional pilgrimage undertaking, though it
  carries less formalised devotional structure than, say, the Char Dham
  or Panch Kedar circuits.

scriptures: |
  The four classical Shakti Peethas in this group (Jwala Ji, Chintpurni,
  Brajeshwari, Naina Devi) draw on the Devi Bhagavata Purana and related
  Shakti Peetha Stotram literature describing Sati's dismemberment.
  Chamunda Devi's mythology, centred on the slaying of Chanda and Munda,
  draws instead on the Devi Mahatmya (Chandi Path) section of the
  Markandeya Purana. Specific verse-level citation for each individual
  site requires expert verification, addressed on each temple's own page.

pilgrimageInfo: |
  **When to visit:** October to March offers the most comfortable
  weather across all five, all located in the Himachal foothills rather
  than high Himalaya, so none carries the seasonal closure of this
  platform's higher-altitude sites.

  **Getting there:** All five are reached by road within Himachal
  Pradesh's Kangra, Una, and Bilaspur districts; Dharamshala (with its own
  airport, Gaggal) and Pathankot (railhead) serve as the main regional
  gateways.

  **Combining the circuit:** Many pilgrims visit several or all five in a
  single multi-day trip given their relative proximity within the same
  broad region, though this is a matter of practical convenience rather
  than a fixed traditional sequence.
