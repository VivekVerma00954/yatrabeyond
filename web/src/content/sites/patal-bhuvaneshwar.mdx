overview: |
  Patal Bhuvaneshwar is a limestone cave temple roughly 14 km from
  Gangolihat in Pithoragarh district, Uttarakhand, extending
  approximately 160 m in length and around 90 feet below the entrance
  point. The cave's natural stalactite and stalagmite formations are
  read in local tradition as representing various deities, and the site
  is popularly described as the meeting point of the three worlds
  (Swarg Lok/heaven, Prithvi Lok/earth, and Patal Lok/underworld),
  giving rise to the name by which the platform owner referred to it.

history: |
  Local tradition credits Raja Ritupurna, a king of the Surya dynasty
  ruling Ayodhya (traditionally placed in the Treta Yuga), as the first
  to discover the cave, in a narrative involving King Nala hiding here
  after a dispute with his wife, Damayanti. The cave's modern pilgrimage
  history is widely said to begin with a visit by Adi Shankaracharya in
  1191 CE, though this date should be treated with caution given that
  Adi Shankaracharya is more commonly dated to several centuries
  earlier; this discrepancy requires expert verification before
  publication. A further tradition holds that the Pandavas visited and
  meditated in the cave during their exile, before their final journey
  into the Himalaya.

spiritualSignificance: |
  The cave is held in local tradition to enshrine Shiva alongside 33
  koti (kinds/categories) of demigods, with individual rock formations
  popularly identified with specific deities and mythological episodes
  as visitors move deeper into the cave. Its symbolic identity as the
  meeting point of the three worlds gives it a distinctive place in the
  wider Kumaon pilgrimage landscape, separate from purely Shaiva
  temple architecture found elsewhere in the region.
