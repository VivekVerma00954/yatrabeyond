quickIdentity: >
  Ayyappa is worshipped as the son of Shiva and Vishnu (in his female form
  Mohini), a deity who bridges Shaivism and Vaishnavism, honoured chiefly
  at the Sabarimala hill shrine in Kerala, one of the largest annual
  pilgrimages in the world.

namesAndEpithets: >
  He is also called **Hariharaputra**, "son of Hari (Vishnu) and Hara
  (Shiva)," and is regarded as a form of the deity Shasta; as a child he
  was named **Manikandan** for the bell (mani) found around his neck.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is typically depicted as an ascetic, seated in meditation with a
  yogic band around his knees, dressed simply and adorned with rudraksha,
  an iconography that reflects the strict vow of celibacy and austerity
  observed by his pilgrims rather than a warrior or overtly royal image.

keyStories: >
  Born to defeat the demoness Mahishi, who could only be killed by a child
  born of both Vishnu and Shiva, he was found as an infant with a bell
  around his neck and raised by King Rajasekhara of the Pandalam kingdom, a
  devotee of Shiva. He later fought and killed Mahishi at the bank of the
  Azhutha river, and this decisive victory is central to his identity as a
  warrior-ascetic protector.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of Shiva and Vishnu in his Mohini avatar; raised by the human king
  Rajasekhara of Pandalam, whose kingdom remains closely tied to the
  Sabarimala pilgrimage tradition.

significance: >
  His pilgrimage season, centred on the forty-one day Vratham observed
  before the ascent to Sabarimala, requires strict celibacy, vegetarianism
  and austerity from pilgrims (who are themselves addressed as "Ayyappan"
  for the duration), and draws tens of millions of devotees annually,
  making it among the largest pilgrimages anywhere in the world.

regionalVariation: >
  His principal shrine at Sabarimala is in Kerala, but devotion to him,
  and observance of the pilgrimage vow, extends across South India and
  among the Malayali diaspora worldwide.
