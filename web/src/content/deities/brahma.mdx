quickIdentity: >
  Brahma is the creator god of the Hindu Trimurti, alongside Vishnu the
  preserver and Shiva the destroyer. Yet despite his exalted role he is
  strikingly little worshipped today: almost no temples are dedicated to him,
  a paradox that is itself part of his story.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Svayambhu** ("self-born"), **Pitamaha** ("grandfather" of all beings),
  **Chaturmukha** ("four-faced") and **Prajapati** ("lord of creatures") name
  him as the origin of creation. He should not be confused with **Brahman**,
  the impersonal absolute of the Upanishads, a distinct concept despite the
  similar name.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is shown with four faces turned to the four directions, reciting the four
  Vedas, and with four arms holding a water-pot (kamandalu), a rosary (mala),
  a sacred text, and often a sacrificial ladle or a lotus. He is typically
  bearded and seated on a lotus, accompanied by his mount the swan (hamsa).
  The lotus recalls his birth: he is said to emerge from a lotus growing from
  Vishnu's navel.

keyStories: >
  Puranic tradition explains his lack of worship through a curse. In one
  widely told account, Brahma spoke a falsehood in a contest with Vishnu over
  who could find the ends of Shiva's infinite pillar of light, and Shiva
  cursed him to receive almost no worship. In another, tied to Pushkar, his
  consort Savitri, angered that he began a great fire-sacrifice with another
  goddess in her place when she arrived late, cursed him to be worshipped
  nowhere but Pushkar. Either way, the tradition uses a curse to account for a
  genuine historical fact: organised Brahma worship faded early.

familyAndRelations: >
  His consort is Saraswati, goddess of knowledge, in most accounts (also named
  as Savitri or Gayatri, treated as forms of one goddess of the Vedas). He is
  called the grandfather of gods and beings, and many sages and Prajapatis are
  said to descend from him.

significance: >
  Doctrinally he completes the Trimurti as the principle of creation, and he
  appears constantly in mythology as the deity who grants boons, often,
  tellingly, to demons, setting cosmic crises in motion. In lived devotion,
  however, he is honoured far more in cosmology and story than in temple
  worship.

regionalVariation: >
  The Jagatpita Brahma Temple at Pushkar in Rajasthan, its present structure
  dating to around the 14th century, is by far the most important, and almost
  the only prominent, temple dedicated to him; Pushkar's annual fair and its
  sacred lake draw large pilgrim crowds. A handful of other Brahma shrines
  exist across India and Southeast Asia (notably the much-visited Erawan
  shrine in Bangkok), but nothing rivals Pushkar's association with him.
