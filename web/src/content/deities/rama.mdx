quickIdentity: >
  Rama is the seventh avatar of Vishnu and the hero-king of the Ramayana:
  the model, across the epic, of ideal conduct as a son, husband, brother,
  and ruler. Devotion to him as fully divine, rather than simply an
  exemplary king, developed over centuries and reached its widest popular
  reach through vernacular retellings written well over a thousand years
  after the epic's earliest layers.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Maryada Purushottam**, "the best among honourable men," names him as
  the one who never crosses an ethical line, whatever it costs him.
  **Raghava** and **Raghunath** point to his lineage, the House of Raghu.
  **Ramachandra**, "moon-faced," speaks to his beauty and calm. **Ram Lalla**
  is used for his child form, and **Dashrathi** simply marks him as
  Dasharatha's son.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He's shown holding the *Kodanda*, his bow, with an arrow: strength held
  in readiness for justice rather than aggression, sometimes with his hand
  raised in a boon-granting gesture. His complexion is often blue or dark,
  linking him visually to Vishnu, and he wears royal, reddish dress. The
  standard grouping, "Rama Darbar," places his brother Lakshmana at his left
  and his wife Sita at his right, with Hanuman kneeling at his feet: the
  image says as much about his relationships as it does about him alone.

keyStories: >
  Denied his rightful throne by a stepmother's old boon, Rama accepts
  fourteen years of exile without protest, taking his wife Sita and brother
  Lakshmana with him, the story's first and clearest demonstration of duty
  overriding self-interest. During that exile, the demon-king Ravana abducts
  Sita to his island kingdom of Lanka; Rama allies with the monkey-king
  Sugriva and his general Hanuman, who locates Sita, and the war that
  follows ends with Ravana's defeat and Sita's rescue. On her return, Sita
  undergoes a trial by fire (Agni Pariksha) to publicly prove her fidelity;
  she's unharmed and the fire god himself affirms her purity, though this
  episode has become a real flashpoint for later criticism, discussed more
  on Sita's own page. Rama's eventual reign, Ram Rajya, is held up as the
  model of just kingship. It's worth knowing that the epic's earliest
  material treats him in a more human register; his standing as fully
  divine, and organized worship built specifically around him, emerged
  later, roughly the 11th to 15th centuries.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of King Dasharatha of Ayodhya and Queen Kausalya, with half-brothers
  Bharata, Lakshmana, and Shatrughna. His wife is Sita. After later public
  doubt over her fidelity, a pregnant Sita is sent away and gives birth to
  twin sons, Lava and Kusha, at the sage Valmiki's forest hermitage: the
  same twins to whom Valmiki first recites the completed Ramayana.

significance: >
  Rama Navami, his birth festival, celebrates the triumph of good over evil.
  Chanting his name (Ram Naam) is treated as a path to liberation in its own
  right. Much of his popular devotional reach today comes through Tulsidas's
  16th-century Ramcharitmanas, a vernacular retelling that recast him as an
  approachable, supreme deity for ordinary devotees rather than a distant
  epic king.

regionalVariation: >
  North India centres large Ram Navami processions and recitation of the
  Ramcharitmanas; South India instead favours the Tamil Kamba Ramayana and
  its own temple traditions. Outside India, the story changes more sharply
  still: Thailand's Ramakien gives Hanuman a far larger role and treats the
  Ravana figure with more sympathy; Malaysia's Hikayat Seri Rama sometimes
  portrays Ravana as the more just of the two; Myanmar's Buddhist Yama
  Zatdaw resets the whole story inside a Theravada Buddhist cosmology. These
  aren't minor local colour: they're genuinely different tellings of who
  the central characters are.
