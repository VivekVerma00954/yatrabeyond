quickIdentity: >
  The Navadurga, "nine Durgas," are nine forms of the goddess Durga
  worshipped especially during Navaratri and Durga Puja. Followers of
  Shaktism and Shaivism often regard them collectively as a single deity in
  nine stages; each also has its own individual profile on this platform.

namesAndEpithets: >
  In worship order across Navratri's nine nights: **Shailaputri**
  ("daughter of the mountain"), **Brahmacharini** ("she who practises
  penance"), **Chandraghanta** ("adorned with the moon as her bell"),
  **Kushmanda** ("the little warm egg," creator of the universe),
  **Skandamata** ("mother of Skanda"), **Katyayani** ("daughter of sage
  Katyayana," slayer of Mahishasura), **Kalaratri** ("goddess of the dark
  night"), **Mahagauri** ("the great fair one"), and **Siddhidatri** ("giver
  of perfection").

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Each form carries her own mount (bull, none, tiger, lion, donkey, ox and
  lotus recur across the nine) and her own combination of weapons drawn from
  a shared pool, trident, discus, sword, bow and arrow, mace, shield, conch
  and bell among them, and each has her own mantra opening "Om Devi
  [name]yai Namah." Taken together, the nine forms are read as stages of the
  same goddess's unfolding, from an ascetic, virgin figure toward her fullest
  and fiercest expressions of Shakti.

keyStories: >
  Hindu mythology holds the nine forms as the nine stages of Durga across
  the nine-day war with the demon-king Mahishasura, the war whose victory is
  celebrated on the tenth day as Vijayadashami. During Navratri, nine
  unmarried girls up to the age of nine (Kanya Puja) are worshipped and fed
  as living incarnations of the nine goddesses. In Bengal's Durga Puja, the
  nabapatrika ("nine leaves") ritual ties together the branches of eight
  plants with a banana plant to represent the nine forms as a single bound
  unit. Separately, the Agni Purana records an alternative list of nine
  names it also calls "Navadurgas" (Rudrachanda, Prachanda, Chandogra,
  Chandanayika, Chanda, Chandavati, Chandarupa, Atichandika, Ugrachanda), a
  distinct textual tradition from the nine described here rather than a
  contradiction of it.

familyAndRelations: >
  Each form carries her own genealogy, detailed in its own profile, but
  collectively they are understood as successive unfoldings of a single
  goddess, closely identified with Parvati and, through her, consort to
  Shiva across most of the nine.

significance: >
  Worshipped above all at Navaratri and Durga Puja. Navratri opens with
  Ghatasthapana, establishing a sacred pot as Shakti's seat for the nine
  nights, and each subsequent night is dedicated to the next of the nine
  forms in order, closing with Navami and, on the tenth day, Vijayadashami.

regionalVariation: >
  West Bengal and eastern India mark the season through Durga Puja's public
  clay-image worship and the nabapatrika ritual, while the north and west
  keep the same nine nights as Navratri, with fasting, garba and dandiya in
  Gujarat and Ramlila culminating in Dussehra elsewhere. The same nine-form
  structure is thus expressed in markedly different regional registers.
