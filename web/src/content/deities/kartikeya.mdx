quickIdentity: >
  Kartikeya, worshipped in South India above all as Murugan, is the god of
  war and victory, son of Shiva and Parvati and younger brother of
  Ganesha, revered as the tutelary deity of the Tamil people and commander
  of the army of the devas.

namesAndEpithets: >
  He is called **Skanda**, **Subrahmanya**, **Shanmukha**, "six-faced," and,
  above all in Tamil devotion, **Murugan**; scholarship holds that the
  originally distinct Tamil deity Murugan and the Sanskritic Skanda merged
  into a single figure following the Sangam era.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is shown as an ever-youthful warrior with six heads, allowing him to
  see in every direction, riding or accompanied by the peacock Paravani,
  sometimes with a rooster emblem on his banner; earlier iconography
  associated him more closely with an elephant mount and with Agni, the
  fire god, before the six-headed, peacock-riding form became standard
  from around the sixth century CE.

keyStories: >
  Born to destroy the demon Tarakasura, whom only a son of Shiva could
  kill, he was raised by the six Krittika stars, giving him his six faces
  and the name Kartikeya, "son of the Krittikas." As commander of the
  gods' army he defeats Tarakasura and, in South Indian tradition, the
  demon Surapadman at Tiruchendur, before receiving the sacred spear
  (vel) that becomes his principal weapon and symbol.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of Shiva and Parvati, younger brother of Ganesha; his consorts are
  named as Devasena and, in the specifically Tamil tradition, Valli.

significance: >
  As the presiding deity of the Kurinji hill country in Tamil tradition and
  the "God of the Tamils," he is central to Kaumaram, a distinct devotional
  stream within Shaivism, with festivals such as Thaipusam and Skanda
  Shashti among his most significant observances.

regionalVariation: >
  His worship as Murugan is overwhelmingly concentrated in Tamil Nadu and
  the wider Tamil diaspora, centred on his six principal abodes (Arupadai
  Veedu), while his worship as Skanda or Kartikeya in North India is
  comparatively limited, a striking regional contrast for a major pan-Indian
  deity.
