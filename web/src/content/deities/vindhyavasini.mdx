quickIdentity: >
  Vindhyavasini, "she who dwells in the Vindhyas," is a form of the Great
  Goddess worshipped at Vindhyachal on the banks of the Ganga in Mirzapur
  district, Uttar Pradesh, one of the Shakti Peethas of India.

namesAndEpithets: >
  She is also called **Kajari** or **Kajali Devi**, from her kohl-dark
  complexion, and is identified in Puranic and Vaishnava tradition with
  **Yogamaya**, the goddess who took the place of the infant Krishna at his
  birth.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is worshipped in a form typical of the wider Devi tradition, dark-hued
  and richly adorned; the Vindhyachal temple complex venerates her together
  with two other nearby goddess shrines, Ashtabhuja and Kalikhoh, forming a
  triangular circuit followed by pilgrims.

keyStories: >
  In the Krishna tradition, when Kansa moved to kill the newborn Krishna's
  place at birth, the goddess Yogamaya took the infant girl's place instead,
  warned Kansa of his coming death, and vanished into the Vindhya hills,
  where she has resided ever since, giving her the name Vindhyavasini. The
  Markandeya Purana separately credits an incarnation of the Goddess at this
  site with the slaying of the buffalo-demon Mahishasura.

familyAndRelations: >
  Understood as a direct form of the Great Goddess (Devi) and, through the
  Krishna-birth legend, closely tied to the Vaishnava story of Krishna and
  Kansa despite her own worship being centred in Shaktism.

significance: >
  As one of the Shakti Peethas, sites marking where parts of Sati's body
  are believed to have fallen, Vindhyachal is a major pilgrimage destination
  for Shakta devotees, with the Kajari festival marking her jayanti drawing
  large crowds.

regionalVariation: >
  Her worship is centred on Vindhyachal in Uttar Pradesh, but reverence for
  her as a form of the Goddess residing in the Vindhya range extends more
  broadly across North and Central India along the range itself.
