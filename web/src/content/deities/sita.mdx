quickIdentity: >
  Sita is Rama's wife and the central female figure of the Ramayana,
  considered by many traditions to be an incarnation of the goddess Lakshmi.
  Her story is bound up with the earth itself, right from her birth to her
  final return to it.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Sita** itself means "furrow": the line cut into a ploughed field, tied
  directly to how she was found. **Janaki** marks her as the daughter of
  King Janaka; **Maithili** and **Vaidehi** both point to her origin in the
  kingdom of Mithila/Videha. **Bhumija**, "earth-born," restates her origin
  story as a name in its own right.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Her visual identity is largely relational rather than built from
  independent attributes: fair or golden complexion, modest royal dress,
  often holding a lotus, shown seated or standing at Rama's right in the
  Rama Darbar grouping alongside Lakshmana and Hanuman. She isn't given
  weapons or a mount of her own, she's recognised by her place beside Rama.

keyStories: >
  King Janaka is said to have found her as an infant while ploughing a
  field, hence her name, and raised her as his daughter; she wins her
  swayamvara (a contest to choose a husband) when Rama alone can string, and
  ultimately breaks, Shiva's great bow. She joins Rama and Lakshmana in
  fourteen years of exile, during which the demon-king Ravana abducts her to
  Lanka; she's held in the Ashoka Vatika grove and refuses his advances
  throughout. After Rama's war with Ravana secures her release, she
  undergoes a trial by fire (Agni Pariksha) to publicly prove her fidelity:
  she emerges unharmed, but the episode has become a genuine point of later
  criticism, discussed below. Still later, public doubt about her resurfaces
  and a pregnant Sita is sent away; she gives birth to twin sons at the sage
  Valmiki's forest hermitage. Rather than face a second public trial, she
  calls on Mother Earth to take her back, and the ground opens and receives
  her, a close of the story that returns her, quite literally, to where she
  began.

familyAndRelations: >
  Raised by King Janaka of Mithila as his daughter (her origin itself is
  treated as mythic rather than an ordinary birth). Wife of Rama; mother of
  twin sons, Lava and Kusha.

significance: >
  Sita is counted among the *panchakanya*, five women held up as ideals in
  Hindu tradition, venerated for devotion, resilience, and sacrifice: the
  traditional reading casts her as the model wife. That reading is actively
  contested today: modern and feminist retellings push back on a purely
  submissive framing, recasting her instead as resilient and questioning,
  both readings are genuinely current in devotional and literary discourse,
  and worth holding side by side rather than picking one as final.

regionalVariation: >
  Rama has drawn more large-scale, institutionalised worship over the
  centuries than Sita on her own, much of it through Tulsidas's
  Ramcharitmanas, though she is worshipped jointly with him as Sita-Ram,
  and independently at some temples. Her birthplace is itself a live
  regional question rather than a settled fact: Sitamarhi in Bihar (at
  Punaura Dham) and Janakpur in Nepal both hold, with strong local and
  institutional backing, that they are the site where Janaka found her.
  Neutral reference sources simply note both claims without settling
  between them, and both places sit within the historical kingdom of
  Mithila, which straddled the modern India-Nepal border, so the dispute is
  about exactly which spot within greater Mithila it happened at, not a
  difference in the underlying story. This page treats both traditions as
  legitimate rather than declaring one correct; her birthplace temple at
  Sitamarhi is linked below as a related sacred site.
