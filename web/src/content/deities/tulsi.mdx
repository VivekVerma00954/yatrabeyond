quickIdentity: >
  Tulsi, or Tulasi, the holy basil plant, is worshipped as a goddess in her
  own right, regarded as an earthly manifestation of Lakshmi and,
  consequently, the constant devotional companion of Vishnu; a Tulsi plant
  is kept and venerated in the courtyard of many Hindu homes.

namesAndEpithets: >
  In her own mythic narrative she is known as **Vrinda**, wife of the asura
  Jalandhara (Shankhachuda in some tellings), before her transformation
  into the sacred plant that bears her more common name, Tulsi.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She has no anthropomorphic temple image comparable to major goddesses;
  she is worshipped directly as the living plant itself, typically grown in
  a special pedestal (tulsi vrindavan) in home courtyards, with a leaf of
  the plant considered an essential, non-negotiable element of Vishnu
  worship.

keyStories: >
  As Vrinda, her unbroken chastity was the source of her husband
  Jalandhara's invincible strength; Vishnu, to end his tyranny, took
  Jalandhara's own form through illusion (Yogamaya) and thereby caused
  Vrinda unknowingly to break her fidelity, at which point Jalandhara lost
  his power and was slain. Discovering the deception, Vrinda cursed Vishnu
  to become stone (an episode connected to his worship in shaligrama form)
  before ending her own life; from her, tradition holds, the Tulsi plant
  arose, and Vishnu vowed never again to accept worship without her leaves.

familyAndRelations: >
  Regarded as an avatar or manifestation of Lakshmi, and so eternally
  linked to Vishnu; the annual Tulsi Vivah ceremony formally re-enacts her
  marriage to him, performed using a shaligrama or amla branch to represent
  Vishnu.

significance: >
  Tulsi leaves are considered essential in Vishnu and Krishna worship
  across nearly all Vaishnava traditions, and Tulsi Vivah, held on the
  eleventh or twelfth day of the bright half of Kartik, marks the
  traditional start of the Hindu wedding season.

regionalVariation: >
  Her cultivation and worship as a household goddess are near-universal
  across Hindu India regardless of region, though the precise form of the
  Tulsi Vivah ritual and the version of her origin story told vary between
  communities and traditions.
