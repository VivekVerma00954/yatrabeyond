quickIdentity: >
  Shiva is one of Hinduism's most widely worshipped deities: ascetic, yogi,
  and, to his devotees, the supreme reality itself. He holds the "destroyer"
  role in the Trimurti alongside Brahma (creator) and Vishnu (preserver), but
  that framing undersells him: destruction here means transformation, clearing
  the way for what comes next, not an ending in itself. He's worshipped both
  in human form and as the aniconic lingam, which is in fact his most common
  temple image.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Mahadeva / Maheshvara** ("Great God/Lord") is the name used wherever he's
  worshipped as supreme. **Rudra**, an older Vedic name meaning something
  like "the Howler," points to his fiercer, untamed origins. **Pashupati**
  ("Lord of Animals") casts him as protector of creatures and herders, a role
  with roots older than classical Hinduism itself. **Nataraja** ("Lord of
  Dancers") names his cosmic dance form. **Nilakantha** ("blue-throated")
  recalls the poison he once swallowed to save the universe. **Trilochana**
  ("three-eyed") and **Bhava/Sharva/Ugra/Ishana** are further Vedic-era names
  emphasizing his sovereign, terrible aspect. Tradition counts over a
  thousand epithets in all.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Look for matted, piled hair (*jata*) with the river Ganga flowing from it
  and a crescent moon tucked in. The story goes that only Shiva's hair could
  break Ganga's fall to earth without destroying it. A third eye sits on his
  forehead, standing for insight so total it can burn away what it looks
  upon. His throat is often shown blue, from holding cosmic poison there
  rather than swallowing or spitting it out. A serpent coils around his neck;
  he carries a trident (dominion over creation, preservation and destruction)
  and a small drum (the beat that starts creation). Ash-smeared skin, a
  tiger-skin seat, sometimes a skull garland: all ascetic, cremation-ground
  imagery, signalling mastery over death rather than fear of it. His mount is
  Nandi, the bull. And then there's the lingam: an aniconic pillar form,
  often paired with the yoni, that's actually the most common Shiva image in
  temples, especially central to Shaiva Siddhanta practice. A composite form,
  Ardhanarishvara, half Shiva and half Parvati, represents the two as one
  inseparable whole.

keyStories: >
  When Shiva's first wife Sati immolated herself after her father Daksha
  publicly insulted him, a grief-maddened Shiva carried her body across the
  universe. The disorder this caused is said to have scattered pieces of her
  form across the land, marking the sites now called Shakti Peethas. During
  the churning of the cosmic ocean (Samudra Manthan), a deadly poison
  surfaced that threatened all creation; Shiva drank it and held it in his
  throat rather than letting it spread, turning his throat blue and casting
  him as protector of last resort. When Kamadeva, god of desire, tried to
  break his meditation with an arrow of longing, Shiva opened his third eye
  and burned him to ash, the story usually told to explain his
  desire-transcending, ascetic nature. And his Tandava, the cosmic dance
  performed in a ring of fire, is said to encode creation, preservation,
  destruction, concealment and grace all at once: less a myth than a piece
  of theology in motion.

familyAndRelations: >
  Sati was his first wife; reborn as Parvati, she became his consort in most
  later tradition. Their sons are Ganesha and Kartikeya (also called Skanda
  or Murugan), both born through unconventional means rather than ordinary
  birth in most versions of the story. Nandi and Shiva's *ganas* (attendants)
  round out what's often depicted as his household.

significance: >
  Devotees turn to Shiva for renunciation, transformation, and release from
  ego and obstacles, he's the ascetic ideal (yoga, tapas) as much as a
  householder god. He's associated with time and death (as Mahakala), with
  healing and protection (as Pashupati), and with liberation (moksha), which
  sets him apart from Vishnu's role of preserving worldly order. Maha
  Shivaratri, his major festival, centres on an all-night vigil and fasting
  rather than celebration in the ordinary sense.

regionalVariation: >
  This is a deity genuinely understood in different, not-fully-compatible
  ways depending on where you look. Shaiva Siddhanta, strong in South India
  and Sri Lanka, holds Shiva as supreme but distinct from the individual
  soul: liberation comes through his grace, not through becoming him.
  Kashmir Shaivism argues close to the opposite: that recognizing your own
  identity *as* Shiva is the entire point. Both traditions consider
  themselves the authentic reading of Shaivism. The tidy "Trimurti" picture,
  Shiva as one-third of a trinity with Brahma and Vishnu, is mostly a later,
  synthesizing device rather than how Shaivas themselves rank him; to them
  he isn't one of three, he's simply supreme. And underneath the Sanskritic,
  Puranic Shiva there's a long-documented layer of tribal and folk worship:
  Shiva-Rudra merged with local mountain, forest, and hunter deities across
  many Indian communities, that likely predates the classical mythology
  rather than being a simplified copy of it.
