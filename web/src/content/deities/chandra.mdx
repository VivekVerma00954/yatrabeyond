quickIdentity: >
  Chandra, also called Soma, is the Hindu god of the Moon, one of the
  Navagraha and ruler of Monday, associated with the mind, emotions and the
  nurturing, tranquil qualities of the night.

namesAndEpithets: >
  His many epithets include **Soma**, tying him to the Vedic ritual drink and
  its deity; **Indu**, "bright drop"; **Nishakara**, "the night-maker"; and
  **Oshadhipati**, "lord of herbs," reflecting the Moon's associations with
  growth and healing plants.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is depicted as fair-skinned, two-armed, holding a club and a lotus, and
  riding a chariot drawn by ten (or, in some accounts, three) white horses.
  A crescent moon at his forehead marks his identity wherever he appears in
  wider Hindu iconography, most famously worn by Shiva.

keyStories: >
  He is said to have emerged, alongside the physician-god Dhanvantari and the
  elixir Amrita, from the churning of the primordial ocean of milk. The best
  known myth of the lunar phases turns on his marriage to the twenty-seven
  daughters of Daksha: favouring Rohini above her sisters, he drew Daksha's
  curse to waste away, a curse later softened so that he wanes for a
  fortnight and waxes again rather than disappearing for good.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of the sage Atri and Anasuya. He married the twenty-seven daughters of
  Daksha, personified as the lunar nakshatras, though tradition holds he
  favoured Rohini above the rest, provoking her sisters' complaint to their
  father and the curse that shapes his waxing and waning.

significance: >
  As Soma he is inseparable from the Vedic sacrificial drink of the same
  name, one of the oldest and most important elements of Vedic ritual.
  Astrologically Chandra governs the mind and emotions, and the lunar
  calendar he embodies structures the timing of festivals and rites across
  Hindu practice.

regionalVariation: >
  Chandra worship is folded into pan-Indian ritual through the lunar
  calendar rather than sustained through dedicated regional temple
  traditions comparable to Surya's or Shani's; his most visible living
  presence is in the fortnightly and monthly rites, such as Karva Chauth
  and Sankashti Chaturthi, that are timed by his phases.
