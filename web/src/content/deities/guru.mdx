quickIdentity: >
  "Guru" here names not a single deity but the guru principle itself, the
  teacher venerated in Hindu tradition as embodying, and mediating, the
  divine for the disciple, honoured above all on the festival of Guru
  Purnima.

namesAndEpithets: >
  The principle is best known through its own defining shloka, "Gurur
  Brahma Gurur Vishnu Gurur Devo Maheshwara, Gurur Sakshaat Param Brahma,"
  "the guru is Brahma, the guru is Vishnu, the guru is Shiva, the guru is
  truly the Supreme Absolute itself."

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  The principle has no single fixed image; it is instead represented
  through the person of one's own living or departed teacher, and
  symbolically through the worship of Vyasa, traditional compiler of the
  Vedas and the Mahabharata, honoured on Guru Purnima as the archetypal
  guru.

keyStories: >
  The verse equating the guru with Brahma, Vishnu and Shiva reads the
  teacher's role through the three great divine functions: as Brahma
  creates, the guru creates understanding in the disciple; as Vishnu
  preserves, the guru protects and sustains the disciple's good qualities;
  as Shiva destroys, the guru destroys the disciple's ignorance and vice.
  Guru Purnima itself is traditionally dated to the day Vyasa was born, or
  to the day the Buddha delivered his first sermon at Sarnath, depending on
  the tradition observing it.

familyAndRelations: >
  As a principle rather than a person, it has no genealogy of its own; it
  is instantiated afresh in every guru-disciple (guru-shishya) relationship
  across Hindu, Jain, Buddhist and Sikh tradition alike.

significance: >
  Celebrated on the full moon of the month of Ashadha (June-July), Guru
  Purnima is the occasion on which disciples formally honour their own
  teachers, spiritual or academic, reflecting the broader Hindu conviction
  that the guru's role is considered equal or even superior to that of the
  gods themselves.

regionalVariation: >
  Observance spans nearly all of India and several other Dharmic
  traditions, with the specific figure honoured, one's own personal guru,
  Vyasa, or the Buddha, varying by community and lineage even as the same
  day and underlying principle is shared.
