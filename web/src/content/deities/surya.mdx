quickIdentity: >
  Surya is the Hindu solar deity, one of the principal gods of the Vedas and,
  as one of the Navagraha, the ruler of Sunday. He is worshipped both as the
  visible sun itself and as the source of light, life and royal authority.

namesAndEpithets: >
  He is called **Aditya**, "son of Aditi," and **Bhaskara**, "maker of
  light." In his Vedic aspect as **Savitr**, the one who impels all motion,
  he is counted among the twelve Adityas alongside Mitra, Varuna and Indra.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is shown standing on a blooming lotus or riding a chariot drawn by
  seven horses, or by a single horse with seven heads, representing the
  colours of light. Unusually among major gods he is typically two-armed,
  holding lotuses, crowned, and haloed, a form that art historians trace in
  part to Iranian and Central Asian influence on his cult.

keyStories: >
  He fathers major figures across the epics: Yama, god of death; Manu,
  progenitor of humanity; Karna in the Mahabharata, born through a boon to
  Kunti; and, in some traditions, Sugriva in the Ramayana. Persistent
  motifs around his household concern his wife Sanjna's shadow-double Chhaya
  and his charioteer Aruna, who together account for the sun's complicated
  family and for the birth of his son Shani.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of the sage Kashyapa and the goddess Aditi. His wife is Sanjna
  (Saranyu); Shani (Saturn) is his son by her shadow-form Chhaya, and the
  Ashvins and Revanta are also named among his children in various texts.

significance: >
  Though dedicated Surya temples declined sharply after the twelfth century,
  his worship continues vigorously through practices such as Surya Namaskar,
  Ratha Saptami and Chhath Puja. Astrologically the Sun retains central
  importance as the graha governing vitality, authority and the soul
  (atma-karaka).

regionalVariation: >
  His most celebrated historical temples stand at Konark in Odisha and
  Modhera in Gujarat. Chhath Puja, his most widespread living festival, is
  concentrated in Bihar, Jharkhand and the Terai region of Nepal, distinct in
  character from the temple-centred worship of earlier centuries.
