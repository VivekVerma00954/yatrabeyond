quickIdentity: >
  Khatu Shyam is worshipped at Khatu in Sikar district, Rajasthan, as the
  divine form taken by Barbarika, a Mahabharata-era warrior granted by
  Krishna the boon of being venerated in the present age (Kali Yuga) under
  the name Shyam.

namesAndEpithets: >
  As Barbarika he is grandson of Bhima (son of Bhima's son Ghatotkacha and
  the princess Maurvi); worshipped he is addressed as **Khatu Shyam Ji**
  or **Baba Shyam**, a title of familiar devotion rather than a name from
  classical scripture.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is typically depicted as a youthful warrior, sometimes shown with only
  his head, in reference to the central legend that follows; devotional
  imagery frequently emphasises his weapons and archery skill as a mark of
  the extraordinary valour attributed to him.

keyStories: >
  Tradition holds that Barbarika, an unmatched archer, vowed before the
  Kurukshetra war to fight for whichever side was losing, a pledge that
  threatened to make the outcome unpredictable; Krishna, testing his
  devotion, asked for his head as an offering, which Barbarika gave
  willingly. In reward for this ultimate sacrifice, Krishna granted the
  severed head the boon of witnessing the entire war and blessed him to be
  worshipped in the coming age as Shyam.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of Ghatotkacha (himself the son of Bhima and Hidimba) and the
  princess Maurvi, placing him within the wider Pandava lineage as
  Bhima's grandson.

significance: >
  His temple, said to have first been built in 1027 CE by the local ruler
  Roop Singh Chauhan, draws millions of devotees annually, especially
  during the Phalguna Mela, when large crowds undertake the Nishaan Yatra,
  carrying sacred triangular flags on foot from Reengus to Khatu.

regionalVariation: >
  His cult is concentrated in Rajasthan but draws pilgrims from across
  North India, and Khatu Shyam devotional songs and bhajans are now widely
  circulated well beyond the immediate region of his temple.
