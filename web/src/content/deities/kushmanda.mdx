quickIdentity: >
  Kushmanda is the fourth of the Navadurga, venerated as creator of the
  universe and best known for the story of restoring the sun's light and
  radiance to creation.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name is usually read as "little warm egg," from *ku* (little), *ushma*
  (warmth, energy) and *anda* (egg), a reference to the cosmic egg she is
  said to have brought forth.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown with eight to ten arms, holding a trident, discus, sword,
  hook, mace, bow, arrow, and two jars, one of amrita (nectar of immortality)
  and one of blood, with one hand in abhayamudra. Her mount is a tiger.

keyStories: >
  After the demon Jatukasura's defeat, two asura brothers, Mali and Sumali,
  undertook penance so intense it radiated visibly across the earth, drawing
  the curiosity of Surya, the sun god, whose closeness reduced them to ash.
  Shiva, angered at this, struck Surya unconscious with his trishula,
  plunging the universe into darkness; the sage Kashyapa, grieving for his
  son, cursed Shiva that he would one day strike down his own son in turn.
  To restore cosmic order, Parvati took Surya's place, creating a sphere of
  fire and light and emerging from it in this radiant form as Kushmanda.
  Kashyapa and his wife Aditi begged her to revive Surya; she gave Kashyapa
  two pots, one filled with blood through his own ascetic power and one with
  amrita, instructing him to administer the mixture to Surya, reviving him,
  and in gratitude blessed Kashyapa with a son destined to be greatest among
  the gods. Sukesha and his wife Devavati, parents of the slain Mali and
  Sumali, brought their sons' ashes to Parvati, who placed an egg in
  Devavati's womb from which the asura sons were reborn. Surya then asked
  Kushmanda to reside within the sun, so that she might continue to give it
  radiance and direct its movement.

familyAndRelations: >
  Consort Shiva; bound up in this myth with the wider Kashyapa-Aditi-Surya
  solar family, though not by literal kinship.

significance: >
  Her day is tied to the sun's radiant energy and to the very origin of
  creation, a theme distinct from the martial emphasis of several other
  Navadurga forms.

regionalVariation: >
  No single named pilgrimage site stands out for her in the sources
  consulted; her worship follows the general pattern of Navratri's
  fourth-night observance.
