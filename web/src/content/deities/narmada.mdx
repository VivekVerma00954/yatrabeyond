quickIdentity: >
  Narmada is the goddess-personification of the river of the same name, one
  of India's seven sacred rivers, revered across Madhya Pradesh and Gujarat
  as a purifying presence often held, in local tradition, to be even more
  forgiving than the Ganga.

namesAndEpithets: >
  She is also called **Reva**, "the leaping one," a name used almost
  interchangeably with Narmada in devotional and literary contexts. Tradition
  holds her to be Shiva's daughter, born from his sweat during penance on
  Mount Riksha.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Unlike Ganga and Yamuna she is rarely given a standard anthropomorphic
  temple image at riverside ghats. Her sacred presence is instead
  represented through the banalinga, naturally lingam-shaped pebbles of white
  quartz found on her riverbed, prized as self-manifest forms of Shiva rather
  than of the goddess herself.

keyStories: >
  Born from Shiva's perspiration as he performed penance on Mount Riksha
  (identified with Amarkantak, her traditional source), she was blessed by
  him with the power to wash away the sins of anyone who so much as sees her
  waters, a boon popularly held to exceed even the need to bathe in the
  Ganga. The Narmada Parikrama, a full circumambulation of the river's course
  undertaken on foot, is one of the most demanding pilgrimages in Hinduism.

familyAndRelations: >
  In the dominant origin myth she is Shiva's daughter. She is grouped with
  Ganga and Yamuna among India's principal river-goddesses, though her cult
  is centred further south and west, in central-western India rather than
  the Gangetic plain.

significance: >
  The Matsya Purana declares every bank along the Narmada sacred. Her source
  at Amarkantak and towns along her course, notably Omkareshwar and
  Maheshwar, are major pilgrimage and temple centres, and pebbles gathered
  from her bed are carried to Shiva shrines across India.

regionalVariation: >
  Her worship and the Parikrama tradition are strongest in Madhya Pradesh
  and Gujarat, the states through which the river flows. Compared with
  Ganga's pan-Indian primacy, devotion to Narmada remains more regionally
  rooted in central-western India, even as her sanctity is acknowledged
  nationwide.
