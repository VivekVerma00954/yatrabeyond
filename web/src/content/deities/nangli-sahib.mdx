quickIdentity: >
  Swami Swarupanand Ji Maharaj (1884-1936) was the second guru of the
  Paramhans Advait Mat lineage, a North Indian devotional sect. He is
  venerated today at his samadhi shrine at the village of Nangli near
  Meerut, known as Nangli Sahib or Nangli Tirath, which gives him the
  registry name used on this platform.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Followers address him as **Hari Har Baba**, **Sadhgurudev Ji** and, in
  recognition of his lasting association with his samadhi site, **Nangli
  Niwasi Bhagwaan Ji**, "the Lord who dwells at Nangli." He is also referred
  to simply as the sect's "Second Guru."

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  As a historical figure from the twentieth century rather than a Puranic
  deity, he has no classical iconography. He is represented through
  photographic portraits and through his samadhi shrine at Nangli, which
  functions as the focal point of devotion in place of a sculpted or painted
  image.

keyStories: >
  Born in the village of Teri, in what is now Kohat district, Pakistan, he
  was initiated into sanyas in the early 1900s by Paramhans Swami Advaitanand
  Ji, founder of the Advait Mat lineage, who gave him the name Swarupanand.
  He went on to build an order of renunciate disciples and founded several
  hundred ashrams across northern India before his death at Nangli in 1936,
  by which time he had more than ten thousand followers.

familyAndRelations: >
  He was the direct spiritual successor, as "second guru," to Paramhans
  Swami Advaitanand Ji Maharaj (1846-1919), founder of the Paramhans Advait
  Mat. The lineage of gurus within the sect continues from him to later
  successors.

significance: >
  Nangli Tirath remains a major pilgrimage and ashram centre for the
  Paramhans Advait Mat, sustained today by the Shri Nangli Sahib Darbar
  Trust. His life is presented within the sect chiefly as one of building
  disciplined renunciate community and spreading his own guru's teaching
  across the region.

regionalVariation: >
  His following is concentrated in northern India, particularly Uttar
  Pradesh, Punjab and Haryana, reflecting the geography of the ashrams
  founded during his lifetime. As with other living devotional lineages, the
  wording of hymns and prayer texts associated with him is treated
  separately from this biographical profile and follows its own sourcing and
  clearance process.
