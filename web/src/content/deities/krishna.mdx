quickIdentity: >
  Krishna is the eighth avatar of Vishnu, worshipped both as an avatar and,
  in several major traditions, as the supreme god in his own right, of whom
  Vishnu himself is a partial expression. He appears across the tradition in
  two quite different registers: the mischievous, flute-playing cowherd of
  Vrindavan, and the composed statesman-charioteer who delivers the
  Bhagavad Gita on a battlefield.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Govinda** ("cow-finder/protector of cows") and **Gopala** ("cowherd")
  root him in his pastoral upbringing. **Madhava** links him to the Yadu
  clan and to Lakshmi. **Mohan** ("enchanter") and **Murlidhar/Muralidhara**
  ("flute-bearer") describe his charm and his signature instrument.
  **Vasudeva** marks him as son of Vasudeva; **Kanha/Kanhaiya** are
  affectionate, everyday names still used for him as a child-god today.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Most often shown with blue or dark skin, standing in a relaxed
  three-bend pose (*tribhanga*) playing a flute, a peacock feather in his
  crown. As the child-god (Bala Krishna) he's shown crawling or reaching for
  butter; as the Gita's speaker he's shown standing in a chariot, one hand
  raised, addressing the warrior Arjuna. The cow and calf appear frequently
  around him, and Radha is very often shown at his side, especially in
  Vaishnava devotional (bhakti) art, though, as below, how central she is
  depends heavily on which tradition you're looking at.

keyStories: >
  Born in a prison cell in Mathura to parents held captive by the tyrant
  king Kamsa, infant Krishna is smuggled across the river to Gokul to be
  raised by the cowherd couple Yashoda and Nanda, safely away from Kamsa's
  attempts to kill him. His childhood in Vrindavan is filled with mischief
  and wonder: stealing butter, subduing the venomous serpent Kaliya in the
  river, lifting the hill Govardhana on one finger for seven days to shelter
  villagers from a storm sent by an angry Indra, and playing his flute for
  the gopis (cowherd women), among whom Radha holds a singular place in
  devotional literature. He later returns to Mathura and kills Kamsa,
  restoring the rightful king. As an adult he becomes a central strategist
  in the Mahabharata war, and on its battlefield delivers the Bhagavad Gita
  to the warrior Arjuna, counselling him on duty, action without attachment
  to outcome, and devotion. His life story is unusually split between these
  two halves: the intimate, playful child and lover of Vrindavan, and the
  composed statesman of Dwarka and Kurukshetra.

familyAndRelations: >
  Biological son of Vasudeva and Devaki; raised by the cowherd couple Nanda
  and Yashoda in Vrindavan. Radha is his beloved in the Vrindavan stories,
  central to Krishna bhakti in many traditions though her exact standing
  differs by school, discussed below. His formally married queens in later
  life include Rukmini and Satyabhama, among others, once he becomes king
  at Dwarka.

significance: >
  His teaching in the Bhagavad Gita, on selfless action and devotion (bhakti)
  as a path to the divine, is one of the most widely read and quoted texts
  in Hinduism, well beyond Krishna devotion specifically. Janmashtami, his
  birth festival, is celebrated widely across India. In bhakti traditions
  more broadly, the intense, personal love expressed toward him by Radha and
  the gopis is held up as a model for how a devotee should love God.

regionalVariation: >
  Gaudiya Vaishnavism, founded by the 16th-century Bengali saint Chaitanya,
  places Krishna as the supreme godhead in his own right, with Radha as his
  essential, inseparable counterpart, not just one gopi among several, this
  is a genuinely different theological ranking from traditions that treat
  Radha as a devotional figure without formalising her status to that
  degree. Elsewhere, Krishna is approached primarily as an avatar of Vishnu
  rather than the supreme god himself, with Radha appearing far less
  prominently. Regional performance and art traditions also diverge sharply:
  Odisha's Jagannath (worshipped at Puri) is widely understood as a form of
  Krishna, with an iconography (large eyes, stump-like arms) that owes
  nothing to the flute-playing Vrindavan image at all.
