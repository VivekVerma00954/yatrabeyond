quickIdentity: >
  Hanuman is the monkey-god of the Ramayana, Rama's most devoted follower,
  and a widely worshipped deity in his own right, celebrated for strength,
  courage, celibate discipline, and total, unwavering devotion (bhakti).

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Anjaneya** and **Anjaniputra** both mark him as the son of the celestial
  nymph Anjana. **Pavanputra** ("son of the wind god") points to his
  divine, non-ordinary parentage. **Bajrangbali** ("one with limbs strong as
  a thunderbolt") is an especially common devotional name in North India,
  and **Maruti** is another widely used variant tied to the wind god
  Vayu/Maruti.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Shown as a muscular figure with a monkey's face and a long tail, often
  mid-flight or in a devotional pose, hands folded before Rama. He's
  frequently pictured tearing open his own chest to reveal Rama and Sita
  enshrined in his heart, a direct visual statement of the devotion he
  represents rather than a narrative episode as such. He carries a mace
  (*gada*) and, in a widespread and dramatic depiction, is shown carrying an
  entire mountain, the Sanjeevani hill, in one hand.

keyStories: >
  A gifted, powerful child prone to mischief, young Hanuman once leapt for
  the sun mistaking it for a fruit, an act that led the god Indra to strike
  him down and, in most versions, prompted his father Vayu to briefly
  withdraw his breath from the world in anger, until the gods intervened
  with blessings that gave Hanuman his great strength and near-invulnerability.
  As an adult he serves the monkey-king Sugriva, and it's Hanuman who finds
  the abducted Sita in Lanka, delivering Rama's ring to her as proof of
  rescue to come, then, famously, allows himself to be captured and set
  alight by Ravana's men, only to use his own burning tail to set fire to
  the city. During the war's most desperate hour, when Lakshmana is
  gravely wounded, Hanuman flies to the Himalayas and, unable to identify
  the single healing herb needed, carries the entire Sanjeevani mountain
  back to the battlefield rather than risk delay. Throughout, his defining
  trait isn't his strength so much as what he does with it: total,
  unhesitating service to Rama, expecting nothing in return.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of Anjana and the wind god Vayu. He has no consort in the main
  tradition, his celibacy (as a *brahmachari*) is itself a defining and
  widely celebrated part of his character, tied closely to the discipline
  and single-minded devotion he represents.

significance: >
  Hanuman is invoked for protection, strength, and the removal of
  obstacles and fear, the Hanuman Chalisa, a 16th-century devotional hymn
  attributed to Tulsidas, is among the most widely recited texts in Hindu
  practice today, well beyond dedicated Hanuman worship. He's held up as
  the ideal devotee, the standard against which selfless service and
  loyalty are measured, and Tuesdays and Saturdays are widely observed as
  days particularly associated with his worship.

regionalVariation: >
  Hanuman worship is remarkably consistent in tone across India compared to
  some other deities on this list, reverence for his strength and devotion
  holds fairly steady region to region, though regional Ramayana traditions
  do vary his prominence and detail. Outside India, Southeast Asian
  retellings give him a considerably larger and more independent role,
  Thailand's Ramakien in particular develops Hanuman into a far more
  central, even romantically involved, character than the main Sanskrit
  tradition allows, which is a genuinely different portrayal, not just a
  stylistic variation.
