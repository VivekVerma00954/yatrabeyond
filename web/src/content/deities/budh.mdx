quickIdentity: >
  Budh, or Budha, personifies the planet Mercury as one of the Navagraha and
  gives his name to Wednesday (Budhavara). He is considered a generally
  benevolent graha associated with intellect, communication and memory.

namesAndEpithets: >
  His name, from Sanskrit *budh*, "to be awake" or "to know," reflects his
  association with a sharp, agile mind. Wednesday, Budhavara in Hindi and
  several other Indian languages, derives directly from him.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is generally shown as a benevolent, green-bodied deity draped in yellow,
  riding a chariot drawn by eight yellow horses, or in some depictions a
  lion, and bearing a sword, club and shield alongside a hand raised in the
  varada mudra, the gesture of granting boons.

keyStories: >
  He is the son of Chandra, the Moon, and Tara, wife of his guru Brihaspati;
  his birth follows the Tarakamaya war, fought between the gods and Chandra
  over Tara's abduction. Grown, he married Ila and fathered Pururavas, the
  legendary founder of the lunar (Chandravanshi) dynasty of kings.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of Chandra and Tara. His marriage to Ila and fatherhood of Pururavas
  place him at the root of the lunar dynasty that runs through much of later
  Puranic and epic genealogy.

significance: >
  As one of the Navagraha he governs intellect, speech, trade and
  short-distance travel in Vedic astrology, worshipped particularly on
  Wednesdays. Indian astronomical treatises, from the fifth-century
  Aryabhatiya onward, record centuries of careful observation of Mercury
  under his name.

regionalVariation: >
  He is worshipped chiefly as part of the Navagraha set installed in temple
  courtyards across India rather than through dedicated standalone Budh
  temples, his role in personal worship being mostly astrological, seeking
  his blessing for clarity of mind and successful communication.
