quickIdentity: >
  Radha is Krishna's beloved and, in the traditions that centre her most,
  considered the feminine counterpart of the divine itself, not simply one
  of the gopis (cowherd women) of Vrindavan. How exalted her status is,
  though, genuinely depends on which tradition you ask, and that variation
  is worth being upfront about rather than flattening.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Radhika** is a common affectionate variant of her name. **Radharani**
  ("Queen Radha") marks her sovereign standing in traditions that treat her
  as co-equal with Krishna. She's also referred to simply as **Vrishabhanu
  Nandini**, "daughter of Vrishabhanu," tying her to her father and her home
  village of Barsana.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Shown as a beautiful young woman, usually in a red or pink sari,
  frequently paired directly with Krishna in the *Radha-Krishna* image:
  standing or dancing together, often mid-*raas leela* (the circular dance
  of Krishna and the gopis). She's rarely depicted alone in the way Krishna
  or Shiva can be; her iconography is built around the pairing itself,
  which is itself telling about how the tradition frames her.

keyStories: >
  Raised in Barsana, near Vrindavan, Radha is Krishna's beloved during his
  cowherd years, and their love, intense, often depicted as fraught with
  separation and longing, is the central emotional narrative of Vrindavan
  devotional literature, especially the raas leela dances. Much of this
  material is not from the earliest Krishna texts at all, it develops later,
  especially through medieval devotional poetry (for instance Jayadeva's
  12th-century Gita Govinda), which is where Radha's role as Krishna's
  supreme beloved gets most fully elaborated. Unlike Krishna's other consorts
  such as Rukmini, whom he formally marries as a king, Radha's bond with him
  is usually told as unmarried, devotional love, held up in bhakti theology
  as a metaphor for the soul's longing for the divine rather than a
  domestic relationship.

familyAndRelations: >
  Daughter of Vrishabhanu, raised in Barsana. Beloved of Krishna during his
  Vrindavan years. Some later traditions describe a marriage between them,
  but this is not the dominant or original telling, most devotional
  literature keeps the relationship in the register of yearning, devoted
  love rather than a married household, which is itself part of its
  theological point.

significance: >
  In Gaudiya Vaishnavism and related bhakti schools, Radha is not a minor
  figure alongside Krishna, she's treated as the embodiment of *bhakti*
  (devotion) itself, and her longing for Krishna is the model devotees are
  asked to imitate in their own relationship with God. Her festival,
  Radhashtami, is observed with particular importance in Vrindavan and
  Barsana.

regionalVariation: >
  This is one of the more genuinely contested figures in this batch. Gaudiya
  Vaishnavism (founded by the 16th-century Bengali saint Chaitanya) and the
  broader Vrindavan devotional world elevate Radha to near-equal, sometimes
  even superior, standing alongside Krishna, some texts in this tradition
  describe her as the source of Krishna's own power (his Shakti). Other,
  more Vishnu-centred Vaishnava traditions, and regions where Krishna
  worship centres on his roles as king or as the Gita's speaker rather than
  the Vrindavan cowherd, give Radha a much smaller or even absent role. In
  other words, whether Radha is "essential" or "supplementary" to Krishna
  worship is not a settled matter across Hinduism, it depends heavily on
  which regional and sectarian tradition is doing the telling.
