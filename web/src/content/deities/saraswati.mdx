quickIdentity: >
  Saraswati is the goddess of knowledge, learning, language, music and the
  arts, one of the Tridevi alongside Lakshmi and Parvati. She has among the
  oldest pedigrees of any Hindu deity: she begins in the Rigveda as a mighty
  river and its presiding goddess, and only later becomes the patron of
  speech, wisdom and creativity she is today.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Sharada** ("she who gives the essence"), **Vagdevi** and **Vani**
  ("goddess of speech"), **Bharati**, and **Vagishwari** all name her as the
  deity of language and eloquence. As a river-goddess she is remembered in
  the lost Sarasvati of the Vedic hymns.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown as a serene, fair woman in a white sari, seated on a white
  lotus or riding a swan (hamsa), sometimes with a peacock nearby. Her four
  hands typically hold a *veena* (the instrument that makes her the source of
  music), a book or palm-leaf manuscript (the Vedas and all learning), a
  *mala* (rosary, for the inner over the outer), and a water-pot. White
  stands for the purity and clarity of true knowledge; the swan for the
  discernment that separates essence from dross.

keyStories: >
  In Puranic tradition she is closely linked to Brahma the creator as the
  embodiment of the speech and knowledge through which creation is ordered.
  Her Vedic identity as a river comes first, though: the Rigveda praises
  Sarasvati as a great flowing river and as the goddess who inspires hymns
  and purifies. Her shift from sacred river to goddess of learning is one of
  the clearest examples in Hinduism of a deity's meaning evolving across many
  centuries.

familyAndRelations: >
  In Puranic accounts she is associated with Brahma; in the Tridevi framework
  she is grouped with Lakshmi and Parvati as the three great goddesses,
  consorts of the Trimurti. These relational claims belong to later Puranic
  theology rather than to her original Vedic character as a river.

significance: >
  She is worshipped by students, scholars, musicians and artists, and invoked
  at the beginning of any learning. Her chief festival is Vasant Panchami, in
  spring, when children are often taught to write their first letters and
  instruments and books are laid before her. The day treats knowledge itself
  as sacred.

regionalVariation: >
  She is especially prominent in eastern India, where Saraswati Puja on Vasant
  Panchami is a major school and college festival in West Bengal and
  neighbouring states. As Sharada she is central to the Sharada Peeth
  tradition of Kashmir and to Kashmiri identity; in the south she is enshrined
  at temples such as Basar. She is also revered beyond Hinduism, in Jain
  tradition and, as Benzaiten, in Japanese Buddhism, a reach few Hindu
  deities have.
