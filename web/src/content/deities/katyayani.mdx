quickIdentity: >
  Katyayani is the sixth of the Navadurga, the warrior form credited in
  scripture with slaying the buffalo-demon Mahishasura, the same central
  myth carried by Durga's own Mahishasuramardini identity. She is first
  named in the Taittiriya Aranyaka of the Yajurveda, well before the
  Puranic myths built around her.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name means "daughter of, or created by, the sage Katyayana": the
  Vamana Purana holds she took form in his hermitage, while the Kalika
  Purana holds he was simply the first to worship her. She is also called
  **Mahishasuramardini**, "slayer of Mahishasura," after her defining feat.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown with four arms, riding a lion, holding a sword and a lotus,
  with her other two hands in the abhaya and varada mudras of protection and
  boon-giving. Her complexion is golden and radiant. She is traditionally
  associated with the colour red, and in yogic and tantric traditions with
  the ajna, or third-eye, chakra.

keyStories: >
  Per the Vamana Purana, when the gods' collective fury at Mahishasura's
  tyranny blazed from their faces as flame, the combined light coalesced
  into a mountain of radiance in the hermitage of the sage Katyayana, from
  which Katyayani emerged, three-eyed, black-haired and eighteen-armed. Each
  god armed her with his own weapon: Shiva's trident, Vishnu's Sudarshana
  Chakra, Varuna's conch, Agni's dart, Vayu's bow, Surya's quiver of arrows,
  Indra's thunderbolt, Kuvera's mace, Brahma's rosary and water-pot among
  them. Thus armed, she went to the Mysore hills, where Mahishasura, taken
  by her beauty, sought her hand; she told him she could only be won in
  battle. He fought her in his buffalo form, and she killed him with her
  sword, earning the title Mahishasuramardini. A separate account, carried
  in the Skanda Purana, credits her with swallowing all of the
  self-replicating demon Raktabija's blood to stop his clones from
  multiplying, aiding the god Bhairava in that later war. In the Bhagavata
  Purana, the gopis of Gokul keep the Katyayani Vrata through the month of
  Margashirsha, praying to win Krishna as their husband, a vow followed by
  the well-known episode of Krishna taking their clothes while they bathe in
  the Yamuna.

familyAndRelations: >
  Consort Shiva. In Shaktism she is grouped with other fierce Shakti forms
  such as Bhadrakali and Chandika, and is also regarded as sister of Surya,
  worshipped alongside him during Chhath Puja in eastern India. Devi Kanya
  Kumari, worshipped at India's southern tip, is held to be her avatar (or
  Parvati's).

significance: >
  She stands at the heart of Durga Puja's central myth, the slaying of
  Mahishasura. Her Tulja Bhavani form at Tuljapur, second among the Shakta
  Pithas, is the family deity of the Bhosale Maratha royal line; tradition
  holds she gave Shivaji the Bhavani sword for his campaigns.

regionalVariation: >
  Worshipped as family deity at Tuljapur by the Bhosale line; honoured
  during Tamil Pongal through sand-idol worship; held temples at Cherthala
  and Kumaranalloor in Kerala; and central to the Braj tradition of the
  gopis' vow during Margashirsha.
