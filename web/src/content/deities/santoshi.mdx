quickIdentity: >
  Santoshi Mata, the "Mother of Contentment" (santosh), is a comparatively
  modern Hindu goddess who rose to nationwide devotion with remarkable speed
  after the 1975 film *Jai Santoshi Maa*. She is worshipped above all through
  a sixteen-Friday vow (vrat) for the fulfilment of wishes and the granting of
  a settled, contented heart.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name is itself her defining epithet: from *santosh*, contentment, she
  is the Mother who grants a satisfied heart rather than mere wealth.
  Devotees address her simply as Santoshi Maa.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is depicted as a benign young goddess seated on a lotus, usually with
  a small number of hands holding a trident, a sword and vessels, in a form
  modelled on the established Mother-goddess iconography. Her worship is
  marked by its simplicity: the Friday offering is famously plain, jaggery
  and roasted chickpeas (gud-chana), and devotees strictly avoid anything
  sour on the fasting day.

keyStories: >
  Popular tradition, given its familiar shape by the 1975 film, makes her the
  mind-born daughter of Ganesha and his wives Riddhi and Siddhi, and the
  sister of Ganesha's sons Shubh and Labh, created in answer to the
  children's wish for a sister. The film's central story follows a devotee,
  Satyavati, whose faith and Friday vrat carry her through hardship, and it
  was this narrative, rather than any ancient scripture, that took the goddess
  into homes across India.

familyAndRelations: >
  In the popular account she is the daughter of Ganesha; she has no
  significant presence in the classical Puranic pantheon. Her family tree is
  essentially the one dramatised in the twentieth century.

significance: >
  Her worship centres on the sixteen-Friday vrat and its katha, undertaken
  for contentment, harmony and the resolution of difficulties. Her appeal has
  been strongest among ordinary households, and especially women, drawn by an
  accessible, inexpensive form of devotion that promises peace of mind.

regionalVariation: >
  Santoshi Mata is a genuinely modern phenomenon, and honesty about that is
  part of respecting her: her rise is documented as a twentieth-century
  development amplified by cinema and popular print, not an ancient temple
  tradition. For that reason the specific wording of her vrat katha and songs
  is of recent, and in places contested or copyrighted, authorship, and this
  platform treats those texts with corresponding care.
