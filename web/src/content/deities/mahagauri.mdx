quickIdentity: >
  Mahagauri, "the great fair one," is the eighth of the Navadurga, goddess of
  beauty and purity, representing Parvati restored to radiant fairness after
  her fierce battle-form.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name joins *maha* (great) and *gauri* (fair-complexioned).

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She has four arms and rides a white ox. Her upper right hand holds a
  trident, her upper left a damaru (small hand-drum), her lower right shows
  the abhayamudra gesture, and her lower left holds a japamala or performs
  the varadamudra.

keyStories: >
  Per the Shiva Purana, the asuras Shumbha and Nishumbha had won a boon from
  Brahma that they could be killed only by a virgin form of Parvati.
  Parvati's complexion was at that time dark, and Shiva would tease her by
  calling her "Kali," the dark one, which agitated her into undertaking
  severe penance to Brahma for a fair complexion. Brahma instead asked her
  first to slay Shumbha and Nishumbha. She went to bathe in the Ganga in the
  Himalaya, where she found the gods praying for the demons' destruction; as
  she bathed, her dark skin washed away entirely, and from that shed skin
  the goddess Kaushiki emerged, while Parvati herself became fair-skinned,
  clothed in white, earning the name Mahagauri. Kaushiki then went to battle
  as Chandika, killing the asura Dhumralochana, producing Chamunda from her
  third eye to kill Chanda and Munda, and killing Raktabija and his clones
  before finally slaying Shumbha and Nishumbha, earning in turn the titles
  Mahasaraswati and Ambika. With the battle won, Mahagauri rode her ox home
  to Kailasha, where Shiva awaited her, and the two were reunited.

familyAndRelations: >
  Consort Shiva; per this legend, mother, with him, of Kartikeya and
  Ganesha.

significance: >
  Worshipped on Ashtami, the eighth night of Navratri, often alongside the
  Kanya Puja tradition of honouring young girls as living forms of the
  Devi.

regionalVariation: >
  No single named pilgrimage site stands out for her in the sources
