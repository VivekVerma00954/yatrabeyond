quickIdentity: >
  Brahmacharini is the second of the Navadurga, Parvati in the form of a
  female ascetic who undertook a punishing penance to win Shiva as her
  husband. She represents unwavering tapasya, discipline and devotion.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name joins *Brahma* (here, the Absolute, the sacred knowledge) and
  *charini*, "one who follows or practises," giving "she who pursues sacred
  religious knowledge," a sense already present in Vedic usage.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown dressed in white as a female ascetic, holding a japamala
  (prayer beads) in her right hand and a kamandalu (water pot) in her left.

keyStories: >
  Parvati resolved to marry Shiva despite her parents' objections and
  undertook a penance said to have lasted 5,000 years, living on bael leaves
  and river water alone. To distract Shiva from his meditation, the gods sent
  Kamadeva, god of desire, to strike him with an arrow of longing; enraged,
  Shiva burned Kamadeva to ash with his third eye, yet Parvati's resolve did
  not waver. During her weakest hour, the asura Prakandasura attacked her
  with a vast army; Lakshmi and Saraswati came to her aid but were
  outnumbered, until her fallen kamandalu released a flood that swept the
  asuras away, and Parvati herself burned Prakandasura to ash. Shiva,
  impressed, approached her disguised as a wandering brahmachari and tested
  her resolve with riddles before revealing his true form and consenting to
  marry her.

familyAndRelations: >
  Consort Shiva, won through this penance. Her story is carried in the Devi
  Bhagavata Purana and the Devi Gita.

significance: >
  Her day is kept as an occasion for strengthening willpower, endurance and
  devotion, echoing the discipline of her own long tapasya.

regionalVariation: >
  A named shrine is the Maa Brahmacharini Devi Durga Mandir at Hanuman Ganj,
  Ballia, Uttar Pradesh; beyond this, her worship follows the general pattern
  of Navratri's second-day observance rather than a distinct regional cult.
