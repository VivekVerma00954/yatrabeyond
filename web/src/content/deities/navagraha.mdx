quickIdentity: >
  The Navagraha, "nine seizers" or "nine planets," are the nine celestial
  deities that form the foundational planetary framework of Jyotisha, Vedic
  astrology, believed to influence human life for good or ill. This entry
  describes them as a system; each graha also has its own individual
  profile on this platform.

namesAndEpithets: >
  The nine are Surya (Sun), Chandra (Moon), Mangal (Mars), Budh (Mercury),
  Brihaspati (Jupiter), Shukra (Venus), Shani (Saturn), and the two lunar
  nodes, Rahu and Ketu. Rahu and Ketu are distinguished from the other
  seven as chayagraha, "shadow planets," having no physical body.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Together they are represented in temple architecture through the
  Navagraha Mandala, a designated shrine, most often a raised platform or
  small pavilion holding nine images or emblems arranged around a central
  Surya, found in the majority of Hindu temples across India as a distinct
  point of worship separate from the temple's main deity.

keyStories: >
  Their conceptual roots lie in the earliest Vedic literature, where the
  Rigveda and Atharvaveda already treat the Sun, Moon and certain stars as
  cosmic forces affecting the earth, though not yet as a fixed group of
  nine. Rahu and Ketu, understood as the severed head and body of the
  slain asura Svarbhanu, were integrated only gradually as full members of
  the group, becoming firmly established as chayagrahas alongside the
  seven visible bodies by around the eighth or ninth century CE.

familyAndRelations: >
  Each graha carries its own genealogy (detailed in their individual
  profiles), but as a set they are understood collectively as the
  planetary forces whose positions at a person's birth are read in the
  birth chart (janma kundali) to determine character and destiny.

significance: >
  Nearly every Hindu temple maintains a Navagraha shrine, and devotees
  perform specific pujas and donations to particular grahas, especially
  Shani, Rahu and Ketu, to ease difficult planetary periods; each graha is
  also associated with a day of the week and specific life domains in
  Jyotisha practice.

regionalVariation: >
  The Navagraha Mandala is a near-universal feature of temple architecture
  across India, though its placement, prominence and the specific rituals
  performed at it vary between North and South Indian temple traditions and
  between individual sampradayas.
