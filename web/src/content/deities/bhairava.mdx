quickIdentity: >
  Kaal Bhairav is the fierce, terrifying form of Shiva, the embodiment of
  Time (kala) as the force that annihilates, and the guardian of sacred
  space. Above all he is the **Kotwal of Kashi**, the divine "chief of police"
  of Varanasi, without whose leave, tradition says, no one truly abides in the
  holy city.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Bhairava** ("the frightful"), **Kaal Bhairav** ("Bhairava of
  Time/Death"), **Kshetrapala** ("guardian of the sacred place") and **Batuk
  Bhairav** (his gentler, child form) name his aspects. In Kashi he is simply
  the **Kotwal**, the city's guardian-magistrate.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is shown fierce and often minimally clad, dark-complexioned, with wild
  hair, three eyes, a garland of skulls, and weapons, a trident, sword, noose
  and a severed head, accompanied by his mount, the dog. The dog, an animal
  considered impure in orthodox settings, is deliberately his companion,
  marking him as the deity who stands outside ordinary purity rules and guards
  the thresholds others fear.

keyStories: >
  A central myth explains his origin: when Brahma, in arrogance, disrespected
  Shiva, Shiva manifested Bhairava, who severed one of Brahma's five heads.
  The sin of that act (Brahmahatya) clung to him as a skull fused to his hand,
  and he wandered as a mendicant until the sin was finally lifted at Kashi,
  which is why Kashi becomes his eternal seat and he its guardian. The story
  ties him to transgression, penance, and the power of the holy city to
  absolve even the gravest sin.

familyAndRelations: >
  He is a direct emanation of Shiva rather than a separate deity with his own
  household, and is counted among the *Ashta Bhairava*, the eight Bhairavas
  guarding the eight directions. As a form of Shiva he stands within the
  Shaiva family rather than heading one of his own.

significance: >
  In Kashi, pilgrims traditionally seek Kaal Bhairav's blessing as the city's
  guardian, and a black protective thread from his temple is worn afterwards.
  He is invoked for the removal of fear, enemies and obstacles, and is
  especially important in Tantric and Shaiva traditions as a fierce protector.
  His festival is *Kaal Bhairav Ashtami* (Bhairava Ashtami).

regionalVariation: >
  Bhairava worship is widespread and takes many local forms: the Kaal Bhairav
  of Ujjain is famously offered liquor as prasad; across the Himalayas and in
  Nepal he is a major Tantric deity (Kathmandu's Kaal and Akash Bhairava); and
  he is revered in Buddhist Tantra as well. His fierce, boundary-guarding
  character makes him a favourite village and town protector well beyond the
  Sanskritic temple tradition.
