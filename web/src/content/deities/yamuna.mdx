quickIdentity: >
  Yamuna, goddess of the river of the same name, is the second of Hinduism's
  great river-deities after Ganga and is inseparable from the Krishna
  devotion of the Braj region, where her banks are the setting for his
  childhood play. She is revered as a gentle, loving mother whose waters are
  especially dear to Krishna.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Yami** is her Vedic name as the twin of Yama. **Kalindi** ("daughter of
  Mount Kalinda," her source) is the name by which she is often known in the
  Krishna tradition, and also the name of one of Krishna's queens identified
  with her. **Yamuna Maiya** is her affectionate address.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is depicted as a beautiful woman, often dark-complexioned in keeping
  with her river's darker waters (a contrast the tradition deliberately draws
  with the fair Ganga), holding a water-pot and lotus and riding a *kurma*
  (tortoise), her mount. Her dark colour and her tortoise distinguish her at a
  glance from Ganga on her makara.

keyStories: >
  As the daughter of the sun-god Surya and twin sister of Yama, the god of
  death, tradition holds that bathing in her waters frees one from the fear of
  death, a boon tied to her brother's realm. Her deepest associations are with
  Krishna: the infant Krishna is carried across the flooded Yamuna on the
  night of his birth; as a boy he subdues the serpent Kaliya in her waters and
  dances on its hoods; and her banks at Vrindavan and Mathura are the stage
  for the raslila. She is counted among Krishna's consorts as Kalindi.

familyAndRelations: >
  Daughter of Surya (the Sun) and Sanjna, and twin sister of Yama, lord of
  death. In the Krishna tradition she is drawn into his circle as the queen
  Kalindi. These two families, the solar and the Braj, together define her.

significance: >
  She is worshipped throughout Braj and along her course, with Yamuna aartis
  and the festival of *Yamuna Chhath* (Yamuna Jayanti) marking her descent to
  earth; bathing in her is held to remove the fear of death and to purify.
  Like Ganga, she faces acute modern pollution, sharpening the tension between
  her sacred status and the river's physical condition.

regionalVariation: >
  Her cult is most intense in Braj (Mathura, Vrindavan) and in the Pushtimarg
  (Vallabha) sampradaya, where she is a supremely important goddess and an
  object of loving devotion in her own right, not merely a sacred river. Her
  prominence is markedly higher in these Krishna-centred communities than in
  regions without the Braj connection.
