quickIdentity: >
  Ganga is the goddess of the river Ganges and the most sacred river-deity of
  Hinduism, revered as a purifying mother whose waters are believed to cleanse
  sin and carry the departed toward liberation. River and goddess are not
  separate: to bathe in the Ganga is held to touch the deity directly.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Ganga Maiya** ("Mother Ganga"), **Bhagirathi** (after the sage Bhagiratha
  who brought her to earth, and the name of one of her Himalayan
  head-streams), **Jahnavi** (from the sage Jahnu), and **Tripathaga**
  ("flowing through three worlds") name her descent and her role.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown as a graceful, fair, crowned woman, richly adorned, holding a
  water-pot (kumbha) and a lotus and riding the *makara*, a composite
  crocodile-like water creature that is her mount. The water, the pot and the
  makara all mark her as the living presence of the river itself.

keyStories: >
  Her great story is the *Gangavatarana*, her descent to earth. The sage
  Bhagiratha performed severe austerities to bring the heavenly river down to
  purify the ashes of his ancestors; because her fall would have shattered the
  earth, Shiva received her in the coils of his matted hair and released her
  gently, which is why he is shown with Ganga in his locks. In the Mahabharata
  she is also the mother of Bhishma. Scriptures variously trace her origin to
  Vishnu's feet and name her as a daughter of Himavat and the elder sister of
  Parvati.

familyAndRelations: >
  Traditions differ: she is described as issuing from Vishnu's feet, and also
  as the firstborn daughter of Himavat (the Himalaya) and thus elder sister of
  Parvati. She is linked to Shiva, who bears her in his hair, and in the
  Mahabharata is the mother of Bhishma.

significance: >
  Bathing in the Ganga, especially at Haridwar, Prayagraj and Varanasi, is
  among the most meritorious acts in Hindu practice; her water is used in
  rituals, and immersing the ashes of the dead in her is believed to aid the
  soul's release. The riverside *Ganga Aarti* performed each evening at
  Haridwar and Varanasi is one of the most recognisable living rituals in
  India.

regionalVariation: >
  Devotion follows her whole course, from her Himalayan source at Gangotri,
  through the great pilgrimage cities of the plains, to her delta in Bengal.
  She is honoured at the vast Kumbh Mela gatherings. Her worship now coexists
  uneasily with the modern reality of severe pollution, which has made the
  river's physical cleaning a matter of public policy alongside its enduring
  spiritual purity.
