quickIdentity: >
  Lakshmana is Rama's younger half-brother and his most devoted companion,
  the model in the Ramayana of selfless service (seva) and loyalty.
  Tradition holds him to be an incarnation of Shesha, the cosmic serpent on
  whom Vishnu reclines, so that where Rama is Vishnu, Lakshmana is the one
  who serves and shelters him.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Saumitra**, "son of Sumitra," names him by his mother. **Lakshman** is
  the common vernacular form. Grouped with his brothers he is one of the
  four sons of Dasharatha, but he is remembered above all by the single
  quality the tradition attaches to him: unwavering devotion to Rama.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is almost never shown alone. In the standard "Rama Darbar" grouping he
  stands at Rama's left with his bow, a younger, fairer figure mirroring his
  elder brother, Sita to Rama's right and Hanuman at their feet. The bow
  marks him as a warrior; his placement, always beside and a step behind
  Rama, marks him as the servant-brother rather than a deity worshipped in
  his own right.

keyStories: >
  When Rama is exiled for fourteen years, Lakshmana leaves his own home and
  comfort to follow him into the forest, choosing service over ease. A
  much-loved tradition holds that he stayed awake the entire fourteen years
  to guard Rama and Sita; the goddess of sleep, Nidra, granted this only
  because his wife Urmila agreed to sleep in his place for all fourteen years
  (the "Urmila Nidra"). The "Lakshman Rekha," the protective line he is said
  to have drawn around Sita before leaving to search for Rama, has entered
  everyday Indian speech as a metaphor for a boundary one must not cross,
  though this episode is more prominent in later and regional retellings than
  in the earliest Valmiki text. In the war at Lanka he is gravely wounded and
  saved only by the Sanjeevani herb Hanuman carries from the mountains.

familyAndRelations: >
  Son of King Dasharatha of Ayodhya and Queen Sumitra, and twin of
  Shatrughna; half-brother to Rama and Bharata. His wife is Urmila, Sita's
  younger sister and a daughter of King Janaka of Mithila. His life is
  defined less by his own household than by his lifelong service to Rama and
  Sita.

significance: >
  He is venerated as the ideal of devotion and service, the younger one who
  subordinates his whole life to a higher love and duty. He is rarely the
  sole focus of a temple; his worship is almost always alongside Rama and
  Sita. A well-known temple associated with him stands at Lakshman Jhula in
  Rishikesh, where tradition holds he meditated and crossed the Ganga.

regionalVariation: >
  Urmila's fourteen-year sacrifice, largely passed over in the Sanskrit
  Valmiki Ramayana, is developed and celebrated in later and regional
  retellings, and has become a subject in its own right in modern literature
  that asks why the epic's women's sacrifices go unsung. As with the wider
  Ramayana, the weight given to Lakshmana's episodes shifts between
  Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas, the Tamil Kamba Ramayana, and other tellings.
