quickIdentity: >
  Parvati is the benevolent, householder face of the Great Goddess (Devi,
  Shakti) and the consort of Shiva. Daughter of the Himalaya, she's mother,
  wife, and, in Shakta theology, the very energy that makes Shiva's power
  active rather than inert.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Uma** is an older name, used almost interchangeably with Parvati in the
  epics. **Gauri** ("the fair/radiant one") is her bright, auspicious form,
  tied to ripened harvests and marital blessing. **Aparna** ("leafless")
  recalls legends of penance so extreme she stopped eating even leaves.
  **Kamakshi** and **Meenakshi** are regional South Indian forms with their
  own temple-specific iconography, at Kanchipuram and Madurai respectively.
  **Durga** and **Kali** are her fiercer aspects, invoked when the cosmos
  needs a warrior rather than a wife, though see the note below on how
  literally to take that equivalence. Regional epithets across India number
  well over a hundred.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She's typically shown as a mature, beautiful woman: fair-skinned, or
  golden-yellow as Gauri, often in a red sari, one hand raised in
  reassurance (*abhaya mudra*) and the other extended in blessing (*varada
  mudra*). She may hold a lotus, a mirror, a rosary, a conch, or a stalk of
  sugarcane (a nod to fertility and the harvest). In her fiercer forms,
  Durga in particular, she rides a lion or tiger, standing for mastery over
  wild, destructive forces; in gentler depictions as Parvati she's usually
  shown simply beside Shiva or Nandi rather than on a mount of her own. The
  composite form Ardhanarishvara, half Shiva and half Parvati in one body,
  is the clearest statement of how tightly the tradition binds the two
  together. Village and folk art often gives her distinctly regional
  features and dress rather than the "classical" Sanskritic look: a real,
  separate visual tradition, not just a rougher copy of the temple image.

keyStories: >
  In an earlier life as Sati, she immolated herself after her father Daksha
  insulted Shiva; she was reborn as Parvati specifically to win him back,
  which frames their marriage as a continuation rather than a fresh start.
  To draw the grieving, withdrawn Shiva out of isolation, she undertook
  extreme austerities, standing amid fire, then snow, until he tested her
  resolve in disguise and finally accepted her; it's usually told as the
  story of devotion succeeding where renunciation alone could not. Wanting
  privacy while bathing, she once formed a son, Ganesha, from turmeric paste
  without Shiva's involvement at all; Shiva, not recognising the boy,
  beheaded him, then restored him to life with an elephant's head: a story
  that asserts her own independent creative power rather than making her
  simply Shiva's other half. When demons like Mahishasura threatened the
  world, the gods' combined energy (or, in some tellings, her own fierce
  energy) took form as Durga to fight them. Devotional retellings often
  flatten this into "Parvati becomes Durga," which is a simplification worth
  knowing about (see below).

familyAndRelations: >
  Daughter of Himavat, the Himalaya personified, and his wife Mena. Wife of
  Shiva; mother of Ganesha and Kartikeya. In Shakta theology she's closely
  identified with Durga and Kali as forms of one Devi, a doctrinal position
  within that tradition, not a claim every Hindu text or community shares.

significance: >
  She's petitioned for marital harmony, fertility, and household wellbeing:
  festivals like Gauri and Gangaur, observed by married women especially in
  Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh, centre on her. In Shakta philosophy she is
  Shakti itself, the animating force without which Shiva is inert, a
  genuine theological claim, not just a devotional turn of phrase. In her
  fiercer forms she's invoked for protection and strength.

regionalVariation: >
  Two things are worth being honest about here. First, Shaktism centres Devi
  (Parvati among her forms) as ultimate reality with Shiva as her complement,
  while most mainstream Puranic and Shaiva material instead centres Shiva
  with Parvati as consort: these aren't just different emphases, they're
  different theological centres of gravity. Second, the popular claim that
  Durga and Kali are simply "Parvati's other forms" doesn't quite hold up
  against the texts: the Devi Mahatmya's own account has Durga forming from
  the combined energy of several male gods to fight a demon, not from a
  straightforward transformation of Parvati. Treat the equivalence as a
  later devotional synthesis rather than settled scripture. Separately, in
  eastern Indian terracotta art and parts of central India, she merges with
  local village-guardian goddess traditions tied to land and livestock, a
  genuinely distinct regional layer, not a variant telling of the Kailasa
  consort story.
