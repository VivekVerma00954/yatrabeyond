quickIdentity: >
  Kalaratri is the seventh, and fiercest, of the Navadurga: a dark
  "goddess of the night" whose very appearance is said to put demons,
  ghosts and negative energies to flight, while granting her devotees
  fearlessness, an inversion captured in her alternate name, Shubankari,
  "the auspicious one."

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name joins *kala* (time, and by extension black) and *ratri* (night).
  She is also called **Shubankari**, **Raudri** and **Dhumorna**. Her name is
  often used interchangeably with **Kali** in popular usage, though some
  sources treat the two as distinct figures. She is named in the
  Mahabharata's Sauptika Parva, and explicitly invoked as an epithet of Devi
  in the Devi Mahatmya itself (Durga Saptashati 1.75): "You are the dark
  might of dissolution, the great night of final dissolution, and the
  terrible night of delusion."

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown dark-complexioned, three-eyed with eyes said to emit flashes
  like lightning, dishevelled hair, and flames issuing from her nostrils in
  battle. She has four hands, two in the varada and abhaya mudras and two
  holding a scimitar and a thunderbolt, wears a garland that shines like the
  moon, and rides a mule or donkey.

keyStories: >
  In the Mahabharata's Sauptika Parva, she appears on the battlefield during
  Ashwatthama's night raid on the sleeping Pandava camp, described as a
  black-hued figure with a bloody mouth, personifying the horror of war. In
  the Devi Mahatmya cycle, after the goddess Ambika (Chandika) emerges from
  Parvati's body to do battle, Parvati's own skin turns dark as a
  consequence, and she is thereafter also called Kalika and Kalaratri; in
  this two-armed form, bearing a scimitar and a blood-filled skull cup, she
  is said to kill the demon king Shumbha. A related telling has her emerge
  from Chandika's forehead to kill the generals Chanda and Munda, earning
  the name Chamunda in the process, and drink the self-replicating demon
  Raktabija's blood to stop his clones from multiplying, before Shiva calms
  her battle-fury by lying in her path, echoing the widely-known story told
  of Kali. In a Skanda Purana account, Parvati sends her as a messenger to
  warn the demon Durgamasura of the coming war, after which Parvati herself
  kills him and takes the name Durga.

familyAndRelations: >
  Consort Shiva. Closely associated with, and in several tellings not
  clearly distinguished from, Kali and Chamunda.

significance: >
  Her worship on Navratri's seventh night is held in special regard by yogis
  and sadhakas; she is associated with the crown (sahasrara) chakra and is
  said to grant siddhis (spiritual powers) and nidhis (riches) to devotees.

regionalVariation: >
  Named shrines include one in Kalika Galli, near the Annapurna-Vishwanath
  lane in Varanasi; a temple at Vindhyachal, Mirzapur, Uttar Pradesh; one at
  Dumri Buzurg, Nayagaon, Bihar; and temples at Patiala and Sangrur in
  Punjab.
