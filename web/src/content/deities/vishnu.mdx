quickIdentity: >
  Vishnu is the "Preserver" of the Trimurti, alongside Brahma the creator and
  Shiva the destroyer: the deity who sustains cosmic order (dharma) and, when
  that order slips too far, descends to earth in one form or another to set
  it right again.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Narayana** ("resting on the waters") describes his cosmic sleep on the
  serpent Shesha between cycles of creation. **Hari** means roughly "one who
  takes away," referring to sin and suffering rather than possessions.
  **Govinda**, **Madhava**, and **Keshava** are epithets tied specifically to
  his Krishna avatar. Vishnu himself is often glossed as "the all-pervading
  one." Devotional literature also gives him a thousand names (the Vishnu
  Sahasranama), of which these are the load-bearing few.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  Usually shown with four arms (sometimes two), each holding a symbol: a
  conch (the primordial sound of creation), a discus (cosmic order and time,
  and his weapon of choice), a mace (authority), and a lotus (purity). His
  mount is Garuda, a giant eagle. He's often pictured reclining on the
  serpent Shesha, afloat on the cosmic ocean in his heaven, Vaikuntha, or
  standing in royal dress flanked by his consorts Lakshmi (Shri) and Bhudevi
  (the Earth).

keyStories: >
  The central idea here is the *Dashavatara*, his ten principal descents,
  each arriving "whenever dharma declines": Matsya the fish saves the first
  man, Manu, from a great flood; Kurma the tortoise supports a mountain
  during the churning of the cosmic ocean; Varaha the boar rescues the earth
  from a demon; Narasimha, half man and half lion, kills a tyrant who
  thought himself un-killable by exploiting the exact wording of that
  tyrant's boon; Vamana the dwarf reclaims the three worlds from an
  overreaching king in three strides; then come Parashurama, Rama, Krishna,
  and, in most lists, the Buddha (some South Indian traditions swap in
  Balarama instead), with Kalki still to come, an apocalyptic figure meant
  to close the current age. A well-known line from the Bhagavad Gita,
  spoken by Krishna as Vishnu, sums up the doctrine behind all of this:
  whenever righteousness fades, he sends himself forth to restore it. The
  churning of the cosmic ocean itself, Vishnu appearing twice over, first
  as Kurma the tortoise and later as the enchantress Mohini, secures the
  nectar of immortality for the gods.

familyAndRelations: >
  His consorts are Lakshmi, goddess of fortune, and Bhudevi, the earth
  itself. His avatars carry their own separate family lines, Rama's
  household, Krishna's household, rather than Vishnu in his base form
  having children of his own.

significance: >
  Vaishnavas turn to him for the restoration of moral order, protection from
  evil, and personal refuge on the path to liberation. In practice, his Rama
  and Krishna avatars draw by far the most devotion of any of his forms.

regionalVariation: >
  Vaishnavism itself splits into several major traditions that don't fully
  agree on the details: Sri Vaishnavism (South Indian, associated with the
  philosopher Ramanuja) treats Lakshmi as an essential mediator between
  devotee and god; the Madhva tradition holds a stricter dualism between
  soul and god; Vallabha's Pushti Marg, from western India, centres on grace
  given freely rather than earned; and Gaudiya Vaishnavism, from Bengal and
  the teacher Chaitanya, is built almost entirely around Krishna rather than
  Vishnu's other forms. Even the core Dashavatara list isn't fixed everywhere
  and whether the Buddha belongs on it at all varies by sect and region.
