quickIdentity: >
  Sheetala Mata, "the cool one," is the folk goddess of healing invoked
  against smallpox, chickenpox, measles and fevers, held to be both the
  sender of these afflictions and their curer. Widely revered as a form of the
  Mother-goddess in North India, she embodies a very old, practical religion
  of health and disease.

namesAndEpithets: >
  Her name, from *shital* ("cool"), is her promise: to cool the fever and the
  eruption. She is called **Sheetala Devi**, and in Bengal and the east is
  grouped with related disease-goddesses. Regional folk forms such as the
  Punjabi **Seetla Mata** carry their own local traditions.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is depicted riding a donkey and holding a short broom, a winnowing fan,
  a small pot (kalash) of water and neem leaves; in fuller forms she has more
  arms bearing a trident and other emblems. Every attribute is practical
  hygiene turned sacred, the broom and winnower for cleanliness, the cool
  water for the fever, and neem, genuinely antiseptic, for the pox, so that
  her iconography reads almost as a folk manual of care for the sick.

keyStories: >
  Tradition identifies her as a form or aspect of Parvati / Devi charged with
  disease. Her legends typically turn on the consequences of neglecting or
  honouring her: communities that fail to keep cool, clean observance suffer
  outbreaks, while her devotees are protected, a mythic frame around the real
  seasonal pattern of pox and fever. Because much of her lore is oral and folk
  rather than fixed in classical Sanskrit scripture, it varies widely from
  region to region.

familyAndRelations: >
  She is regarded as an incarnation or aspect of Parvati within the broader
  Devi tradition, and in regional cults is often associated with a group of
  sister disease-goddesses and with a male guardian figure. Her "family" is
  more a local pantheon of health than a fixed Puranic lineage.

significance: >
  She is worshipped especially at *Sheetala Ashtami* (Basoda), on which no
  fresh fire is lit and only food cooked the day before, "cold food", is
  eaten, a practice with an obvious seasonal-hygiene logic as warmer weather
  arrives. Devotees petition her for protection from epidemics and for the
  recovery of the sick.

regionalVariation: >
  Her worship is strongest across North India, Bengal and among diaspora
  communities from these regions; in Bengal she is closely linked with the
  disease-goddess complex, and in Punjab and the northwest her Seetla Mata
  form has distinct customs. The eradication of smallpox has shifted her role
  from that specific disease toward a broader guardianship of children's
  health and protection from illness.
