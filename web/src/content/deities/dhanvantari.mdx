quickIdentity: >
  Dhanvantari is venerated as the physician of the gods, an avatar of
  Vishnu, and the deity credited in the Puranas with promulgating the
  practice of Ayurveda, India's traditional system of medicine.

namesAndEpithets: >
  He carries no single distinguishing epithet beyond his own name, which
  Puranic tradition treats as inseparable from his role as divine healer,
  the source to whom Ayurveda itself is traced.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  He is generally shown with four arms, holding a conch, a pot of amrita
  (the elixir of immortality), medicinal herbs, and sometimes a book
  representing Ayurvedic knowledge, an iconography built entirely around
  his identity as healer rather than warrior or creator.

keyStories: >
  According to the Bhagavata Purana, he emerged from the Ocean of Milk
  during the Samudra Manthana, the churning undertaken by devas and
  asuras with Mount Mandara and the serpent Vasuki, appearing with the pot
  of amrita in hand. Tradition also holds that he later incarnated on
  earth as a king of Kashi (present-day Varanasi), through whom Ayurvedic
  knowledge was transmitted to humankind.

familyAndRelations: >
  Regarded as an avatar of Vishnu; his role positions him within the
  Vaishnava avatar tradition specifically as the divine source of healing
  knowledge rather than as a warrior or cosmic-order avatar.

significance: >
  Dhanvantari Trayodashi (Dhanteras), two days before Diwali, is celebrated
  in his honour and is widely observed as an auspicious day for health and
  new purchases; he remains the patron figure invoked at the start of
  Ayurvedic study and practice across India.

regionalVariation: >
  His worship is strongest in South India, particularly Kerala and Tamil
  Nadu, where Ayurveda is most actively practised and where dedicated
  Dhanvantari temples, such as the well-known Thottuva temple in Kerala,
  are found.
