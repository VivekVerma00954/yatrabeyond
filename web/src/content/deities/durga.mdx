quickIdentity: >
  Durga is the great warrior goddess of Hinduism, the fierce and protective
  form of the Divine Feminine (Shakti) who slays demons that gods and men
  cannot. In Shakta theology she is not a subordinate consort but the supreme
  power of the universe in her own right.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Mahishasuramardini** ("slayer of the buffalo-demon Mahishasura") is her
  defining epithet. **Amba / Ambe** and **Bhavani** are affectionate maternal
  names; **Sherawali** ("the one who rides the lion") is a popular North
  Indian form; the **Nava Durga** are her nine forms invoked over the nine
  nights of Navratri.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown as a beautiful but formidable woman with many arms (usually
  eight or ten), each bearing a weapon given to her by a different god, a
  trident, discus, sword, bow, thunderbolt and conch among them, riding a lion
  or tiger. The classic image is *Mahishasuramardini*: mid-stride upon the
  buffalo-demon, spearing him even as her face stays serene. The many arms
  show her wielding all the gods' powers at once; the calm face amid battle
  shows mastery, not rage.

keyStories: >
  Her central story, told in the *Devi Mahatmya*, is the slaying of
  Mahishasura, a buffalo-demon granted a boon that no man or god could kill
  him. The defeated gods pour their combined energies into a single blazing
  goddess, Durga, who takes their weapons and, after a long battle, destroys
  him, an origin worth stating precisely, because she is formed from the gods'
  collective power rather than as a simple transformation of any one goddess.
  She fights other demons too, including Shumbha and Nishumbha, and from her
  brow springs the still fiercer Kali.

familyAndRelations: >
  In the wider tradition she is identified with Parvati, consort of Shiva,
  with Kali described as issuing from her; but in her own scripture, the Devi
  Mahatmya, she is the independent supreme Devi from whom other powers emerge.
  The equivalence with Parvati is a later synthesis and is best held a little
  loosely against the text's own account.

significance: >
  She is worshipped above all at *Navratri* ("nine nights") and *Durga Puja*,
  celebrating her victory over Mahishasura as the triumph of dharma over evil.
  She is invoked for protection, strength and the removal of obstacles and
  enemies, and stands, for many devotees today, as an image of feminine power
  and courage.

regionalVariation: >
  Durga Puja is the pre-eminent festival of West Bengal and eastern India, a
  vast public celebration of elaborate clay images immersed at its close,
  whereas in the north and west the same season is kept as Navratri with
  fasting, garba and dandiya (Gujarat), and Ramlila culminating in Dussehra.
  The same victory is thus celebrated in strikingly different registers across
  regions.
