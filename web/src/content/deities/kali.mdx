quickIdentity: >
  Kali is the fierce, black-skinned goddess of time, death and transformative
  destruction, worshipped as the Divine Mother whose terrifying appearance
  conceals a fiercely protective, liberating love. She is a central figure of
  Shaktism and Tantra, where her destruction of ego and illusion is understood
  as the gateway to spiritual freedom.

namesAndEpithets: >
  **Mahakali** names her supreme, all-encompassing form; **Bhadrakali** and
  **Chamunda** name specific battle-forms tied to particular myths; **Dakshina
  Kalika** is her form especially worshipped in Bengal. Her name derives from
  *kala*, "time," casting her as the power that consumes all things.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She is shown dark-skinned, garlanded with skulls or a chain of freshly cut
  heads, girdled with severed arms, tongue lolling, standing or dancing on the
  prostrate body of Shiva. Typically four-armed, she holds a sword and a
  severed head in her left hands while her right hands make gestures of
  fearlessness and blessing, a single image holding both terror and reassurance.

keyStories: >
  In the *Devi Mahatmya*, Kali springs from the brow of Durga in the heat of
  battle and, in one famous episode, licks up every drop of blood from the
  demon Raktabija before it can touch the ground and spawn a new double of
  him. Having slain her foes she dances in an uncontrollable fury that
  threatens to unmake the world, and only stops, tongue caught out in shock,
  when she realises she has stepped on her own husband Shiva, who had lain
  down in her path to calm her.

familyAndRelations: >
  She is closely linked to Shiva as his consort and to Durga as her fiercest
  emanation, though in Shakta and Tantric traditions she is worshipped not as
  a subordinate consort but as the supreme Devi in her own right, the ground
  from which all other gods, Shiva included, arise.

significance: >
  Central to Bengali Shaktism, where Kali Puja is celebrated on the new-moon
  night of Diwali, and to the Tantric traditions that see confronting her
  fearsome form as a direct path to overcoming fear of death and dissolving
  the ego. Ramakrishna's devotion to Kali at Dakshineswar carried her worship
  to a wide modern and international audience.

regionalVariation: >
  Worship is strongest in Bengal, Assam and Odisha, centred on temples such as
  Kalighat and Dakshineswar in Kolkata, but she is venerated across India as a
  protector against danger and disease. Depictions and emphasis vary
  considerably between calm, benign renderings favoured in some devotional
  contexts and the wilder battlefield imagery emphasised in Tantric art.
