quickIdentity: >
  Devi, "goddess," names the Hindu concept of the Great Goddess (Mahadevi),
  the all-embracing feminine divine understood in Shaktism as the source
  from which every specific goddess, Durga, Kali, Lakshmi, Saraswati,
  Parvati and the rest, ultimately manifests.

namesAndEpithets: >
  As **Mahadevi**, "great goddess," she is held to unite all other
  goddesses within herself, so that each named goddess is approached as one
  of her manifestations rather than as a wholly separate deity.

iconographyAndSymbols: >
  She has no single fixed image, since any depiction of any goddess can be
  read, in Shakta theology, as an image of Devi herself; her presence is
  as much a theological principle, the female energy or Shakti of the
  divine, as a pictured form.

keyStories: >
  The Devi Mahatmya (also called the Durga Saptashati), a text of roughly
  seven hundred verses within the Markandeya Purana dated to around the
  fifth or sixth century CE, is the foundational scripture describing her
  as the supreme reality and creator of the universe, manifesting in forms
  such as Durga and Kali to defeat demons that even the male gods cannot
  overcome.

familyAndRelations: >
  In earlier Vedic-period assimilation she came to represent the Shakti,
  or power, of Shiva as his consort; in the fuller Shakta theology that
  develops later, however, she is treated as the ground of being from which
  all gods, Shiva included, derive rather than as dependent on any consort.

significance: >
  She is central to Shaktism, the goddess-centred stream of Hinduism, and
  her scripture is recited during Navaratri and Durga Puja across India.
  On this platform the "devi" key is used specifically where a prayer or
  site honours the Goddess in general rather than one clearly named aspect.

regionalVariation: >
  Devi worship in its many specific forms spans the length of India, from
  Bengal's Durga and Kali traditions to South India's Devi temples and the
  pan-Indian network of Shakti Peethas; the underlying theology of a single
  unifying Goddess is shared even as her named forms and local traditions
  differ widely.
